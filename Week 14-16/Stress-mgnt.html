Stress Management for Peace Officers

An Interactive Survival Guide

Types of Stress

Fundamental Stress Concepts +

Stress Definition

Stress is the natural reaction of the mind and body to a demand placed on it, pleasant or unpleasant. Biochemical and hormonal processes occur which affect physical and psychological functioning. You NEED stress; it adds flavor, challenge, and opportunity, but unmanaged stress can seriously affect your physical and mental well-being.

Eustress (Positive)

A stressor that is perceived as positive.

  • Getting a promotion
  • Getting married
  • Achieving an accomplishment

Distress (Negative)

A stressor that is perceived as negative.

  • An injury or illness
  • Divorce
  • Being fired

Acute Stress

Short-term stress with a quick resolution. This is the type of stress our body handles the best, gearing up with the 'fight or flight' response and then settling down.

Example: An officer is confronted by a threatening dog. Their mind and body gear up to handle the threat, and once the dog is controlled, the stress response quickly subsides.

Chronic Stress

Prolonged, unrelieved stress, also called cumulative stress. It's like driving with your foot on the brake, leading to wear and tear on the body and mind. An extreme example is **burnout**, where a person loses energy and interest because of unrelieved stress.

Example: The daily hassle of driving in heavy traffic to and from work, compounded by excessive paperwork and rotating shifts, leads to a constant feeling of being worn down.
Critical Incident & Post-Traumatic Stress +

Critical Incident Stress (CIS)

This is heavy-duty, incident-specific stress that causes significant psychological and physical discomfort for **2 days to 1 month**. CIS is considered a normal response to an abnormal, extreme event.

CIS Triggers Checklist:

  • Working a gruesome traffic accident
  • Involvement in a use-of-force incident or shooting
  • Body recovery of a child
  • Facing a life-threatening situation

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

PTSD creates major distress and long-lasting disruptive changes in a person's life. It is not "normal" stress and may require professional help. Symptoms are similar to CIS but are **prolonged past 1 month**.

Key Differentiator of PTSD:

  • The person is unable to make the event part of their past, keeping it vividly in their present.
  • Triggers can cause a person to react with the same intensity, fear, and anxiety as when they were in the original event. It's as if they are **re-living it**, not just remembering it.
  • Symptoms persist for more than one month and disrupt daily life.
Practical Example: An officer involved in a shooting experiences nightmares and anxiety for a few weeks (CIS). If, months later, a car backfiring causes them to drop to the ground in panic, reliving the shooting with the same intensity, it may indicate PTSD.

The "Fight or Flight" Response

Physiology of the Stress Response +

The Nervous System's Role

When your brain perceives a threat, it activates a complex interaction between the nervous and endocrine systems. This is the "Fight or Flight" response, designed to prepare you for physical action.

Sympathetic Nervous System

The "accelerator." It creates biochemical changes to prepare you to respond to a threat.

Parasympathetic Nervous System

The "brake." It's responsible for rest, digestion, and recovery. During a threat, this system is reduced or turned off.

What Happens to Your Body and Mind?

Activation of the Sympathetic Nervous System releases a "stress cocktail" of hormones, causing immediate and widespread changes.

Physical & Perceptual Changes Checklist:

  • **Increases:** Heart rate, blood pressure, breathing, sweating, muscle tension, alertness.
  • **Blood Flow:** Shunted to arms, legs, and brain; shunted away from non-critical organs (stomach, intestines, etc.).
  • **Senses:** Pupils dilate, hearing and vision become more acute for threats, but depth perception is reduced. Time perception can change.
  • **Non-critical functions stop:** Digestion, immune response, and tissue repair are compromised.
Practical Implication: These changes are life-saving in an acute physical confrontation but are damaging during chronic, long-term stress (like an extended investigation or marital problems). The body can't stay in high alert, leading to wear and tear.
Long-Term Effects of Chronic Stress +

Symptoms of Prolonged Wear and Tear

When the body is in a constant state of pseudo-readiness from chronic stress, it impairs the smooth transition between the sympathetic and parasympathetic systems, leading to a host of issues.

Cognitive / Emotional

  • Cognitive impairment (memory, focus)
  • Alcoholism / Substance Abuse
  • Suicide
  • Rapid mood changes
  • Overly suspicious or hostile
  • Argumentative / Blaming others
  • Depression

Physical / Social

  • Digestive disorders
  • Headaches
  • Excessive Illnesses
  • High Blood Pressure / Heart Disease
  • Sleep Disorders
  • Emotionally distancing / Relationship problems
  • Divorce

Common Stressors for Peace Officers

Sources of Officer Stress+

Four Main Categories of Stressors

  • Stressors External to the Agency: Frustration with the judicial system, negative media coverage, perceived lack of public support.
  • Stressors Internal to the Agency: Offensive policies, poor training, lack of recognition, excessive paperwork, perceived favoritism.
  • Stressors in the Work Itself: Shift work, frequent exposure to hardship, boredom interrupted by sudden action, fear and danger, work overload.
  • Stressors Confronting the Individual Officer: Fears about job competence, "fishbowl living" under public scrutiny, family (marital, children, parents), and financial pressures.
Law Enforcement Personalities & Characteristics +

Traits that Help on the Job but Hurt at Home

Certain personality traits make for a good, safe officer, but can often wreak havoc on the home front when not managed.

  • Cynicism and Distrust: Always looking for what is wrong in people; the "us vs. them" mentality.
  • Emotional Control: Can result in the inability to emotionally engage with family and friends at home.
  • The Need for Control: Necessary on the job, but the inability to relinquish or share control can be difficult in personal relationships.
  • Sensation Seeker / Adrenalin Junkie: Remaining hypervigilant off duty; a need for excitement that makes normal life seem mundane.
  • Over-protectiveness: Can smother family members and create friction.
Hypervigilance Rollercoaster +

Hypervigilance: The On-Duty State

Hypervigilance is the elevated alertness of surroundings required for officer survival. It's a threat-based perspective, constantly considering the "what-ifs." This heightened arousal state is necessary and can feel pleasant on the job.

The Off-Duty Crash

When an officer goes off duty, the biological rollercoaster crashes. The Sympathetic NS throttles back, and the Parasympathetic NS activates, leading to a desire to "veg out."

Symptoms of the Off-Duty Crash:

  • Extreme fatigue
  • Detachment and isolation
  • Emotional withdrawal from family
  • Reduced interaction and engagement in hobbies
The Family Perspective: Co-workers see the officer as alert, energetic, and engaged on duty. The family sees that same officer as tired, withdrawn, and non-communicative at home. This disconnect is a major source of relationship stress.

Stress Management & Relaxation

Three Approaches to Management +

You may not be able to control the event, but you can control your reaction to it. The goal is to make stress work for you, not against you.

  • Avoid: Reduce or eliminate exposure to a stressor. (e.g., Leave early to avoid traffic, learn to say "no," set boundaries).
  • Alter: Minimize the physiological response to a stressor. (e.g., Use problem-solving, planning, and direct communication to change the situation).
  • Adapt/Accept: Build resistance to stress or change your perspective. This is key for unavoidable stressors.

Building Resistance and a Balanced Life

Nurturing a balance across different areas of your life gives you a broad base of support.

  • Psychological: Take time away from work, focus on hobbies, gain knowledge in areas outside the job.
  • Physical: Get proper rest and relaxation, exercise to play out the "fight or flight" response, and focus on good nutrition.
  • Social: Engage in community activities and nurture relationships both inside and outside of law enforcement.
  • Spiritual/Faith: Nurturing this area can give meaning to all other areas of life.

Changing Perspective

The impact of a positive attitude is key to managing unavoidable stress. This involves being aware of and correcting unrealistic expectations and negative self-talk.

Practical Example: Instead of thinking "I should succeed at everything I do," reframe it to "When I fail, I'll get up and try again or try something different." Use humor when appropriate to dampen stress and change moods.
Relaxation Techniques +

Engaging the Parasympathetic Nervous System

These techniques are designed to actively engage your body's "braking" system to calm your mind and body.

Relaxation Methods:

  • Visualization / Guided Imagery: Mentally picture yourself succeeding or imagine being in a calm, peaceful place, engaging all your senses.
  • Thought Stopping / Blocking: Force your mind off of stressors by refusing to dwell on them and actively focusing on a different, specific topic.
  • Tactical Breathing: Breathe in for a count of 4, hold for 4, and exhale for 4. This slow, deep breathing directly activates the Parasympathetic NS, lowering heart rate and blood pressure.
  • Progressive Muscular Relaxation: Tense and then relax different muscle groups to learn to recognize and release physical tension.
Tactical Application: An officer approaching a high-risk traffic stop can use tactical breathing to lower their heart rate from an irrational level (175+ bpm) to an optimal performance level (115-145 bpm), improving fine motor skills and cognitive processing.

Critical Incidents & Suicide Awareness

Stress & Performance +

The Inverted-U Principle

As your heart rate increases, your ability to perform physically and process information improves up to a point, then it begins to drop significantly.

Performance vs. Heart Rate:

  • ~115 BPM: Fine motor skills begin to deteriorate (e.g., double-locking handcuffs, writing).
  • ~145 BPM: Complex motor skills deteriorate (e.g., complex ground fighting). Vision changes begin (tunnel vision).
  • > 175 BPM: Auditory changes, loss of peripheral vision, increased reaction time. May lead to irrational behavior like repetitive, non-productive actions.
  • > 200 BPM: Irrational behavior, repetitive non-productive activities.

During a critical incident, you may experience time slowing down, sounds being muffled, and memory lapses. Most memory returns in 24-48 hours, so initial reports should be considered preliminary.

Five Phases of Traumatic Event Transition

Persons who experience traumatic events typically go through five phases:

  • Denial Phase: Refusal to believe the event occurred.
  • Anger Phase: Feeling resentment ("why me?").
  • Bargaining Phase: Wishing the event never took place.
  • Depression Phase: Often the longest phase.
  • Acceptance Phase: Resuming a normal life. This can be reached sooner with professional counseling.

After a shooting: The aftermath can cause secondary trauma due to media, investigations, and public opinion. It's common for an officer to feel like "the bad guy," so keeping the officer informed and allowing them as much control as possible is crucial.

How to Assist a Coworker After a Critical Incident +

Immediate Support Checklist

Your presence and practical help can be invaluable to a coworker who has experienced a traumatic event.

  • Assist with basic needs first: Ensure they are safe and help notify their family.
  • Handle small duties: With consent, help with things like feeding their dog, handling phone calls, or bringing meals.
  • Provide physical presence: Sit quietly, listen if they want to talk, but allow for silence if they don't.
  • Listen, Listen, Listen: Don't offer answers; offer presence and comfort.
  • Give reassurance: Remind them that what they are experiencing is a normal reaction to an abnormal situation.
  • Ensure someone stays with them.
  • Assist with resources: Connect them with the CISM team, EAP, or chaplains.
  • Follow Department guidelines.
Suicide Awareness & Intervention +

Common Signs of Suicidal Thoughts

  • Experiencing significant losses (relationship, health, job).
  • Expressing hopelessness, depression, or guilt.
  • Changes in sleep and/or appetite.
  • Alcohol/Substance abuse.
  • Talking about death or wanting to die.
  • Minimal social support; isolation.

Intervention Strategy: A.I.D. L.I.F.E.

A step-by-step model for assisting a coworker who may be contemplating suicide.

  • Ask: Don't be afraid to ask directly, "Are you thinking of killing yourself?"
  • Intervene Immediately: Take action. Tell them they are not alone.
  • Don't Keep it a Secret.
  • Locate Help: Seek a mental health professional, peer support, chaplain, or family.
  • Inform a Supervisor of the situation.
  • Find Someone to Stay With the Person.
  • Expedite: Get help immediately. **Do not leave the person alone.**

Substance Abuse Awareness

Alcohol & Drug Abuse in Law Enforcement +

Myths vs. Realities of Alcoholism

  • Myth: Once an alcoholic, always an alcoholic.
    Reality: Alcoholism is a disease for which recovery is possible with a dedicated program.
  • Myth: Alcohol helps you sleep.
    Reality: It interferes with necessary REM sleep, impairing overall sleep quality.
  • Myth: Alcoholism is caused by a lack of willpower or weak character.
    Reality: The causes are complex, implicating physical, genetic, psychological, and social factors.
  • Myth: All alcoholics are skid-row drunks.
    Reality: Many hold high-level jobs and function well for years.
  • Myth: Alcoholics drink every day.
    Reality: Abuse patterns vary widely, from daily drinking to periodic binges.

Signs of Drug / Alcohol Abuse

  • Failed attempts to cut back on use.
  • Difficulty enjoying life without alcohol/drugs.
  • Declining work performance.
  • Feeling guilty, or angry if confronted.
  • Hiding alcohol or drugs.
  • Blackouts or memory lapses.
  • Blaming others for problems related to use.
  • Financial or relationship problems.

Assisting a Co-Worker

Co-workers are often the first to see a problem but the last to intervene. Avoiding the problem contributes to the denial of the abuser.

How to Help Checklist:

  • **Be aware** and recognize the problem.
  • **Urge** the person to seek help.
  • **Encourage** the person after they seek help.
  • **Focus on performance and safety**, not a personal diagnosis. Tell the officer you are concerned about their safety and the safety of others.
  • If they refuse help, **discuss your concerns with a supervisor.**
Practical Example: An officer notices their partner frequently smells of alcohol and has been making errors on reports. Instead of accusing them, the officer says, "I'm concerned about you and about our safety on calls. I think it would be a good idea to talk to someone from EAP or peer support."
