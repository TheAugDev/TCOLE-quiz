Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

A Guide for Law Enforcement Professionals

Defining Brain Injuries

Types of Brain Injuries +

Acquired Brain Injury (ABI)

An ABI is an injury to the brain that occurs after birth and is not related to a congenital or degenerative disease. It can cause temporary or permanent impairments.

Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

A type of ABI caused by an external force that disrupts normal brain function.

Causes Checklist:

  • A blow to the head
  • A penetrating head injury
  • Falls, assaults, car crashes, blast injuries

Non-Traumatic Brain Injury

A type of ABI caused by an internal event rather than an external force.

Causes Checklist:

  • Stroke or aneurysm
  • Infection (meningitis, encephalitis)
  • Drug-induced, exposure to toxins
  • Anoxia (lack of oxygen), near drowning
Prevalence, Causes & Examples +

Prevalence of TBI

TBI is a leading cause of death and disability in the U.S. Over 2.2 million people sustain a TBI each year, with over 144,000 in Texas alone. This number is greater than annual diagnoses for Alzheimer's, breast cancer, HIV/AIDS, prostate cancer, lung cancer, and ALS combined.

Leading Causes of TBI in the U.S.

  • Falls: 40%
  • Struck by/against something: 16%
  • Motor vehicle crashes: 14%
  • Assaults: 11%
  • Other/Unknown: 19%

Populations with Higher Risk

  • Children (0-4) & Adolescents (16-19): Most likely to have a TBI-related ER visit.
  • Older Adults (75+): Highest rates of TBI-related hospitalizations and deaths.
  • Domestic Violence Survivors: Prevalence of TBI is over 35%.
  • Incarcerated Populations: Estimated TBI prevalence is 60.3%.
  • Homeless Populations: TBI prevalence is between 30-40%.
  • Veterans: An estimated 10-20% of Iraq veterans have some level of brain injury.

Real-World Examples

  • Historical Figures: Harriet Tubman, Phineas Gage, Abraham Lincoln.
  • Public Figures: Gabby Giffords (gunshot wound), Tracy Morgan (car crash), Bob Woodruff (blast injury).
  • Movies Featuring Brain Injury: 50 First Dates, The Vow, The Bourne Identity, Memento, Concussion.

Effects, Symptoms & Recovery

Brain Functions and Injury Severity +

The Brain's Control Center

A brain injury in a specific lobe affects the functions controlled by that lobe. For example, an injury to the frontal lobe can impact personality, decision-making, and impulse control.

  • Frontal Lobe: Thought, memory, behavior, personality, problem-solving.
  • Parietal Lobe: Language and touch.
  • Temporal Lobe: Hearing, learning, emotions.
  • Occipital Lobe: Visual processing.
  • Cerebellum: Balance and coordination.
  • Brain Stem: Breathing, heart rate, temperature.

Determining Injury Severity

Severity (Mild, Moderate, or Severe) depends on factors like duration of consciousness loss and memory loss. However, imaging results (MRI/CT) may be normal for all levels of severity.

FactorMildModerateSevere
Loss of Consciousness0-30 minutes>30 min but <24 hrs>24 hrs
Memory Loss<24 hrs>24 hrs but <7 days>7 days
"Mild" brain injuries are deceptive. A person may look "normal," but multiple "mild" injuries can have severe, cumulative consequences and even lead to death. All brain injuries must be taken seriously.
Changes and Comorbidities +

Three Categories of Change After Brain Injury

Cognitive Changes

  • Difficulty with memory
  • Lack of concentration
  • Delayed thinking/processing
  • Reckless decision-making
  • Difficulty communicating

Emotional/Behavioral

  • Irritability / Aggression
  • Anxiety / Depression
  • Mood swings
  • Inappropriate social behavior
  • Impulsivity

Physical Changes

  • Poor balance
  • Impaired fine motor skills
  • Vision or hearing issues
  • Slurred speech
  • Chronic headaches / Seizures

The Recovery Process

Recovery is different for everyone and is best thought of as a chronic condition rather than a simple injury. It is an ongoing process throughout life.

  • Some individuals may feel they return to "normal."
  • Others must learn a "new normal" with new limitations and adaptations.

Common Comorbidities

Comorbidities are two or more simultaneously present chronic diseases or conditions. The effects of a brain injury can often lead to their development.

  • Substance use/abuse
  • Sleep problems
  • Depression and suicidality
  • Epilepsy
  • Neurodegenerative diseases (ALS, Alzheimer's)
  • Fatigue

Intervention & Prevention

Recognizing a Brain Injury or Stroke +

Immediate Signs of Brain Injury

If someone experiences any of the following after their head or body has been hit or jolted, encourage them to go to the ER immediately.

  • Confusion or disorientation
  • Loss of consciousness
  • Severe headache
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Blood or clear fluid draining from nose or ears
  • Slurred speech or trouble walking

Note: Symptoms may not show up for days or even weeks.

Recognizing a Stroke: F.A.S.T.

Use the F.A.S.T. acronym to quickly identify the signs of a stroke.

  • F - Face Drooping: Is one side of the face drooping or numb? Ask the person to smile.
  • A - Arm Weakness: Is one arm weak or numb? Ask the person to raise both arms.
  • S - Speech Difficulty: Is speech slurred or hard to understand? Ask them to repeat a simple sentence.
  • T - Time to call 9-1-1: If any of these signs are present, call 9-1-1 immediately, even if the symptoms go away.
Prevention Strategies +

Child Safety

  • Keep floors clear, secure rugs, and use safety gates.
  • Use nonslip mats in bathtubs.
  • Ensure playground surfaces are shock-absorbent.
  • Prevent abusive head trauma by educating caregivers on the Period of PURPLE Crying (an evidence-based approach to understanding normal, but frustrating, infant crying patterns).
  • Under Texas's Natasha's Law, coaches and trainers must be trained on concussion management.

Motor Vehicle Safety

  • Always wear seat belts.
  • Never drive intoxicated or distracted.
  • Ensure children are in age and size-appropriate safety seats.

General Safety

  • Wear properly fitted helmets for activities like riding bikes, motorcycles, ATVs, or playing contact sports.
  • For older adults, remove tripping hazards, install grab bars, and maintain a physical activity program.

Effective Communication Strategies

Creating a Safe Environment +

DO:

  • Be encouraging, respectful, and patient.
  • Use a positive or neutral tone.
  • Stay calm, even if behavior is inappropriate.
  • Focus on the person's message, not the delivery.
  • Show empathy for feelings (not behavior).

DO NOT:

  • Threaten, interrupt, or argue.
  • Challenge or shame the person.
  • Use a condescending tone.
  • Finish their sentences for them.
  • View the situation as a competition.
Accommodating and Finding Help +

Practical Communication Tips

  • Reduce distractions (lights, sounds, other people).
  • Speak slowly, simply, and clearly.
  • Focus on one subject at a time.
  • Check to see if they have understood you.
  • Use active listening skills (paraphrase, clarify).
  • Defer judgment.

Resource Facilitation & Unmet Needs

You will likely encounter individuals with a brain injury who have unmet needs. Referring them to resources can have a hugely positive effect.

  • Physiological needs: Food, shelter, health care.
  • Safety needs: Personal, physical, financial security.
  • Belongingness needs: Friendship, family, intimacy.
  • Esteem needs: Self-respect, confidence, independence.
Practical Tool: Some brain injury survivors carry a wallet-sized emergency contact card. This can help an officer find someone who can assist in managing the situation and provide crucial information. Be aware of these cards.
