Crisis Intervention Training (CIT)

A Comprehensive Study Guide

Core Concepts & Models

CIT Origins & Goals

Origin: The CIT program was conceived in Memphis, TN, in 1987 after police shot and killed a 26-year-old man with a history of mental illness who was cutting himself with a knife.

Primary Goal: To calm persons with mental illness who are in crisis and refer them to mental health care services rather than incarcerating them. It is foremost about officer safety.

Impact on Community: CIT has been shown to decrease the need for higher levels of police intervention, decrease officer injuries, and redirect those in crisis from the criminal justice system to the healthcare system, increasing public trust.

Critical Decision-Making Model (CDM)

The CDM is a framework to organize decision-making, make better decisions under pressure, and articulate your actions after an incident.

Core Principles:

  • Police Ethics & Agency Values
  • Proportionality
  • Sanctity of Human Life

The 5 Steps:

  1. Collect Information: Gather facts and intelligence.
  2. Assess Situation, Threats, and Risks: Analyze the collected information. Is there a threat? What are the risks?
  3. Consider Police Powers and Agency Policy: What legal authority do you have? What does policy dictate?
  4. Identify Options and Determine Best Course of Action: What can you do? What are the pros and cons of each option?
  5. Act, Review, and Re-assess: Implement your decision and continuously evaluate its effect. The model is a continuous loop.
Behavioral Crisis & Assessment

Behavioral Crisis: An episode of mental and/or emotional distress creating instability or danger, considered disruptive by the community, family, or the person themselves. The person may be more prone to acting instinctually (self-preservation) rather than with logical thought.

Signs of a Person in Crisis: Not responding to verbal commands, lack of coherence, agitation, talking to themselves, poor hygiene, unusual clothing/behavior.

Initial 3-Point Assessment:

  1. Level of Comprehension: Do they understand you? Can they follow instructions? Are their comments logical?
  2. Behavior: How are they acting? Note self-care, hygiene, coordination, and compliance. Is non-compliance potentially due to a mental health issue?
  3. Emotion: What is their prevailing emotional state (anger, fear, sadness, mania)? Is it appropriate for the context?
Emotional-Rational Thinking Scale

This scale illustrates the relationship between emotions and rational thinking. When a person is in crisis, their emotional level is high, and their ability to think rationally is low. The goal of de-escalation is to lower their emotions so they can think more clearly and make better decisions.

Example: You respond to a minor traffic accident. One driver is screaming, cursing, and blaming the other (High Emotions, Low Rationality). They are unable to simply exchange insurance information. Your goal is to lower their emotional state (using empathy, active listening) to a point where they can think rationally and complete the necessary steps to resolve the situation.

De-escalation Strategy

The 3 Steps for a De-escalation Response
  1. Time: Do not initiate force or action unless there is a serious, imminent physical threat. Slow the situation down. Don't rush. Patience is key, as many emotional outbursts are short-lived.
  2. Barriers/Distance: Use an appropriate reactionary gap. This can be physical distance or objects (barriers) that provide cover. The formula is: Distance + Cover = Time. Time gives you more options.
  3. Dialogue: Use communication techniques (like the Behavioral Staircase) to de-escalate and obtain voluntary compliance.
Tactical Concepts
  • Tactical Pause: If there's no immediate threat upon arrival, pause to collect information and prepare before acting.
  • Tactical Repositioning: Continuously move to maintain a position of advantage and a safety zone (reactionary gap).
  • Team Roles: In a team response, assign roles. Only ONE officer should be communicating with the subject. Other roles include lethal cover, less-lethal options, radio usage, and perimeter control.
  • Containment (Safety Zone): Establish a perimeter to secure the scene. This is not a fixed distance (e.g., 21 feet) but should be based on your judgment of the situation.
5 Universal Truths of Human Interaction
  1. All cultures want to be treated with Dignity and Respect.
  2. All people would rather be asked than told what to do.
  3. All people want to know why they are asked or told to do something.
  4. All people would rather have options than threats.
  5. All people want a second chance to make matters right.

Communication Techniques

The Behavioral Change Staircase Model

A model for gaining voluntary compliance through rapport and influence. You must progress through the steps in order.

Example Scenario: You are dispatched to a "welfare check" for a man pacing and yelling in a public park.

  1. Active Listening: You approach calmly from a safe distance, introduce yourself, and use open-ended questions. "What's going on today?" He vents about losing his job and being unable to pay rent. You listen without interrupting, gathering information.
  2. Empathy: You say, "I can only imagine how stressful and frustrating that must be. It sounds like you're in a really tough spot." This validates his feelings without agreeing with his yelling.
  3. Rapport: You notice he's wearing a Dallas Cowboys hat. You find a "hook." "I'm a Cowboys fan myself. That was a rough game on Sunday." This brief moment of shared interest builds a connection and lowers his defensiveness.
  4. Influence: Now that you have some rapport, you can start guiding his behavior. "I want to help you figure this out. Would you be willing to walk over to that bench with me so we can talk more privately and see what resources might be available?" You've given him an option, not a command.
  5. Behavioral Change: He agrees and sits on the bench. The immediate crisis (public yelling) is resolved, and you can now work on the problem-solving phase in a calmer state. This is a successful "baby step."
The LEAPS Model

A communication model focused on de-escalation.

  • Listen: Let the person vent while you gather information.
  • Empathize: Put yourself in their shoes. Verbalize your understanding of their feelings.
  • Ask: Use who, what, when, where, how to gather more information and show interest.
  • Paraphrase: Reiterate what they've said in your own words to ensure you understand.
  • Summarize: Bring it all together, summarizing their view, the options, and the next steps.

Pro-LEAPS phrases: "What can I do to help?", "I can see you are upset, tell me what is wrong", "For your safety and mine..."

Anti-LEAPS phrases to avoid: "Calm down.", "What's your problem?", "You people...", "Because I said so."

Verbal & Non-Verbal Skills

AID LIFE (For Suicidal Subjects):

  • Ask about suicide.
  • Intervene immediately.
  • Don't leave them alone.
  • Locate help (resources, family).
  • Inform them of your concern.
  • Find someone to stay with them.
  • Expedite help.

Effective Communication Tips:

  • "I" Statements vs. "You" Statements: Use "I" statements ("I feel nervous when you...") to avoid sounding accusatory like "You" statements ("You are not listening").
  • Reflecting Statements: Neutral responses to encourage more talking ("I see," "Tell me more about that").
  • Emotional Contagion: People often mirror the emotions of those around them. Model calm behavior.
  • Tactical Transparency: Explain an action before initiating it so the person knows what to expect. Example: "When you come out, I need you to show me your hands, so that I know you don't have a weapon." Use only when safe to do so.
Things to AVOID
  • Problem-solving too quickly.
  • Using accusatory "You" and "Why" questions.
  • Being authoritative or judgmental.
  • Leading with your own feelings.
  • Diagnosing their illness.
  • Arguing, expressing disapproval, or trying to convince them their delusions aren't real.
  • Crowding their personal space.
  • Pacifying ("Calm down") or derailing their train of thought.

Mental Health Overview

Key Statistics & Definitions

Mental Illness (Legal): An illness, disease, or condition (excluding epilepsy, dementia, substance abuse, or intellectual disability) that substantially impairs a person's thought, perception of reality, emotional process, or judgment, or grossly impairs their behavior.

Insanity (Legal - TPC 8.01): An affirmative defense to prosecution where, at the time of the conduct, the actor, as a result of severe mental disease or defect, did not know that their conduct was wrong.

Key Statistics:

  • 1 in 5 US adults experience a serious Mental Illness (MI).
  • 1 in 10 police calls involve a person with MI.
  • Nearly two-thirds of all people with a diagnosable mental illness do not seek treatment.
  • 3-5% of violent acts can be attributed to serious mental illness.
  • People with severe MI are over 10 times more likely to be victims of violent crime.
Categories of Disorders
  • Mood Disorders: Persistent emotional states. Includes Depression (major depressive disorder lasts >2 weeks) and Bipolar Disorder (cycles of mania and depression). Depression is the #1 factor in suicide.
  • Thought Disorders: Disrupt a person's ability to think clearly or perceive reality. Includes Psychosis (a break from reality with delusions/hallucinations) and Schizophrenia.
  • Personality Disorders: Enduring, inflexible patterns of thinking and behaving that deviate from cultural expectations. Examples: Paranoid (distrust), Antisocial (rule-breaking, no remorse), Borderline (instability in relationships, impulsivity), Narcissistic (grandiose, lacks empathy).
  • Trauma & PTSD: Reaction to an extreme traumatic event. Symptoms include re-experiencing the event, avoidance, negative thoughts/mood, and hyperarousal.
  • Cognitive Disorders: Decline in cognitive function. Includes Delirium (short-term confusion), Dementia/Alzheimer's, and Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI).
  • Developmental Disorders: Conditions from childhood. Includes Autism Spectrum Disorder and Intellectual Disability (IQ below 70).
Substance Abuse & Co-Occurring Disorders

Many individuals use substances to self-medicate for mental health symptoms. This leads to co-occurring disorders. Substances can have adverse effects with prescribed medications.

DRE Categories:

  • CNS Depressants: Slow down brain/body (Alcohol, Xanax, Valium, GHB, Rohypnol).
  • CNS Stimulants: Speed up heart rate/body (Cocaine, Methamphetamine, Adderall).
  • Hallucinogens: Alter perception (LSD, Psilocybin, Ecstasy).
  • Dissociative Anesthetics: Inhibit pain by cutting off brain's perception (PCP, Ketamine).
  • Narcotic Analgesics: Relieve pain, induce euphoria (Heroin, Morphine, Oxycontin, Fentanyl).
  • Inhalants: Breathable substances (Paint thinners, gasoline, canned air).
  • Cannabis: Active ingredient is THC (Marijuana, K2/Spice).
Psychotropic Medications & Stigma

Psychotropic Medication: Any medication capable of affecting a person's mind, emotions, and behavior.

Reasons for Non-Compliance: Unpleasant side effects (weight gain, etc.), high cost/lack of insurance, feeling better and believing they no longer need it, stigma.

Stigma:

A mark of disgrace or shame. Stigma around mental illness is a major barrier to treatment. Its components include:

  • Labeling: Assigning someone to a category based on their condition.
  • Stereotyping: Believing all people with that condition share certain (usually negative) traits.
  • Creating a division: Separating "us" from "them".
  • Discrimination: Acting differently toward someone based on the label.

Officer & Subject Safety & Wellness

Veterans & Special Considerations

Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI): Caused by impact to the head or blast injuries. Mild TBI is most common (75%). Can affect attention, memory, coordination, and social awareness.

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD): Can develop after exposure to a traumatic event. Symptoms include:

  • Intrusion: Reliving the event (flashbacks, nightmares).
  • Avoidance: Avoiding reminders (people, places, sounds) of the trauma.
  • Negative Mood/Thoughts: Self-blame, loss of interest, feeling detached.
  • Hyperarousal/Hypervigilance: Being "on edge," easily startled, irritable.

Moral Injury: Damage done to one's conscience when they perpetrate, witness, or fail to prevent acts that violate their own moral beliefs.

Survivor's Guilt: Arises when someone survives an event that others did not, leading to feelings that they should have died instead.

Military Sexual Trauma (MST): A unique trauma perpetrated by a trusted fellow service member. 23% of women report sexual assault in the military.

Building Rapport with Veterans: Leverage shared commonalities with law enforcement (desire for public safety, adherence to structure, ethos of self-sacrifice).

Suicide Risk Assessment

Key Risk Factors: Male age 15-34, depression, substance intoxication, previous attempts, feelings of hopelessness, lack of support, recent loss, feeling like a burden.

Protective Factors: Spiritual faith, employment, access to treatment, connectedness, flexible coping skills.

Assessment (Plan - Method - Means - Intent): You must ask directly.

  1. Plan: Do they have a specific plan?
  2. Method/Means: Do they have the means to carry out the plan?
  3. Intent: How determined are they to follow through?

Myths vs. Reality:

  • Myth: If one talks about suicide, they are not serious. Reality: Most people who die by suicide have given definite warnings.
  • Myth: Signs of improvement mean a declining risk. Reality: The risk can be highest as they may have gained the energy to act on their plan.
Officer Stress & Suicide

Hypervigilance: The elevated state of alertness required for police work. The "Biological Rollercoaster" refers to swinging from this on-duty state to a state of exhaustion, detachment, and low energy off-duty.

Law Enforcement Suicide Statistics:

  • An average of 130 law enforcement suicides occur every year.
  • More officers die of suicide than from shootings and traffic accidents combined.
  • Almost 1 in 4 police officers has thoughts of suicide at some point.

Stress Relaxation Techniques:

  • Visualization / Guided Imagery: Mentally picturing a peaceful, calming place or scenario.
  • Thought Stopping / Blocking / Focusing: Consciously stopping negative thoughts and refocusing.
  • Progressive Muscular Relaxation: Tensing and then relaxing different muscle groups.

Legal & Diversion

Legal Considerations for Intervention

Texas SB 1849: Mandates that officers learn de-escalation techniques and that agencies make a good faith effort to divert persons in crisis to proper treatment.

Emergency Detention (Health & Safety Code 573.001): A peace officer may take a person into custody without a warrant if the officer believes:

  1. The person has a mental illness, AND
  2. Because of that illness, there is a substantial risk of serious harm to the person or others unless immediately restrained, AND
  3. There is not sufficient time to obtain a warrant.

"Substantial risk" can be demonstrated by the person's behavior or evidence of severe emotional distress and deterioration. The officer must complete a Notification of Emergency Detention form.

Limitation of Liability (HSC 571.019(a)): "People acting in good faith, reasonably, and without negligence are not civilly or criminally liable."

Jail Diversion Programs

Goal: To redirect individuals with mental illness from the criminal justice system to the healthcare system. This can reduce recidivism, increase public safety, and be more cost-effective.

Pre-arrest Diversion: Diverting a person from the criminal justice system before charges are filed (e.g., transport to a mental health facility instead of jail for a low-level offense).

Post-arrest Diversion: Provides an alternative to criminal case processing after charges have been filed. Successful completion can lead to dismissal of charges.

Mental Health Courts: A form of post-arrest diversion that combines judicial supervision with community mental health treatment. Participants are typically diagnosed with a serious mental illness. These courts have been shown to significantly reduce recidivism (e.g., 20% vs 72% for non-participants).
