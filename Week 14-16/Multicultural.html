Multiculturalism & Human Relations

A Guide for Law Enforcement Professionals

Understanding Prejudice & Discrimination

Core Definitions +

Attitude

A cognitive position based on knowledge, feelings, and experiences that influences behavior.

Race

Refers to groups with common ancestry and physical characteristics.

Ethnicity

Refers to shared culture and background, including common ancestry, language, and religion.

Culture

A way of thinking and acting based on tradition; learned behavior passed between generations.

Ethnocentrism

Regarding one's own culture as the "center of the universe" and the basis for all comparisons.

Prejudice

An adverse judgment or opinion formed beforehand or without knowledge of the facts; i.e., bias.

Discrimination

Acting on the basis of prejudice.

The Functions & Feelings of Prejudice +

Functions of Prejudice

Prejudice serves several psychological and social functions for the person holding the prejudice.

  • Ethnocentrism: Provides egotistic satisfaction through comparing others with oneself.
  • Stereotype/Categorical Treatment: Creates convenient mental groupings for people one is not knowledgeable about.
  • Scapegoat: Provides a person or group to blame when things go wrong.
  • Projection: Provides an outlet for one's own tensions and frustrations.
  • Authoritarian Personality: Symbolizes affiliation with a more dominant group.
  • Societal Strain: Justifies discrimination that is seen as an advantage to the dominant group.

Four Basic Feelings of Prejudiced Persons

Most prejudiced individuals harbor a combination of these four attitudes.

  • Feeling of Superiority: A self-assured belief that they are better than others.
  • Others are Strange and Different: A feeling that the other group is alien, which promotes social exclusion.
  • Proprietary Claims: A belief that as a member of their group, they are entitled to exclusive rights or privileges.
  • Fear: A belief that an outside group is trying to intrude and threaten the things that belong to them.
Functions of Discrimination +

How Discrimination Operates

The act of discrimination has tangible effects on both the perpetrator and the victim.

  • Tends to reinforce prejudice concerning the group's alleged inferiority.
  • Limits the other group's effectiveness in business, education, and political office.
  • Affords an avenue to economic exploitation of the group being discriminated against.

The Role of Impartial Policing

Principles of Impartial Enforcement +

What is Impartial Enforcement?

The peace officer's role includes enforcing laws impartially and supporting the concept that all persons are equally subject to the law and will be treated equally by it.

  • Requires that no person be treated unfairly, unjustly, or with bias or prejudice.
  • Requires that no person be given more favorable treatment than another in the same situation.
  • Applies to both the interaction of the law with a citizen and the interaction between citizens when law enforcement is involved.
  • It is an ideal condition we must constantly strive to achieve.

Why is a Peace Officer's Role so Sensitive?

  • Officers often work alone and make decisions demanding impartiality with no other witnesses.
  • Temptations from citizens to be partial can be overwhelming and go undetected.
  • Deviations from fair treatment are often easily excused as necessary for "order maintenance."
  • The public's impression of the entire justice system often comes from their contact with a single peace officer.
Key Concepts in Human Relations+

Foundational Beliefs from Social Science

These concepts underpin the principles of democratic and impartial policing.

  • Every individual is entitled to equal rights and dignities by virtue of being human.
  • The right to be free implies the right to be different.
  • We should try to understand people different from us.
  • All people share common needs: social needs, health, employment, shelter, food, and a positive self-image.
  • We tend to categorize people and make judgments about them, which can lead to stereotyping.
  • Democracy cannot work for some unless it works for all.
Benefits and Application of Impartiality +

How to Apply Impartiality

  • Base enforcement action on the nature of the situation and severity of the offense, not personal factors.
  • Participate in the justice process (charging, testifying) in a way that does not favor one group over another.
  • Communicate by word and action that all people, including justice personnel, are subject to the law.

Benefits of Impartial Policing

  • Preserves the balance between those who govern and those who are governed, which is essential for democracy.
  • Builds respect and cooperation with the community, which can help reduce crime.
  • Upholds a fundamental moral and professional requirement for all peace officers.
  • Boosts self-respect and professional respect.
Practical Example: An officer stops two cars for speeding at the same rate in the same zone. One driver is cooperative and from the local community; the other is argumentative and from out of town. Impartial enforcement means both drivers receive the same citation based on the severity of the offense, not their attitude or origin.

Cross-Cultural Communication & Sensitivity

Skills for Effective Communication +

What is Cross-Cultural Communication?

A field of study that focuses on how people of differing cultures communicate with each other. Understanding these principles is imperative to facilitate dialogue and reduce conflict in a multicultural society.

Essential Skills for Human Relations

Developing these skills can improve interactions and build community trust.

  • Gather knowledge and information about the person(s) you are interacting with.
  • Be non-judgmental.
  • Tolerate ambiguity and approaches different from your own.
  • Show warmth, genuine interest, and empathy.
  • Be willing to communicate.
Consequences of Insensitivity vs. Sensitivity +

Adverse Consequences of Insensitivity

  • Hurt feelings
  • Anger
  • Loss of personal and professional respect
  • Ineffective performance
  • Negative behavior

Positive Consequences of Sensitivity

  • Respect of the community
  • Respect from fellow professionals
  • Support for democratic principles
  • Self-respect
Practical Example: An officer responds to a noise complaint at a celebration for a cultural holiday they are unfamiliar with. An insensitive approach would be to immediately order the music off and threaten citations. A sensitive approach would be to first try to understand the nature of the gathering, show respect for their traditions, and then explain the complaint and work toward a mutually agreeable solution, like lowering the volume after a certain time.
