Public Order & Corruption

A Comprehensive Penal Code Study Guide

Bribery, Corruption & Abuse of Office

Bribery, Coercion, & Improper Influence

36.02. Bribery

A person intentionally or knowingly offers, confers, solicits, or accepts a benefit in exchange for a public servant's decision, opinion, vote, or other exercise of discretion.

Punishment: 2nd Degree Felony

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person act intentionally or knowingly?
  • Was a `benefit` offered, given, solicited, or accepted?
  • Was this benefit for a `public servant`, party official, or voter?
  • Was the benefit in exchange for the recipient's vote, decision, opinion, or exercise of official discretion?
Example: A driver pulled over for speeding offers the officer a $100 bill and says, "Can we just make this go away?" Both the driver (for offering) and the officer (if they accepted) have committed Bribery.

36.03. Coercion of Public Servant or Voter

A person uses coercion (a threat) to influence a public servant's official action or a voter's vote.

Punishment: Class A Misdemeanor, but a 3rd Degree Felony if the coercion is a threat to commit a felony.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person use `coercion` (a threat)?
  • Was the target a `public servant` or `voter`?
  • Was the purpose to influence an official duty, make them violate a duty, or influence a vote?
Example: A business owner tells a city inspector, "If you don't approve my building permit, I'll make sure that damaging personal information about you gets leaked to the press."

36.09. Offering Gift to Public Servant

A person offers or gives any benefit to a public servant that they know the public servant is prohibited by law from accepting.

Punishment: Class A Misdemeanor

Elements Checklist:

  • Did a person offer, confer, or agree to confer a `benefit`?
  • Was the recipient a `public servant`?
  • Did the actor `know` the public servant was prohibited by law from accepting the benefit?
Example: A citizen knows a judge is overseeing their case and sends a $500 gift basket to the judge's office. This is Offering a Gift to a Public Servant.
Abuse of Office

39.02. Abuse of Official Capacity

A public servant, with intent to obtain a benefit or to harm/defraud another, intentionally or knowingly: 1) violates a law relating to their office, or 2) misuses government property, services, or personnel.

Punishment: Follows the theft value ladder, from Class C Misdemeanor to 1st Degree Felony.

Elements Checklist:

  • Is the person a `public servant`?
  • Did they act with intent to get a `benefit` OR intent to `harm or defraud` another?
  • Did they `intentionally or knowingly` violate a law related to their job OR misuse government property/personnel?
Example: A city employee uses a city-owned truck and tools over the weekend to run their personal landscaping business. The value of the use determines the punishment level.

39.03. Official Oppression

A public servant acting under color of office intentionally: 1) subjects another to unlawful mistreatment, arrest, search, seizure, etc., 2) denies or impedes another's rights, or 3) subjects another to sexual harassment.

Punishment: Class A Misdemeanor.

Elements Checklist:

  • Is the person a `public servant` acting `under color of office`?
  • Did they `intentionally` do one of the following:
  • A) Subject another to `unlawful` mistreatment, arrest, search, or seizure?
  • B) Deny or impede another's rights, `knowing` it was unlawful?
  • C) Subject another to `sexual harassment`?
Example: During a traffic stop, an officer tells the driver, "You're too sexy for a ticket. How about you give me your number instead?" This is sexual harassment under Official Oppression.

39.04. Improper Sexual Activity with Person in Custody

An official, employee, or volunteer of a correctional/juvenile facility, or a peace officer, intentionally engages in sexual contact, intercourse, or deviate sexual intercourse with an individual in custody or under supervision.

Punishment: State Jail Felony to 1st Degree Felony depending on the circumstances.

Elements Checklist:

  • Is the actor an official, employee, volunteer of a facility, or a peace officer?
  • Did they `intentionally` engage in `sexual contact, sexual intercourse, or deviate sexual intercourse`?
  • Was the other individual `in custody` or under official supervision?
Example: A peace officer transports a detainee to jail and, during the transport, engages in sexual contact with them. This is a violation regardless of consent.

39.05. Failure to Report Death of Prisoner

A person required to investigate and file a report on an in-custody death (under CCP Art. 49.18) fails to do so, or files a report omitting known facts.

Punishment: Class B Misdemeanor.

Elements Checklist:

  • Was the person `required` by law to investigate and report an in-custody death?
  • Did they fail to investigate, fail to file the report, OR fail to include known facts in the report?
Example: The chief of a law enforcement agency knows specific details about an inmate's death but intentionally leaves those facts out of the official report filed with the attorney general.

39.06. Misuse of Official Information

A public servant uses non-public information for personal gain, to speculate, or to coerce another into not reporting information to law enforcement.

Punishment: 3rd Degree Felony (or Class C Misdemeanor for coercing someone not to report).

Elements Checklist:

  • Is the person a `public servant`?
  • Did they rely on `information not made public` that they had access to because of their job?
  • Did they use that information to acquire a financial interest, speculate, OR coerce someone from reporting a crime?
Example: A city planning official learns that a new highway exit will be built in a specific location. Before this information is public, they tell a family member to buy the adjacent land to profit from the future development.
Witnesses, Informants & Impersonation

36.05. Tampering with Witness

With intent to influence, a person offers a benefit to or coerces a witness/prospective witness to testify falsely, withhold testimony, elude process, or absent themselves from a proceeding.

Punishment: 3rd Degree Felony, but it's enhanced to the same level as the underlying criminal case.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person act with `intent to influence` a witness or prospective witness?
  • Did they `offer a benefit` or `coerce` the witness?
  • Was the purpose to make the witness testify falsely, withhold testimony, elude a summons, or not show up?
Example: The defendant in a theft case (Class A Misd.) offers a witness $500 to change their testimony. The defendant has committed Tampering with Witness, a 3rd Degree Felony.

36.06. Obstruction or Retaliation

A person intentionally or knowingly harms or threatens to harm another by an unlawful act in retaliation for or to prevent their service as a public servant, witness, informant, or person reporting a crime.

Punishment: 3rd Degree Felony, enhanced to a 2nd Degree Felony if the victim is a juror.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person `intentionally or knowingly` harm or threaten to harm another?
  • Was the harm or threat an `unlawful act`?
  • Was it `in retaliation for` (or to prevent) the victim's service as a public servant, witness, informant, or crime reporter?
Example: After being arrested for a parole violation, an individual tells the arresting officer, "Wait 'til I get out, you're going to pay for this in blood." This is a threat of harm in retaliation for the officer's service.

37.11. Impersonating Public Servant

A person impersonates a public servant with intent to induce another to submit to their pretended authority OR knowingly purports to exercise any function of a public office without legal authority.

Punishment: 3rd Degree Felony.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person `impersonate` a public servant?
  • Did they do so with `intent to make someone submit` to their fake authority? OR
  • Did they `knowingly` try to `exercise a function of a public office` without legal authority?
Example: A man puts a flashing red light on his car, pulls someone over, and demands to see their license and registration, pretending to be a police officer.

37.12. False Identification as Peace Officer

A person makes, provides, or possesses a fake badge, ID, or other item bearing a law enforcement insignia, knowing the person identified is not a peace officer.

Punishment: Class B Misdemeanor.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person make, provide, or possess an item (badge, ID, etc.) with a law enforcement insignia?
  • Did the item identify someone as a peace officer?
  • Did the actor `know` the person identified was not actually a peace officer?
Example: A person buys a realistic-looking "Sheriff" badge online and keeps it in their wallet, knowing they are not a law enforcement officer.

Offenses Against the Family & Children

Marriage, Support & Custody

25.01. Bigamy

A person who is legally married purports to marry or lives with another person under the appearance of being married.

Punishment: 3rd Degree Felony. Enhanced based on the age of the other person.

Elements Checklist:

  • Is the person legally `married`?
  • Did they then purport to marry someone else OR live with someone else `under the appearance of being married`?
Example: A man who is legally married in Texas travels to another state and marries a second woman without divorcing his first wife.

25.05. Criminal Nonsupport

A person intentionally or knowingly fails to provide support for their child younger than 18.

Punishment: State Jail Felony.

Elements Checklist:

  • Is the person a parent required to provide child support?
  • Is the child younger than 18?
  • Did the person `intentionally or knowingly` fail to provide that support?
Example: A parent who is under a court order to pay child support stops making payments for several months, despite having the financial ability to do so.

25.03. Interference with Child Custody

A person takes or retains a child younger than 18, knowing it violates a court order.

Punishment: State Jail Felony.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person take or keep a child under 18?
  • Did the person `know` their action violated a court order related to custody?
Example: A non-custodial parent picks up their child for a weekend visit but refuses to return the child on Sunday evening as required by the custody agreement.

25.031. Agreement to Abduct from Custody

A person agrees, for remuneration (payment), to abduct a child younger than 18 by force, threat, or stealth, knowing it's in violation of a custody order.

Punishment: State Jail Felony.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person make an `agreement`?
  • Was the agreement for `payment` (remuneration)?
  • Was the purpose to `abduct` a child under 18?
  • Did the actor `know` the child was under the control of a court order?
Example: A parent pays someone to forcibly take their child from the other parent, who has legal custody.
Enticing, Harboring, and Grooming

25.04. Enticing a Child

With intent to interfere with lawful custody, a person knowingly entices, persuades, or takes a child younger than 18 from their parent or guardian.

Punishment: Class B Misdemeanor. Enhanced to a 3rd Degree Felony if the actor intended to commit a felony against the child.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person act with `intent to interfere with lawful custody`?
  • Did they `knowingly` entice, persuade, or take a child under 18?
  • Was the child taken from a parent, guardian, or similar custodian?
Example: An adult who is not a parent or guardian convinces a 15-year-old to leave home and come stay with them, without the parents' permission.

25.06. Harboring Runaway Child

A person knowingly harbors a child and is criminally negligent about whether the child (younger than 18) is a runaway or has escaped from custody.

Punishment: Class A Misdemeanor.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person `knowingly harbor` (shelter) a child?
  • Were they `criminally negligent` about whether the child (under 18) was a runaway?
Example: An adult lets their child's 16-year-old friend, who they know has run away from home, stay at their house for a week without notifying the parents or police.

15.032. Child Grooming

With intent that a sexual offense be committed, a person knowingly persuades, entices, or coerces a child younger than 18 to engage in specific conduct that would constitute that sexual offense.

Punishment: 3rd Degree Felony, enhanced to a 2nd Degree Felony if the actor has a previous conviction for certain sexual offenses.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person act with the `intent` that a sexual offense be committed?
  • Did they `knowingly` persuade, entice, or coerce a child under 18?
  • Was the goal to make the child engage in conduct that would be a sexual offense?
Example: An adult communicates with a 15-year-old online, developing a relationship with the intent to eventually persuade them to meet for a sexual act. The act of persuading/enticing with that intent is Child Grooming.
Sale & Purchase of a Child

25.08. Sale or Purchase of Child

A person offers to accept or accepts a thing of value for the delivery or possession of a child for the purposes of adoption.

Punishment: 3rd Degree Felony.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person possess, or have custody of, a child under 18?
  • Did they offer to accept, or accept, a `thing of value`?
  • Was this in exchange for delivering the child to another for the purpose of `adoption`? (Also applies to the buyer).
Example: A person offers another person $10,000 to acquire their infant child for an illegal adoption. Both the person offering and the person accepting have committed the offense.

25.09. Advertising for Placement of Child

A person advertises in public media to place, provide, or obtain a child for adoption.

Punishment: Class A Misdemeanor.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person `advertise` in public media (including internet)?
  • Was the advertisement to place, provide, or obtain a child for `adoption`?
Example: A person posts on a public social media page that they are looking to "find a good home for" their child in exchange for money, outside of any legal adoption agency process.

Public Order & Gambling

Public Nuisance & Safety

42.061. Silent or Abusive Calls to 9-1-1 Service

A person makes a call to a 9-1-1 service when there is no emergency and knowingly/intentionally remains silent or makes abusive/harassing statements.

Punishment: Class B Misdemeanor.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person call 9-1-1 when there was `no emergency`?
  • Did they `knowingly or intentionally` remain silent OR make abusive/harassing statements?
Example: A person repeatedly calls 9-1-1 to complain about their neighbor's dog barking, tying up emergency lines.

42.14. Illumination of Aircraft by Intense Light

A person intentionally directs a light from a laser pointer at an aircraft.

Punishment: Class C Misdemeanor. Enhanced to Class A if it impairs the operator's ability to control the aircraft.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person `intentionally` direct a light at an aircraft?
  • Was the light from a `laser pointer` or other intense source?
Example: A person on the ground aims a green laser pointer at a helicopter flying overhead.

48.05. Prohibited Camping

A person intentionally or knowingly camps in a public place without the effective consent of the agency managing the place.

Punishment: Class C Misdemeanor.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person `intentionally or knowingly` "camp" (reside temporarily with shelter)?
  • Was it in a `public place`?
  • Was it `without effective consent` from the managing authority?
Example: A person sets up a tent and sleeping bag on a public sidewalk and lives there for several days without permission from the city.

50.02. Unlawful Use of Fireworks

A person explodes or ignites fireworks with the intent to interfere with a law enforcement officer or flee from a lawful arrest/detention.

Punishment: State Jail Felony. Enhanced to 1st Degree Felony if it causes SBI to an officer.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person explode or ignite `fireworks`?
  • Did they do so with the `intent to interfere` with an officer's duties OR `flee` from a lawful arrest?
Example: As an officer attempts to arrest a suspect, the suspect's friend begins shooting Roman candles at the officer to create a distraction and help the suspect get away.
Gambling

47.02. Gambling

A person: 1) makes a bet on a game/contest, or 2) plays and bets for money at any game with cards, dice, etc.

Punishment: Class C Misdemeanor.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person make a `bet` on the outcome of a game, contest, or election? OR
  • Did they play and bet for `money or other thing of value` at a game?
Example: Two people bet $20 on the outcome of a football game.

47.03. Gambling Promotion

A person intentionally or knowingly operates or participates in the earnings of a gambling place or engages in bookmaking.

Punishment: Class A Misdemeanor.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person `intentionally or knowingly` do one of the following:
  • A) Operate or share in the profits of a `gambling place`?
  • B) Engage in `bookmaking`?
  • C) For gain, become a custodian of bet money?
Example: The owner of an illegal "game room" profits from the electronic slot machines operating inside.

47.04. Keeping a Gambling Place

A person knowingly uses or permits another to use property they own or control as a gambling place.

Punishment: Class A Misdemeanor.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person `knowingly` use property they own/control as a gambling place? OR
  • Did they `knowingly` permit another to use it as a gambling place?
Example: A property owner knowingly rents out a warehouse to a person who is using it to run an illegal casino.

47.05. Communicating Gambling Information

With intent to further gambling, a person knowingly communicates information on bets or odds.

Punishment: Class A Misdemeanor.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person act with `intent to further gambling`?
  • Did they `knowingly communicate` information about bets, odds, etc.?
Example: A bookie sends out text messages with the point spreads for upcoming basketball games.

47.06. Possession of Gambling Device, Equipment, or Paraphernalia

With intent to further gambling, a person knowingly possesses a gambling device or altered equipment (e.g., loaded dice).

Punishment: Class A Misdemeanor.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person act with `intent to further gambling`?
  • Did they `knowingly` own, make, transfer, or possess a `gambling device`, `altered equipment`, or `paraphernalia`?
Example: During a raid on an illegal poker game, officers find a player in possession of a deck of marked cards.

Inchoate & Organized Crime

Inchoate (Preparatory) Offenses

15.02. Criminal Conspiracy

A person, with intent that a felony be committed, agrees with one or more persons to engage in the conduct, AND one of them performs an overt act in pursuance of the agreement.

Punishment: One category lower than the object felony.

Elements Checklist:

  • Was there an `intent` for a felony to be committed?
  • Did the person `agree` with at least one other person to commit the felony?
  • Did at least one of the conspirators perform an `overt act` to further the agreement?
Example: Alice and Bob agree to rob a bank. Bob then goes and buys ski masks and a getaway car. Both have committed Criminal Conspiracy because there was an agreement and an overt act (buying masks/car).

15.03. Criminal Solicitation

With intent that a capital felony or 1st degree felony be committed, a person requests, commands, or attempts to induce another to engage in the conduct.

Punishment: 2nd Degree (for a 1st Degree Felony) or 1st Degree (for a Capital Felony).

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person act with `intent` that a Capital or 1st Degree Felony be committed?
  • Did they `request, command, or attempt to induce` another person to commit it?
Example: Mario calls Dormir and tells him he "needs some thieving cockroach to sleep with the fishes," offering his "normal fee." Mario has solicited Dormir to commit murder.

15.031. Criminal Solicitation of a Minor

With intent that a listed offense be committed (e.g., Murder, Aggravated Robbery), a person requests or attempts to induce a minor (under 17) to engage in the conduct.

Punishment: One category lower than the solicited offense (with exceptions for gang members).

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person act with `intent` for a serious felony to be committed?
  • Did they request, command, or attempt to induce a `minor` (under 17) to commit it?
Example: A gang leader tells a 16-year-old prospective member to go rob a convenience store to prove his loyalty.

15.04. Renunciation Defense

An affirmative defense where the actor voluntarily and completely renounces their criminal objective and makes a substantial effort to prevent the offense.

Elements Checklist:

  • Is this for an `inchoate` offense (Attempt, Conspiracy, Solicitation)?
  • Did the actor `voluntarily and completely` renounce their criminal objective?
  • Did they take `affirmative action` that prevented the commission of the crime?
Example: After agreeing to a burglary, one conspirator has a change of heart, calls the police to report the plan, and talks the other conspirators out of it before the crime occurs.
Organized Crime & Terrorism

71.02. Engaging in Organized Criminal Activity

With intent to establish, maintain, or participate in a combination (3+ people) or criminal street gang, the person commits or conspires to commit one or more listed offenses.

Punishment: One category higher than the most serious offense committed.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person act with `intent to participate` in a `combination` (3+ people) or `criminal street gang`?
  • While having that intent, did they `commit or conspire to commit` one of the offenses listed in the statute?
Example: A person commits a Burglary of a Habitation (a 2nd Degree Felony). If it's proven they did this with the intent to participate in a criminal street gang they are a member of, the charge is enhanced to a 1st Degree Felony under this statute.

71.022. Coercing, Inducing, or Soliciting Membership in a Gang

A person knowingly recruits or solicits another to become a member of a criminal street gang, requiring an offense as a condition of membership.

Punishment: 3rd Degree Felony.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person `knowingly` coerce, recruit, or solicit someone into a `criminal street gang`?
  • Did joining the gang require the commission of a crime?
Example: A gang member tells a teenager they can join the gang, but only after they successfully steal a car.

76.02. Terrorism

A person commits a listed offense (e.g., Murder, Arson, Aggravated Robbery) with the intent to intimidate or coerce the public or influence government policy.

Punishment: One category higher than the underlying offense, with minimum terms of confinement.

Elements Checklist:

  • Did the person commit (or conspire to commit) one of the listed offenses (like Murder, Arson, etc.)?
  • Did they do it with the specific `intent to intimidate/coerce the public` OR `influence government policy`?
Example: A person commits Arson by setting fire to a government building with the stated goal of influencing a piece of legislation. The Arson charge can be enhanced under the Terrorism statute.

Federal Law & Civil Penalties

Federal Criminal Civil Rights Statutes

18 U.S.C. ¬ß 241 - Conspiracy Against Rights

Makes it unlawful for two or more persons to conspire to injure, oppress, threaten, or intimidate any person in the free exercise of any right secured by the U.S. Constitution or laws.

Example: Two or more officers agree ahead of time to plant evidence on a suspect to ensure a conviction.

18 U.S.C. ¬ß 242 - Deprivation of Rights Under Color of Law

Makes it a crime for any person acting "under color of law" to willfully deprive a person of a right or privilege protected by the U.S. Constitution or laws.

Example: An officer willfully uses excessive force during an arrest, resulting in bodily injury. This is the primary statute used to criminally prosecute individual officers for such civil rights violations.
Civil Penalties & Sexual Harassment

Civil Liability

In addition to criminal charges, officers can be subject to civil lawsuits. The most common is a 42 U.S.C. ¬ß 1983 lawsuit for civil rights violations.

Sexual harassment can also lead to civil penalties for the officer and their department under rules from the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) and the Texas Commission on Human Rights.
