Family Violence

An Interactive Study Guide

Dynamics of Family Violence

Key Principle: No "Typical" Victim or Suspect

Family violence transcends all walks of life, affecting people of varying backgrounds, cultures, ages, education levels, occupations, and economic statuses. There is no single profile for a victim or an abuser.

Common Characteristics of Offenders
  • Poor self-image and feels internally inadequate.
  • Extreme jealousy and constant accusations of infidelity.
  • Blames violence on others (stress, partner's behavior, alcohol).
  • Holds rigid gender role beliefs (e.g., male as head of house).
  • May be a public "charmer" but violent in private.
  • Uses violence to solve problems because it has worked in the past.
  • Denies or minimizes the seriousness of the violence.
  • Often witnessed or was a victim of family violence as a child.
Victim Effects & Barriers to Leaving

Psychological & Emotional Effects on Victims:

  • Shame, blame, and guilt for the violence.
  • Denial, minimization, or making excuses for the abuser.
  • Emotional withdrawal and isolation from friends and family.
  • Depression, suicidal thoughts, and potential substance abuse.
  • Distrust of others and the legal system.

Common Reasons Victims Stay:

  • Hope that the abuser will change.
  • Economic and emotional dependence.
  • Fear of retribution or losing custody of children if they leave.
  • Religious or cultural beliefs that discourage divorce or separation.
  • Lack of awareness of available resources and support systems.
  • Threats related to immigration status.

Models & Types of Abuse

Cycle of Violence vs. Power & Control Wheel

While the "Cycle of Violence" (Tension, Explosion, Honeymoon phases) is a known model, many victims report that the Power & Control Wheel more accurately describes their experience. It is the preferred model for discussing offender behavior.

8 Behaviors of the Power & Control Wheel:

  • Coercion & Threats: Making and/or carrying out threats to do something to hurt her.
  • Intimidation: Making her afraid by using looks, actions, gestures.
  • Emotional Abuse: Putting her down, making her feel bad about herself.
  • Isolation: Controlling what she does, who she sees, what she reads, where she goes.
  • Minimizing, Denying, and Blaming: Making light of the abuse and not taking her concerns about it seriously.
  • Using Children: Making her feel guilty about the children.
  • Economic Abuse: Preventing her from getting or keeping a job.
  • Male Privilege: Treating her like a servant; making all the big decisions.
The Three Types of Abuse

Physical Abuse

  • Spitting, pinching, shoving
  • Throwing things
  • Slapping, hitting, punching
  • Kicking, stomping
  • Strangling, shooting, burning

Emotional Abuse

  • Withholding affection
  • Constant monitoring
  • Verbal abuse, name-calling
  • Isolation from family/friends
  • Humiliating or degrading
  • Injuring pets, destroying property

Sexual Abuse

  • Forced sex after a beating
  • Not allowing use of birth control
  • Use of objects during sex
  • Forced sex with others
  • Forced sex in front of children

Legal Framework for Family Violence

Key Family Code Definitions
  • Family (FC 71.003): Includes individuals related by blood or marriage, former spouses, parents of the same child, and foster parents/children.
  • Household (FC 71.005): Persons living together in the same dwelling, regardless of relationship.
  • Dating Relationship (FC 71.0021): A continuing relationship of a romantic or intimate nature.
  • Family Violence (FC 71.004): An act by a family/household member against another intended to cause harm, injury, assault, or a threat that places them in fear of imminent harm.
Key Penal Code Offenses

Assault by Impeding Breath/Circulation (Strangulation) - 3rd Degree Felony

Reference: PC 22.01(b)(2)(B). An assault causing bodily injury is enhanced to a 3rd Degree Felony if the actor intentionally, knowingly, or recklessly impedes the normal breathing or circulation of a family/household member by applying pressure to the throat/neck or blocking the nose/mouth.

Continuous Violence Against the Family - 3rd Degree Felony

Reference: PC 25.11. A person commits this offense if, during a period of 12 months or less, they commit two or more assaults causing bodily injury against a family/household member. The prior acts do not need to have resulted in a conviction.

Officer Arrest Authority (CCP 14.03)

A peace officer MAY arrest without a warrant if they have probable cause to believe a person has committed:

  • An offense involving family violence.
  • A violation of a protective order (if not committed in the officer's presence).
  • Interference with an Emergency Request for Assistance.

Mandatory Arrest: A peace officer SHALL arrest without a warrant if they have probable cause to believe a person has violated a protective order in the officer's presence.

Officer Response & Investigation

Identifying the Predominant Aggressor

A thorough and objective investigation is required to determine the predominant aggressor, rather than making dual arrests. Consider the following:

  • Who poses the most danger to the other?
  • Is one party in fear of the other?
  • Is there a history of violence by one party?
  • Was the amount of force used proportionate and reasonable?
  • Is one party physically larger and stronger?
  • Do the injuries appear to be offensive or defensive?
  • Is one person overly helpful while the other is fearful?
Officer Duties & Procedures (CCP 5.04 & 5.05)

Primary Duties:

  • Protect any potential victim.
  • Enforce the law and make lawful arrests.
  • Enforce protective orders.
  • Provide the adult victim with a written notice of their legal rights and available community services (in English and Spanish).

Reporting Requirements:

  • A written report is required for any call that may involve family violence.
  • The report must include names, date/time/location, injuries, incident description, and whether the suspect is in the military.
  • If the address is a licensed foster home, a report must be made to DFPS within 24 hours.
  • If the suspect is in the military, written notice must be sent to the appropriate military authority.
