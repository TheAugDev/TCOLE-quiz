The Preliminary Investigation

5 Stages of a Preliminary Investigation

  1. Protection and Security of a Scene: The first priority is ensuring the safety of all individuals and securing the scene from contamination.
  2. First Responder Investigation: The initial on-scene investigation conducted by the first responding officer(s).
  3. Processing the Crime Scene: The systematic search for and collection of evidence.
  4. Scene Wrap-up: Final review of the scene and evidence collection before release.
  5. Conclude and Document: Completing all necessary reports and documenting the investigation.
Procedures Upon Arrival

Initial Assessment:

  • Approach cautiously and be alert for unusual activity (persons, vehicles, suspects fleeing).
  • Assess the number of suspects and if weapons are involved.
  • Request appropriate backup if needed.
  • Use appropriate tactics for crimes in progress (e.g., safe driving, waiting for backup, communicating with dispatch).

First Responder Duties:

  • Establish if a crime has been committed and determine its type.
  • If an eyewitness is present, get descriptions (suspect, vehicle, direction of flight) and broadcast the information immediately.
  • Locate and separate victims and witnesses for interviews.
  • Protect the crime scene from contamination.
  • Record all data in field notes, including observations, statements, and evidence details.

Legal Authority & Warrantless Entry

Curtilage
The area of open space immediately surrounding a dwelling, considered part of the house and protected by the 4th Amendment.
Open Fields
Areas outside the curtilage. Under the "Open Fields Doctrine," officers may enter and search these areas without a warrant.
Determining Curtilage: The 4 Factors (U.S. v. Dunn)
  1. Distance: The proximity of the area to the home.
  2. Enclosure: Whether the area is within an enclosure surrounding the home.
  3. Nature of Use: The nature of the uses to which the area is put (e.g., intimate home activities).
  4. Protection from Observation: The steps taken by the resident to protect the area from observation.
Key Case Law on Warrantless Entry & Searches
Brigham City, Utah v. Stuart: Police may enter a home without a warrant if they have an objectively reasonable basis for believing an occupant is seriously injured or imminently threatened with such injury (exigent circumstances).
Payton v. New York: Absent exigent circumstances or consent, police may NOT enter a person's home to make a routine felony arrest without a warrant.
Maryland v. Buie: Incident to an arrest, officers may conduct a "protective sweep" of the premises, limited to a cursory inspection of spaces where a person may be found, if they have reasonable suspicion that the area harbors an individual posing a danger.
Chimel v. California: A warrantless search incident to a lawful arrest can only cover the area within the arrestee's immediate control ("wingspan").

Crime Scene Management

Securing the Crime Scene

Priorities:

The priorities at any scene are PEOPLE first, then EVIDENCE. Ensure the scene is safe, render aid, and then focus on preservation.

Establishing Perimeters:

  • Establish defined entry and exit points to create a single path in and out.
  • Use ropes, barricades, or crime scene tape to create an inner (crime scene) and outer (buffer zone) perimeter.
  • Maintain an entry log to document everyone who enters and exits the scene. The chain of custody begins with controlling access.
Evidence & Victim Identification

Evidence Preservation:

  • Do NOT touch, move, or handle any evidence until it has been photographed, unless it poses an immediate threat.
  • Alert other responders (EMS, Fire) to the location of evidence to minimize destruction.
  • If a weapon must be moved for safety, do so with gloved hands, move it as little as possible, and document the action.

Identifying Victims:

  • Serious Injury: If the person is alive and conscious, obtain their identification and next of kin information.
  • Death: Do NOT move or disturb the body. Obtain permission from the Medical Examiner or Justice of the Peace before attempting to locate identification on the deceased.
