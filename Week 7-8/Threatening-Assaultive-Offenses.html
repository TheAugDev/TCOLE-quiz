Threatening & Assaultive Offenses

An Interactive Study Guide

Threatening & Intimidation Offenses

Key Family Code Definitions
  • Family Violence (FC 71.004): An act by a family/household member against another intended to cause harm, injury, assault, or a threat that places them in fear of imminent harm.
  • Household (FC 71.005): A unit of persons living together in the same dwelling, regardless of relation. Includes people who previously lived there.
  • Dating Relationship (FC 71.0021): A continuing relationship of a romantic or intimate nature. Determined by length, nature, and frequency of interaction.
Terroristic Threat (PC 22.07)

A person commits an offense if they threaten violence to a person or property with a specific intent:

Class B Misdemeanor: Intent to cause a reaction by an emergency agency OR place a person in fear of imminent SBI.
Class A Misdemeanor: Intent to place a family member or public servant in fear of imminent SBI OR to prevent/interrupt use of a public place.
State Jail Felony: Intent to place a peace officer/judge in fear of imminent SBI OR pecuniary loss is $1,500+ from interrupting a public place.
3rd Degree Felony: Intent to cause impairment of public services, place the public in fear of SBI, or influence government.
Harassment (PC 42.07) & Stalking (PC 42.072)
Harassment (Class B Misdemeanor): With intent to harass, annoy, alarm, etc., a person makes obscene calls, threatens BI, causes a phone to ring repeatedly, or sends repeated harassing electronic communications.
Stalking (3rd Degree Felony): On more than one occasion, knowingly engages in conduct (including harassment) that they know will be regarded as threatening, causing the victim or their family to be placed in fear or feel harassed/alarmed. Enhanced to a 2nd Degree Felony with a prior conviction.

Physically Assaultive Offenses

Assault (PC 22.01)
(a)(2) & (a)(3) - Class C Misdemeanor: Intentionally or knowingly threatening another with imminent bodily injury OR causing offensive physical contact.
(a)(1) - Class A Misdemeanor: Intentionally, knowingly, or recklessly causing bodily injury to another.
3rd Degree Felony Enhancement: Assault (a)(1) becomes a 3rd Degree Felony if against a public servant, security officer, EMS, or a family member with a prior FV conviction or by impeding breath (strangulation).
2nd Degree Felony Enhancement: Assault (a)(1) becomes a 2nd Degree Felony if against a peace officer or judge, or is a second FV assault involving impeding breath.
Aggravated Assault (PC 22.02)

An assault becomes Aggravated Assault if it causes serious bodily injury OR the actor uses or exhibits a deadly weapon.

2nd Degree Felony: Base offense level for Aggravated Assault.
1st Degree Felony: Enhanced if against a public servant, in a drive-by shooting causing SBI, or against a family member causing SBI with a weapon.
Injury to a Child, Elderly, or Disabled Individual (PC 22.04)

An offense if a person, by act or omission, causes SBI, serious mental injury, or bodily injury to a protected individual.

State Jail Felony: Criminal negligence causing BI.
3rd Degree Felony: Recklessly causing BI or Intentionally/Knowingly causing BI.
2nd Degree Felony: Recklessly causing SBI.
1st Degree Felony: Intentionally/Knowingly causing SBI.
Other Assaultive Offenses
Deadly Conduct (Reckless): Recklessly engaging in conduct that places another in imminent danger of SBI.
Deadly Conduct (Discharging Firearm): Knowingly discharging a firearm at or in the direction of individuals, a habitation, building, or vehicle.
Abandoning or Endangering: Engaging in conduct that places a child, elderly, or disabled person in imminent danger of death/BI/impairment.
Leaving a Child in a Vehicle: Intentionally or knowingly leaving a child younger than 7 in a vehicle for more than 5 minutes, unless attended by someone 14 or older.

Unlawful Restraint & Kidnapping (Chapter 20)

Restrain (PC 20.01): To restrict a person's movements without consent, so as to interfere substantially with their liberty, by moving them or confining them.
Abduct (PC 20.01): To restrain a person with intent to prevent their liberation by either secreting them where they are not likely to be found, or using or threatening to use deadly force.
Unlawful Restraint (PC 20.02)
Class A Misdemeanor: The base offense for intentionally or knowingly restraining another.
State Jail Felony: If the victim is younger than 17.
3rd Degree Felony: If the actor recklessly exposes the victim to a substantial risk of SBI, or if the victim is a public servant.
2nd Degree Felony: If the victim is a known peace officer or judge.
Kidnapping & Aggravated Kidnapping
Kidnapping (PC 20.03): Intentionally or knowingly abducting another person. A 3rd Degree Felony.
Aggravated Kidnapping (PC 20.04): Abducting another with intent to hold for ransom, use as a shield, facilitate a felony, inflict BI/sexual abuse, terrorize, or interfere with government. A 1st Degree Felony. (Can be reduced to 2nd Degree if victim is released voluntarily in a safe place).

Justification & Use of Force (Chapter 9)

General Principles of Justification
  • Justification as a Defense (PC 9.02): It is a defense to prosecution that the conduct in question is justified under this chapter.
  • Threats as Justifiable Force (PC 9.04): Producing a weapon to create an apprehension that deadly force will be used if necessary does NOT constitute the use of deadly force.
  • Reckless Injury of Innocent Third Person (PC 9.05): Justification is unavailable in a prosecution for recklessly injuring or killing an innocent third person.
  • No Duty to Retreat (PC 9.31/9.32): A person who has a right to be present, has not provoked the other, and is not engaged in criminal activity is NOT required to retreat before using justified force.
Self-Defense & Defense of Others (PC 9.31, 9.32, 9.33)

A person is justified in using force when they reasonably believe the force is immediately necessary to protect against another's unlawful force.

When is Deadly Force Justified?

When the actor reasonably believes deadly force is immediately necessary to protect against unlawful deadly force OR to prevent the imminent commission of aggravated kidnapping, murder, sexual assault, aggravated sexual assault, robbery, or aggravated robbery.

Castle Doctrine (PC 9.32(b)): The actor's belief that deadly force was necessary is presumed reasonable if the person against whom it was used was unlawfully and with force entering (or attempting to enter) the actor's occupied habitation, vehicle, or place of business.

Protection of Property (PC 9.41, 9.42)

A person is justified in using force to prevent a trespass or unlawful interference with property.

Deadly force is justified to protect property only to prevent the imminent commission of arson, burglary, robbery, aggravated robbery, or theft/criminal mischief during the nighttime, AND the property cannot be protected by other means or using lesser force would create a substantial risk of death or SBI.
