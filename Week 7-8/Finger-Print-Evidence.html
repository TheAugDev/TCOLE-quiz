Fingerprint Patterns & Identification Points

The 3 Basic Fingerprint Patterns

Loops (60%)

Ridges enter from one side, curve back, and exit on the same side. Has one delta.

Whorls (35%)

Ridges form a circular or combination pattern. Has two deltas.

Arches (5%)

Ridges enter from one side, rise in the center, and exit on the opposite side. Has no deltas.

Fingerprint Identification Points (Minutiae)
Bifurcation: A single ridge forks and becomes two or more ridges.
Ending Ridge: A ridge that makes a sudden stop.
Ridge Island: A ridge that forks and then joins back into one ridge, forming a small island.
Dot: An isolated, small ridge that looks like a dot.
Short Ridge: A ridge that is noticeably shorter than the other ridges in the area.
Divergence (Delta): The area where two parallel ridges separate to surround the pattern area.

Print Composition & Surface Types

Latent Print Composition (The Matrix)

The substance left behind on a surface is called the matrix. The hands and feet only have eccrine glands, which are sweat glands.

  • Therefore, 99% - 99.5% of a "clean" fingerprint's matrix is WATER.
  • Oils and other substances found in prints are contaminants picked up from touching other parts of the body (like the forehead or nose, which have sebaceous glands) or the environment.
Surface Types and Their Effect on Prints

Porous Surfaces

Absorb the water content of the print quickly, leaving other residues behind.

Examples: Paper, untreated wood, cardboard.

Processing: Non-powder methods are best.

Semi-Porous Surfaces

Slowly absorb the water content over minutes to hours.

Examples: Waxed surfaces, varnished wood, some paints.

Processing: Dusting can be successful depending on the age of the print.

Non-Porous Surfaces

Do not absorb any part of the print matrix. Prints are very fragile.

Examples: Glass, plastic, smooth metal.

Processing: Dusting methods typically have the best success.

Collection & Processing Techniques

Dusting & Lifting Latent Prints

Important Considerations:

  • Visible prints (e.g., in blood, grease, or dust) should be photographed and NOT dusted, as powder will destroy them.
  • Ideally, all latent prints should be photographed before lifting.
  • If a print is faint, you can try to re-hydrate it with a soft exhale of breath, let it dry, and then re-brush.

Dusting Procedure:

  1. Ensure your brush is clean and the bristles are separated.
  2. Use a very small amount of powder.
  3. Brush lightly with only the tips of the bristles, using short, quick strokes.
  4. Follow the natural flow of the ridges until the print becomes visible.

Lifting Procedure:

  1. Use enough tape to cover the entire print, with a "handle" folded on each end.
  2. "Pop" the tape to remove curl and twist the ends to keep it flat.
  3. Apply the tape starting from the center and rubbing outwards to avoid air bubbles.
  4. Peel the tape gently from the surface to lift the print.
Rolling Fingerprints

Proper technique is crucial for obtaining clear, usable prints for a ten-print card.

  • Start with the right hand, from thumb to little finger.
  • Ink the finger from the tip to just below the first joint.
  • Roll from "nail to nail" for a complete impression.
  • Roll thumbs TOWARD the center of the body.
  • Roll fingers AWAY from the center of the body.
  • The officer should exert the pressure while the subject relaxes their hand.
  • After rolling all ten fingers, take the "plane" (flat) impressions at the bottom of the card.
