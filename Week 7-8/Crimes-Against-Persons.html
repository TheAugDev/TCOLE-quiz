Injury & Forensic Terminology

Types of Wounds

Lacerations

Tears in tissue caused by direct blunt force. Edges are ragged and bruised. Does not correspond to the shape of the instrument.

Incised Wounds

Cuts with regular, sharp, clean-cut edges and no bruising. Bleed freely.

Stab & Puncture Wounds

Piercing injuries that may extend into internal organs. The surface appearance tends to conform to the weapon's pattern.

Gunshot Wounds

Entrance wounds are often smaller than the bullet diameter due to skin stretching. Exit wounds are typically larger, ragged, and torn.

Close-Range Gunshot Wound Indicators
  • Contact Wound: Muzzle held directly against the skin. Edges are torn and charred from heat and expanding gases.
  • Smudging (2-18 inches): A deposit of smoke and soot around the entrance wound. It is on the surface and can be wiped off.
  • Tattooing (2-18 inches): Residue of unburned powder granules driven under the skin. It is permanent and cannot be wiped off.
Postmortem Indicators

Postmortem Lividity

A purplish discoloration on parts of the body nearest the ground due to blood settling. Appears 1-2 hours after death and can indicate if the body was moved.

Rigor Mortis

The stiffening of muscles after death. Starts in the neck/jaw 5-6 hours after death, affects the whole body within 18 hours, and disappears within 36 hours.

Body Temperature

The body cools from its normal 98.6Â°F. The rate of cooling depends on air temperature, clothing, and body size, and can help estimate time of death.

Putrefaction

Decomposition of body tissues. Signs include bloating, skin discoloration, and blisters. The rate is influenced by environmental temperature.

Homicide Investigation Procedures

First Responder Duties at a Homicide Scene
  • Care for any injured persons.
  • Protect the integrity of the crime scene.
  • Determine the offense and identify the victim.
  • Record the scene with notes and sketches; arrange for photos.
  • Obtain names and information of all witnesses.
  • Interview witnesses and separate them to preserve individual statements.
  • Determine (if possible) the time, place, cause, and means of death.
  • Ascertain the victim's background and activities to develop a motive.
Child Fatality Investigations

Neonaticide (Death of a newborn within 24 hours)

To prosecute, investigators must link the child to the mother, show the death was an intentional act or willful omission, and establish that the child was born alive (e.g., air in lungs, food in stomach).

SIDS & SUID Investigations

  • SIDS (Sudden Infant Death Syndrome): A medical diagnosis for an unexplained death of an infant under 1 year. Officers should NOT use this term at the scene.
  • SUID (Sudden Unexpected Infant Death): A broader term for deaths where the cause is not obvious. Can be SIDS, accidental suffocation, neglect, or homicide.
  • Any infant death over 6 months should bring suspicion of abuse; 12 months is considered too old for a SIDS death.
  • First responders must ask detailed questions about the infant's health history, last meal, sleeping position, bedding, and scene conditions.
Suicide Investigations

A suicide ruling is only made after a thorough investigation; the initial presumption is accidental death. Be aware that family may suppress evidence like notes or medicine containers.

Key Considerations:

  • Firearms: Is the wound consistent with the weapon? Check for powder burns. Where is the weapon in relation to the victim?
  • Notes: Preserve any suicide notes for fingerprint, DNA, and handwriting analysis.
  • Hanging: Marks on the neck should be high. Do not cut the knot when taking the body down; cut above it to preserve it as evidence.
  • Cuttings: Look for "hesitation marks" on the wrists.
  • Struggle: Always look for signs of a struggle (injuries, disturbed items) that might contradict a suicide ruling.

Key Evidence Types

Bloodstain Evidence

Bloodstain patterns can reveal the origin of the blood, direction of impact, speed of the force, and the positions and movements of the victim and assailant.

Spatter Velocity

  • Low Velocity (<5 ft/s): Large drops (4-8mm), from dripping blood or cast-off.
  • Medium Velocity (25-100 ft/s): Smaller drops, from blunt force trauma (e.g., bat blows).
  • High Velocity (>100 ft/s): Fine mist or spray, from gunshots or machinery.

Key Patterns

  • Cast-off: Blood released from a moving object (e.g., a weapon's backswing).
  • Arterial Spurt: Blood exiting under pressure, showing pressure fluctuations.
  • Back-spatter: Blood traveling back towards the weapon and shooter, seen only at close range.
DNA Evidence

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is unique to an individual (except identical twins) and is a powerful tool for associating victims and suspects with each other or a crime scene.

Common Sources of DNA:

  • Blood
  • Semen
  • Saliva
  • Hair
  • Sweat
  • Body Tissues
  • Bones / Teeth
  • Skin Cells

CODIS: The results of DNA analysis are compared with known samples in the Combined DNA Index System database.
