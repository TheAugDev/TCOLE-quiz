OC Spray

A Comprehensive Study Guide for Law Enforcement

Introduction & History

Training & Effectiveness

User certification is typically a 4 to 8-hour course, with follow-up training recommended every 1-2 years.

An IACP/NIJ study found OC spray to be effective up to 90% of the time in human encounters. The study also showed a significant decrease in assaults on officers and officer injuries when OC was available as a force option.

History of Chemical Agents
  • ~2000 B.C.: Ground peppers in rice paper used in wars between India and China.
  • ~428 B.C.: Spanish forces used fumes from burning pitch and sulfur.
  • WWI: CN (Tear Gas) and Chlorine gas used as chemical weapons. CN was later adopted by law enforcement.
  • 1960s: CS (Riot Gas) becomes the standard riot control agent for the U.S. Military and police.
  • 1990s: A dramatic increase in demand for OC sprays in law enforcement due to a need for alternative force options and to reduce officer injuries.

The Science of OC Spray

Key Definitions
  • Oleoresin Capsicum (OC): An oil and a resin extracted from the capsicum pepper. It is an inflammatory agent.
  • Capsaicin: The most prevalent of the capsaicinoids; the active ingredient in OC.
  • Pungency: The heat or intensity of the pepper.
  • Scoville Heat Units (SHU): A measure of the heat of capsicum. Pure capsaicin is 15,000,000 SHU.
  • Carrier: Transports the agent (OC).
  • Propellant: Propels the carrier from the canister.
Dispenser Nomenclature & Dispersal

Parts of a Dispenser: Actuator (button), Release Valve, Nozzle, Cap, Valve Stem, Canister.

Modes of Dispersal:

  • Cone/Mist: Wide dispersal, effective up to 6-8 feet. Can be affected by wind.
  • Coherent Stream: A targeted stream effective from 4 to 15 feet. Less subject to cross-contamination.
  • Foam: Sticks to the target, effective up to 8 feet.
  • Burst: High-volume discharge for multiple subjects.

Effects & Decontamination

Physiological & Psychological Effects

Physiological Effects:

  • Involuntary closing of the eyes.
  • Burning sensation on the skin.
  • Inflammation of mucous membranes, causing coughing and difficulty breathing.
  • Increased blood flow to affected areas (vasodilation).

Psychological Effects:

  • Anxiety: Fear of the unknown, especially for first-time exposure.
  • Fear: Unpleasant emotion caused by the expectation of pain.
  • Panic: The reality of one's fears. Can lead to fleeing without thought or auditory exclusion (not hearing commands).
Decontamination Procedures

Normal recovery occurs within 15 to 45 minutes.

  1. Remove the subject from the contaminated area.
  2. Establish verbal rapport and begin calming the subject.
  3. Provide physical relief: Flush eyes with copious amounts of cool water and expose to fresh air (face into the wind).
  4. Use a wet paper towel followed by a dry one to blot the face. Do not rub.
  5. Monitor the subject until the effects are no longer persistent. Avoid placing them in a prone position that could restrict breathing.

Call for Medical Aid If:

  • The subject loses consciousness.
  • Symptoms persist after 1 hour of decontamination.
  • There are signs of an allergic reaction (swelling lips, hives).
  • The subject makes a credible request for medical help.

Deployment & Tactics

Carrying & Drawing

Recommended Carry Positions: Reaction Side Front or Weapon Side Front. The canister should not interfere with the draw of the firearm.

Gripping: Use the thumb to discharge. This provides a stronger grip and allows for strikes if a suspect charges.

The 3 S's of Deployment
  1. SHOUT
  2. SHORT BURST
  3. SHUFFLE
  • Shout: Use loud, short, clear verbal commands ("Police! Get back!"). Use a code word like "Spray!" to alert other officers.
  • Short Burst: Spray the target area (eyes, nose, mouth) in a 1-2 second burst. The recommended distance is at least 1 meter (approx. 3 feet).
  • Shuffle: Move offline after spraying. This makes you a more difficult target. Once the OC takes effect, shuffle in to gain control.

Use of Force Context

Justification & Force Continuum

To use force, lawful authority must exist, and that force must be OBJECTIVELY REASONABLE.

OC is an alternative use of force option, not intended to replace empty hand control, impact weapons, or firearms. When justified, early use of OC with verbal commands can reduce the possibility of having to use a higher level of force and decrease injuries to both officers and suspects.

Comparison of Chemical Agents
Agent Type Effects Onset
CN (Tear Gas) Irritant / Lachrymatory Mild tearing, tightness of chest, mild nausea. 1.5 - 20 seconds
CS (Riot Gas) Irritant Involuntary eye closing, severe pain, uncontrollable coughing, intense panic. 1 - 60 seconds
OC (Pepper Spray) Inflammatory Involuntary eye closing, severe pain, inflammation of mucous membranes, difficulty breathing. Immediate - 5 seconds
