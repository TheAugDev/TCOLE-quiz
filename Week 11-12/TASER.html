TASER 10 & 7

A Comprehensive Study Guide

TASER 10: Nomenclature & Core Concepts

Key Features

Capacity: Capable of deploying up to 10 probes from a single magazine.

Range: Maximum range of 45 ft (13.71m), with an effective range of 40 ft (12.19m).

Targeting: Individually targeted probes. Each trigger pull deploys one probe, allowing the operator to create a custom probe spread.

De-escalation: Features a "Warning Alert" with a 1000-lumen pulsing light and audible tone. It does NOT have a warning arc.

Drive-Stun: The TASER 10 has NO drive-stun capability due to its low-voltage output design.

Components & Operation

Selector Switch: Ambidextrous switch. UP for ARMED, DOWN for SAFE. Press-and-hold down for Function Test. Press down three times for Stealth Mode.

Trigger Operation: First pull deploys one probe (no electricity). Second pull deploys a second probe and begins a 5-second cycle if a connection is made. Subsequent pulls deploy one probe each and add 5 seconds to the cycle.

Magazine: Holds 10 cartridges. Different magazines for Duty (black), Live Training (purple), HALT (blue), and Inert (red).

Battery Pack: Rechargeable. Tactical and compact options available. Haptic feedback option provides vibration alerts.

TASER 7: Key Differences

Core Feature Comparison

Capacity: The TASER 7 holds two cartridges, which can be fired in sequence.

Warning Arc: The TASER 7 features a visible electrical arc at the front of the device when the ARC switch is depressed. This is a de-escalation tool that the TASER 10 replaces with its light-and-sound "Warning Alert."

Drive-Stun Capability: The TASER 7 CAN be used for drive-stun applications for pain compliance. This is a major difference from the TASER 10, which cannot perform a drive-stun.

Operation

ARC Switch: The TASER 7 has a separate ARC switch. Depressing this switch will activate the warning arc (if no cartridges are deployed) or can be used to apply a drive-stun directly to a subject.

Trigger Operation: Pulling the trigger on a TASER 7 deploys a cartridge with two probes simultaneously and immediately begins the 5-second cycle.

General Deployment Considerations

Targeting
  • Preferred Target (Rear): Below the neck, targeting large muscle groups. This is the most preferred target area.
  • Preferred Target (Front): Lower torso (below the chest) and legs. Aim to "split the beltline" to maximize probe spread.
  • Avoid: Intentionally targeting the head, eyes, throat, chest, or genitals unless legally justified by a higher level of force.
Increased Risk Situations
  • Falls: Be aware of the subject's position. A fall from an elevated position, onto a hard surface, or near sharp objects can cause serious injury or death.
  • Flammables: TASER devices can ignite flammable liquids, fumes, gases, or other substances (e.g., gasoline, meth labs, some defense sprays).
  • Higher Risk Populations: Use with caution on pregnant women, the elderly, small children, and individuals with low body mass.
  • Running Subjects: Deploying on a running subject increases the risk of an uncontrolled fall and reduces probe accuracy.
Causes of Limited Effectiveness

Effectiveness can be limited by:

  • A missed shot or only a single probe hitting the subject.
  • Close probe spread (less than 12 inches is less effective).
  • Incomplete or broken circuit (e.g., wires break).
  • Loose or thick clothing preventing probes from making contact.
  • Subject has low nerve or muscle mass in the impact area.

Post-Incident Procedures

Immediate Actions

Control Under Power: Use the 5-second cycle as a "window of opportunity" to control and cuff the subject. This can reduce the need for additional cycles.

Monitor Subject: Continuously monitor the subject's medical condition. If they are unresponsive or show signs of medical distress, initiate EMS/CPR protocols immediately.

Probe Removal & Evidence

Probe Removal: Follow agency policy. Treat probes that have penetrated skin as contaminated biohazards. Pull probes straight out; do not twist. Inspect the probe to ensure the barb is intact.

Evidence Collection: Consistent with agency policy, consider photographing probe impact sites and any injuries. Collect spent cartridges, probes, and wires as evidence.

Reporting

Documentation is critical. Fully document all relevant circumstances, including:

  • The subject's threats, behaviors, and actions.
  • De-escalation efforts used.
  • Justification for each trigger pull and 5-second cycle.
  • The location of all probe impacts on the subject.
  • Any injuries sustained by the subject or officers.
