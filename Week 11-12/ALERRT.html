ALERRT Active Shooter Response

A Comprehensive Study Guide for Law Enforcement

Core Concepts & Guiding Principles

Priority of Life Scale & Operational Goals

Revised Priority of Life Scale (Post-Columbine):

  1. Innocent Civilians
  2. Law Enforcement / Responders
  3. Attackers

Operational Goals:

  1. Stop the Killing: Isolate, Distract, Neutralize the threat. This is the first priority.
  2. Stop the Dying: Stabilize and Transport the wounded. This is the second priority, addressed after the threat is stopped.
Lessons Learned from Major Incidents
  • Columbine High School (1999): Revealed the need for ALL responders to immediately enter and neutralize a threat, leading to the revised Priority of Life scale.
  • Virginia Tech (2007): Highlighted the importance of educating civilians on response strategies (Avoid/Run, Deny/Hide, Defend/Fight).
  • Robb Elementary (2022): Showed that a lack of incident command leads to confusion and inaction. Reinforced the principle: Active killing OR dying (critically injured victims) = PUSH until the threat is neutralized.
Hostage/Barricade vs. Active Shooter

This is a critical first determination. Ask: Is there reliable evidence of active killing or dying?

  • YES: It is an **Active Shooter** situation. Take immediate action to stop the killing.
  • NO: Treat as a **Hostage/Barricade** situation. Issue an updated LCAN, request SWAT/Negotiators, and make an immediate action plan while waiting.
5 Team Concepts and Principles
  • 1. Stay together as much as possible: Maximizes communication, threat coverage, and firepower. Avoid the urge to divide and conquer.
  • 2. Maintain 540-degree security: Continuously scan 360 degrees around, plus high and low angles.
  • 3. Communicate effectively: Use plain, short, concise speech. Confirm messages are understood.
  • 4. Threshold evaluation: Visually clear areas before entering them to gain information and tactical advantage.
  • 5. Speed of movement: Move at the appropriate speed for the circumstances (Exigent vs. Deliberate). Time equals life, but reckless speed leads to casualties.

ABCs of Cover: Your protection comes primarily from effective return fire, not hiding.

  1. Accurate return fire is your best protection.
  2. Body armor is your next level of protection.
  3. Cover is your final option.

Team Movement & Approach

Approaching the Attack Site & Cover

Approach Principles: Balance speed with security. Plan your route, consider unexpected approaches, and don't block access routes with your vehicle.

Effective Cover vs. Concealment:

  • Cover: Stops bullets (e.g., concrete walls, engine blocks, large trees).
  • Concealment: Hides you but does not stop bullets (e.g., bushes, standard car doors, drywall).
Movement Under Fire: Bounding Overwatch

A combat-proven technique for safely moving through an ambush zone.

  1. Divide into two teams: an **Overwatch Team** and a **Bounding Team**.
  2. The **Overwatch Team** provides cover fire and scans for threats.
  3. The **Bounding Team** sprints from one piece of cover to the next (a "bound").
  4. Bounds should be short (2-3 seconds).
  5. Once the Bounding Team is set, they become the Overwatch Team, and the process repeats.
Hallway Movement Formations

Formations allow for 540-degree security while moving.

  • Two-Person Tether: Point officer moves forward, rear guard covers the back while maintaining light physical contact.
  • Three-Person Tether: Point and a wingman cover front/sides, rear guard covers back.
  • Four-Person Diamond: Point, left cover, right cover, and rear guard. Efficient and flexible for various environments.
Navigating Stairways

Stairways are extremely dangerous 540° threat environments. The key is to move through them quickly and quietly without stopping.

  • Maintain your hallway formation (Tether or Diamond) while ascending.
  • Adjust positions slightly to better cover high and low angles.
  • Do not stop on the stairway to plan or discuss. Get off the stairs before the adversary knows you are there.
  • If a threat is encountered, engage with overwhelming fire and get off the stairway as quickly as possible.
Breaching & Entering Locked Buildings

Gain access quickly and safely, using only items you carry. Getting inside is what matters, not the specific technique.

  • Check Doors First: Is it actually locked? Check nearby doors.
  • Use Keys / KnoxBox: The quickest and quietest method if a key is immediately available.
  • Breaking Windows: A good option. Use a tool, then clear all glass shards from the frame with a baton or rifle muzzle before entering.
  • Kicking/Ramming: Only effective on inward-opening wooden doors. Steel commercial doors will not break and may cause injury.
  • Firearm Breaching: A last resort for trained personnel only. Can create more hazards than it solves if done improperly.

Room Entry Tactics

Universal Firearm Safety Rules

These rules are non-negotiable and paramount to preventing accidents.

  1. Treat all firearms as if they are loaded.
  2. Keep your finger off the trigger unless you intend to press it.
  3. Never point a firearm at anyone unless you are justified.
  4. Be sure of your target's foreground, backstop, and beyond.
Firearm Positions

Maintaining muzzle discipline and readiness is key.

  • High Combat-Ready: Arms extended, pistol pointed at a potential threat, sights just low enough to see hands/waist. Used when assessing a threat.
  • Low Combat-Ready: Arms relaxed, pistol 6-8 inches from sternum with muzzle level. Used when searching or moving with no friendlies in front.
  • SUL Position: Muzzle-down position used whenever other responders are in front of you to prevent flagging.
Triangle of Success & Core Principles

A successful room entry is built on three elements:

  • Surprise: The base of the triangle. Enter without broadcasting your intent.
  • Speed: Move quickly through the threshold to your point of domination.
  • Decisive Action: Controlled aggression and clear communication to achieve the objective.

Core Principles on Entry:

  • Go in the opposite direction of the person in front of you.
  • Move to your point of domination (POD) and STOP to avoid crossfire.
  • Scan your sector of fire and address threats/unknowns with commands.
  • Call out danger areas you cannot clear (e.g., open closets, other doors).
  • Clearly coordinate with the team before moving from your POD.
Corner-Fed vs. Center-Fed Rooms

Corner-Fed Room: A room where there is not enough space for a person to fit between the doorway and the nearest side wall. Most common type.

Center-Fed Room: A room with enough space for a person to stand on both sides of the doorway. Presents a greater challenge as there are "unknown" walls in two directions.

General Entry Flow (Strong Wall Technique):

  1. **#1 Officer:** Enters and moves toward a deep corner to clear the hard corner.
  2. **#2 Officer:** Enters in the opposite direction of #1.
  3. **#3 & #4 Officers:** Follow the pattern (opposite of the person before) and fill in the gaps, establishing a line and covering their sectors.
Post-Entry & Exiting

Post-Entry Priorities ("Closing Down the Room"):

  1. Address live bodies with commands.
  2. Identify and communicate danger areas.
  3. Form a plan to clear danger areas before moving from POD.
  4. Keep weapons on downed bodies until they are secured.

Exiting a Room: After clearing a room, you must tactically reclaim the hallway. Use the **"Israeli Lean"** (a coordinated, simultaneous lean out from both sides of the doorway) to regain visual control of the hallway with minimal exposure.

Medical & Casualty Care

Priorities & Guidelines

Good medicine can be poor tactics. Threat mitigation (Stop the Killing) ALWAYS takes priority over medical care. Do not stop to provide aid if the active threat is not neutralized.

Initial care is limited to rapid interventions to stabilize for evacuation. Get yourself and the casualty "off the X."

Hemorrhage Control

Massive extremity bleeding is a leading cause of preventable death. A tourniquet is the primary tool.

  • "Go High or Die": Apply the tourniquet as high up on the injured limb as possible, over clothing.
  • Tighten the windlass until the bleeding stops completely.
  • A properly applied tourniquet should be applied in under 20 seconds.
  • Do not remove or loosen a tourniquet once applied.
Airway Management & Assessment

Recovery Position: Used for unconscious or semi-conscious casualties to keep their airway open and allow fluids to drain. Roll them onto their side with their head supported.

Rapid Casualty Assessment (Post-Threat):

  1. Visually scan for massive bleeding and apply tourniquets.
  2. Perform a "Blood Sweep" (raking motion over the body) to find hidden wounds.
  3. Address airway by placing unconscious casualties in the recovery position.
  4. Triage to determine evacuation priority (most severe injuries first).
Casualty Evacuation

LE Rescue Model: The focus of ALERRT Level 1. Law enforcement officers provide initial care and transport casualties out of the warm zone to a CCP or cold zone for EMS.

Carry Techniques: Use the simplest method to move a casualty.

  • One-Person Drag: Grab wrist/clothing and pull to cover.
  • One-Person Carry: Lift from behind, under armpits, grabbing opposite wrists.
  • Two-Person Assist: For conscious casualties. Drape arms over responders' shoulders.
  • Two-Person Carry: For unconscious casualties. One lifts upper body, one lifts legs.

Response Checklist (S.I.M. Acronym)

Use this checklist of priorities immediately after all known threats have been neutralized to transition from "Stop the Killing" to "Stop the Dying."

S - Security

Establish a secure area for treating the wounded.

  1. Cover the Attacker(s): Assign an officer to focus on each downed attacker.
  2. Consolidate & Vet Unknowns: Move all uninjured individuals to one area, hands visible, to free up responders.
  3. Handcuff the Attacker: Use a contact/cover officer approach.
  4. Cover the Entrance: Assign an officer to the doorway to mark the room, communicate externally, and ensure a safe linkup with follow-on teams.
  5. Establish CCP: Designate a Casualty Collection Point and plan a secure corridor for evacuation.
I - Incident Command

Address critical command tasks while others perform medical aid. The doorway officer is often best positioned for this.

  1. Update LCAN: Provide dispatch with the new situation status.
    • "Command, Unit 107. Threat is down. We are in the library with multiple victims. Suspect is in custody."
  2. Request Resources: Clearly state needs (e.g., "Need EMS to a staging area at the north parking lot," "Need additional teams for evacuation").
  3. Manage Incoming Responders: Direct follow-on teams to assist with security, medical, or creating the secure evacuation corridor. Prevent self-deployment.
M - Medical

Begin medical interventions simultaneously with IC tasks once initial security is set.

  1. Triage: Quickly identify casualties with the most severe, life-threatening injuries (e.g., massive bleeding, airway issues). They are the priority for evacuation.
  2. Treat: Perform rapid interventions.
    • Apply tourniquets for massive extremity bleeding.
    • Perform blood sweeps to find other major wounds.
    • Place unconscious casualties in the recovery position to protect their airway.
  3. Transport: Begin moving the most critical casualties through the secure corridor to the extraction point/CCP for handoff to EMS.

Special Threats & Incident Command

Responding to IEDs

Four Rules for Stationary IEDs:

  1. Don't touch the bomb or bomber. Assume they are live and can be triggered remotely or by movement.
  2. Create distance and angles. Blast waves and shrapnel are deadliest in a straight line. Get behind a corner.
  3. Mark and communicate the IED's location to Command and other teams.
  4. Find an alternate route. Do not attempt to bypass an IED unless there is absolutely no other option and active killing is occurring.

For hand-deployed devices, yell "BOMB COVER" (move back) or "BOMB GO" (move forward past it) and get into a secure room before detonation.

Incident Command System (ICS)

The "Blue Tsunami": The uncontrolled wave of responding officers that can swamp a scene and hinder response if not managed.

ICS is the tool to manage this chaos. It must be established early.

First Officer on Scene Responsibilities:

  1. Assume command via radio ("Unit 107 will have COMMAND").
  2. Give an **LCAN** report:
    • Location: "Van Buren Elementary School."
    • Conditions: "Children running out, teacher reports shooter in library."
    • Actions: "I am making entry through the front door."
    • Needs: "I need follow-on officers to link up with me."
  3. Move to stop the killing.

Command should be transferred to an exterior, less-involved officer as soon as feasible to manage the overall scene.

Mental Preparedness & Stress Effects

The Brain Under Stress: X-System vs. C-System

Understanding your brain's reaction to stress is key to performance.

  • C-System (RefleCtive): Your rational, human brain. It's flexible, allows for planning and learning, but is SLOW and takes effort. It shuts down under high stress.
  • X-System (RefleXive): Your primitive, reptile brain. It's FAST and effortless, responding automatically with pre-programmed actions (Fight, Flight, Freeze). It takes over in a crisis.

Goal of Training: To program the correct tactical skills into the X-System so they can be performed automatically and correctly under stress when the C-System is offline.

Common Sensory Side Effects of Stress

These are normal X-System responses designed to focus you on a threat.

  • Tunnel Vision: Your field of focus narrows to only the immediate threat. You must consciously force yourself to scan.
  • Auditory Exclusion: You may stop hearing what is happening around you, including commands from teammates.
  • Time Dilation: Events may seem to happen in slow motion or speed up.
  • Reduced Motor Skills: Fine motor skills (like intricate reloading) degrade. Gross motor skills (like running and pointing) are enhanced.
Managing Stress & The OODA Loop

Keeping the C-System Online:

  • Combat Breathing: A controlled breathing pattern (e.g., in for 3, hold for 2, out for 3) can lower heart rate and help regain composure.
  • Habituation: Realistic training makes real events less stressful because your brain has been exposed to similar stimuli.
  • Take Action: Acting, even in a small way, prevents the feeling of helplessness and reduces stress. Freezing is always the wrong response.

The OODA Loop (Observe, Orient, Decide, Act): In a gunfight, you must cycle through this decision-making process faster than your opponent. Training skills into your X-System makes your loop faster because you don't have to "decide" anymore—you just act.
