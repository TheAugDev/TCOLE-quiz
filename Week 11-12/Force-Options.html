Force Options

A Comprehensive Study Guide

Definitions & Core Concepts

Key Definitions

Force: Strength or energy brought to bear; compulsion or constraint exerted upon a person or thing. An aggressive act necessary to accomplish an objective.

Reasonable Force: The amount of lawful physical coercion sufficient to achieve a legitimate law enforcement objective and is objectively reasonable under the facts, circumstances, and alternatives confronting an officer at the time.

Deadly Force (PC 9.01): Force that is intended or known by the actor to cause, or in the manner of its use or intended use is capable of causing, death or serious bodily injury.

The Concept of Control

Control is the degree of influence an officer must exert over a violator to take them safely into custody. It is a "two-way street": an officer must be in complete self-control to be able to control a violator. The objective is to elicit cooperation.

Emotional responses often result from uncertainty, which can lead to compensating behaviors like hesitation, verbal abuse, or unnecessary force.

Legal Justifications for Force (Penal Code Chapter 9)

General Justifications
  • Justification as a Defense (PC 9.02): It is a defense to prosecution that the conduct in question is justified under this chapter.
  • Confinement as Justifiable Force (PC 9.03): Confinement is justified when force is justified, if the actor takes reasonable measures to terminate the confinement as soon as he knows he safely can.
  • Threats as Justifiable Force (PC 9.04): The threat of force is justified when the use of force is justified. A threat to cause death or SBI by producing a weapon is justified when the use of deadly force would be justified.
  • Public Duty (PC 9.21): Conduct is justified if the actor reasonably believes it is required or authorized by law, by the judgment or order of a competent court, or in the execution of legal process.
  • Necessity (PC 9.22): Conduct is justified if the actor reasonably believes it is immediately necessary to avoid imminent harm.
Protection of Persons
  • Self-Defense (PC 9.31): A person is justified in using force when they reasonably believe it is immediately necessary to protect against another's use of unlawful force.
  • Deadly Force in Defense of Person (PC 9.32): Justified to protect against another's use of deadly force or to prevent imminent commission of aggravated kidnapping, murder, sexual assault, or robbery.
  • Defense of Third Person (PC 9.33): Justified in using force to protect a third person if the actor reasonably believes their intervention is immediately necessary.
Law Enforcement Specific Justifications

Arrest and Search (PC 9.51):

  • A peace officer is justified in using force when they reasonably believe it is immediately necessary to make an arrest or search, or to prevent escape after arrest.
  • Deadly force is justified only when the officer reasonably believes it is immediately necessary to make an arrest or prevent escape AND there is a substantial risk that the person to be arrested will cause death or SBI to another if the arrest is delayed, OR the person is committing or attempting to commit certain violent felonies.

Preventing Escape from Custody (PC 9.52): The use of force to prevent the escape of an arrested person is justified when the force could have been used to effect the arrest.

Landmark Use of Force Cases

Graham v. Connor, 490 U.S. 386 (1989)

This case established the "objective reasonableness" standard for all claims of excessive force under the Fourth Amendment.

The reasonableness of a particular use of force must be judged from the perspective of a reasonable officer on the scene, rather than with the 20/20 vision of hindsight. The calculus must allow for the fact that officers are often forced to make split-second judgments in circumstances that are tense, uncertain, and rapidly evolving.

The Graham Factors:

  • The severity of the crime at issue.
  • Whether the suspect poses an immediate threat to the safety of the officers or others.
  • Whether the suspect is actively resisting arrest or attempting to evade arrest by flight.
Tennessee v. Garner, 471 U.S. 1 (1985)

This case addressed the use of deadly force to prevent the escape of a fleeing felon. The Supreme Court held that it is unconstitutional to use deadly force to stop a fleeing felon unless the suspect poses a significant threat of death or serious physical injury to the officer or others.

Example: An officer cannot shoot an unarmed burglary suspect who is climbing a fence to escape if the suspect poses no immediate threat.

Qualified Immunity Cases

Qualified Immunity shields government officials from liability for civil damages if their conduct does not violate clearly established statutory or constitutional rights of which a reasonable officer would have known.

  • Milstead v. Kibler: Police officers performing a discretionary function enjoy an immunity that shields them from liability unless their conduct violates a federal right, and the right was clearly established at the time.
  • Okonkwo v. Fernandez: A defendant official must affirmatively plead the defense of qualified immunity.

This model illustrates the range of force options available to an officer. The appropriate level of force depends on the totality of the circumstances, particularly the subject's actions.

  • Professional Presence: A visible display of authority.
  • Verbal Commands: Clear, concise directions.
  • Weaponless Strategies: Takedowns, come-alongs, pressure points.
  • Weapon Strategies: Chemical agents (OC spray), electronic devices (TASER), impact weapons (baton).
  • Deadly Force: Force likely to cause death or serious bodily injury.

Officer Conduct & Consequences

Consequences of Excessive Force

Improper or excessive use of force can lead to:

  • Federal Criminal Prosecution: Under federal civil rights laws like Title 18, U.S.C. Sections 241 (Conspiracy Against Rights) and 242 (Deprivation of Rights Under Color of Law).
  • Civil Lawsuits: Officers can be sued in federal court under 42 U.S.C. ยง 1983 for civil rights violations, and in state court for torts.
  • Departmental Discipline: Including suspension, demotion, or termination.
  • Loss of Public Trust: Damaging the relationship between the police and the community.
Officer Created & State Created Danger

Officer Created Danger: Occurs when an officer's reckless or negligent actions create a dangerous situation where one did not previously exist, leading to a justifiable but avoidable use of force.

State Created Danger: A basis for a lawsuit where the state's conduct puts a person at a substantial risk of harm, and the state consciously disregards that risk.

Example (State Created Danger): An officer arrests a driver and leaves the passenger stranded in a high-crime area at 2:30 AM, where she is later assaulted. The officer's actions created the danger. (Wood v. Ostrander)

Duty to Intervene & Render Aid

Recent Texas law (HB 3712) and case law establish clear duties for officers:

  • Duty to Intervene: An officer has a duty to intervene to stop another officer from using excessive force.
  • Duty to Report Excessive Force: Officers are required to report any instances of excessive force by another officer.
  • Duty to Render Aid: Officers have a duty to provide or summon medical aid for an injured person, including a suspect injured during an arrest.
