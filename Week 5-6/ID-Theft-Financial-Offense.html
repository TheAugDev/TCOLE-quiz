ID Crime Fundamentals

Identity Crime
The theft or misuse of personal or financial identifiers to gain something of value or facilitate other criminal activity.
Identity Theft
A type of identity crime where someone uses your personal information (name, SSN, etc.) without permission to commit fraud.
How Identity Crimes Occur
  • Stolen wallet, purse, or briefcase
  • Mail theft or fraudulent change of address
  • "Dumpster Diving" for discarded documents
  • Business record theft (internal access or hacking)
  • Posing as a landlord or employer to get a credit report
  • Sharing too much personal info online
  • Phishing, spoofing, and other email/social media scams
  • "Shoulder surfing" or spy cameras at ATMs
  • Credit/debit card skimmers
Crimes Associated with Identity Theft

Identity crime is often a facilitator for other, more serious offenses.

Crimes Leading to ID Theft

  • Robbery / Burglary
  • Mail Theft
  • Theft of Business/Medical Records
  • Computer Intrusion

Facilitating Crimes

  • Narcotics / Weapons Trafficking
  • Money Laundering / Mortgage Fraud
  • Homicide / Terrorism
  • Organized Crime

Financial Crimes (Penal Code Chapters 31 & 32)

Fraudulent Use or Possession of Identifying Information (PC 32.51)

A person commits an offense if, with intent to harm or defraud another, they obtain, possess, transfer, or use the identifying information of another person without consent.

The number of items of identifying information determines the offense level:

  • Less than 5 items: State Jail Felony
  • 5 to 9 items: 3rd Degree Felony
  • 10 to 49 items: 2nd Degree Felony
  • 50 or more items: 1st Degree Felony

Enhancement: The offense is increased to the next higher category if the victim is an elderly individual.

Check & Payment Card Offenses
Stealing or Receiving Stolen Check (PC 32.24): Stealing an unsigned check or receiving one knowing it's stolen. A Class A Misdemeanor.
Issuance of Bad Check (PC 32.41): Passing a check knowing there are insufficient funds. A Class C Misdemeanor. Knowledge is presumed if payment is refused and the issuer fails to pay within 10 days of receiving notice.
Credit Card or Debit Card Abuse (PC 32.31): A broad offense covering fraudulent use, theft, or possession of credit/debit cards. A State Jail Felony, enhanced to a 3rd Degree Felony if the victim is elderly.
Fraudulent Use/Possession of Credit/Debit Card Info (PC 32.315): A newer offense targeting skimmers. Possessing counterfeit cards or card info without consent. Punishment ladder is identical to PC 32.51 (SJF for <5 items, up to 1st Degree for 50+).
Other Fraud Offenses

False Statement to Obtain Property or Credit (PC 32.32): Intentionally or knowingly making a materially false or misleading written statement to get property or credit (including a mortgage loan). The punishment follows the standard theft ladder based on the value of the property/credit obtained.

Money Laundering & Exploitation

Money Laundering (PC 34.02)

A person commits an offense if they knowingly acquire, possess, transfer, transport, or conduct a transaction with the proceeds of criminal activity.

Punishment Ladder:

  • $2,500 to < $30,000: State Jail Felony
  • $30,000 to < $150,000: 3rd Degree Felony
  • $150,000 to < $300,000: 2nd Degree Felony
  • $300,000 or more: 1st Degree Felony
Exploitation & Financial Abuse
Exploitation of Child, Elderly, or Disabled Individual (PC 32.53): The illegal or improper use of a person or their resources for monetary or personal benefit. A 3rd Degree Felony.
Financial Abuse of Elderly Individual (PC 32.55): Knowingly engaging in the wrongful taking, appropriation, or use of an elderly individual's money or property. This offense has its own unique value ladder, starting at a Class B Misdemeanor (under $100) and going up to a 1st Degree Felony ($150,000 or more).

Prevention & Investigation

Security Alerts & Freezes

Security Alert

A notice on a consumer file that alerts creditors that your identity may have been compromised. Must be placed within 24 hours of request and remains for at least 45 days.

Security Freeze

A notice that prohibits a credit reporting agency from releasing your credit report without your express authorization (usually via a PIN). Must be placed within 5 business days of a written request.

Steps for Victims of ID Crime
  • File a Police Report: This is crucial for credit bureaus and financial institutions to block fraudulent activity.
  • File a Report with the FTC: Use IdentityTheft.gov to get a personalized recovery plan.
  • Contact Credit Bureaus: Place a fraud alert or security freeze with Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion.
  • Notify Financial Institutions: Contact banks and credit card companies to close fraudulent accounts and dispute charges.
  • Notify Other Agencies: Contact the Social Security Administration, U.S. Passport Agency, and DPS as needed.
Information for an ID Crime Report

A good report is vital for investigation and for the victim's recovery process.

  • Confirm the offense type (e.g., ID Theft vs. Credit Card Abuse).
  • Determine jurisdiction (can be where the offense occurred OR where the victim resides).
  • Document the date of occurrence, all fraudulent account numbers, and the specific personal identifiers used.
  • Ask the victim to gather all relevant documentation (statements, letters, credit reports).
  • Have the victim fill out an agency-specific identity crime incident form if available.
