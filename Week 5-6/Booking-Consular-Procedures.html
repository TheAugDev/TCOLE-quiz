Booking, Searching, & Related Procedures

I. Importance of a Good Search
  • To ensure the safety of officers, staff, and other inmates.
  • To locate and seize contraband or evidence.
  • To maintain the security of the facility.
  • To eliminate suicide risks and prevent in-custody deaths (requires suicide screening).
  • To properly safeguard the prisoner's personal property.
II. Maintaining Security in Correctional Facilities (PC 9.53)

An officer/employee is justified in using force when they reasonably believe it is necessary to: maintain facility security, ensure the safety of others, or ensure their own safety.

III. Use of Laser Pointers (PC 42.13)

Knowingly directing a laser pointer at a uniformed safety officer is an offense. Classifications:

  • Class C Misdemeanor: General offense.
  • 3rd Degree Felony: If it causes bodily injury.
  • 1st Degree Felony: If it causes serious bodily injury (SBI).
IV. Death in Custody (CCP 49.18)

Initial Report: If a person dies in a penal institution, the sheriff must inform the Precinct JP as soon as practicable.

Investigation: If a person dies in peace officer custody or a correctional facility, the agency director must investigate and file a written report on the cause of death with the Attorney General within 30 days.

V. Jail Searching Procedures

Key Principles:

  • Perform proper searches on ALL prisoners.
  • Let your hands search; eyes should observe for movement. NO PATTING.
  • Except in an emergency, searches must be by an officer of the same sex and out of view of the opposite sex.
  • Be systematic: top to bottom. Inspect all property removed (shoes, belt, etc.).

Strip Searches (TCJS 265.2(b)): Only performed when personnel reasonably believe it's necessary to find weapons/contraband that pose a threat. Must be done by same-sex staff in a dignified manner.

Florence v. Board (2012): The Supreme Court ruled that jails can conduct strip searches on all arrestees, even those with minor offenses, without reasonable suspicion, due to significant security interests.

VI. Property Inventory (32.46)

Verbally describe property in the prisoner's presence on the correct form. Describe items objectively (e.g., "yellow in color metal ring" not "gold ring"). Detail clothing by type, color, size, labels, etc. Have the prisoner sign the inventory.

VII & VIII. Recording Data & Info Retrieval

Booking Data: Record comprehensive personal data (name, DOB, address, SSN, etc.), vehicle data, and arrest data (charge, officers, time, etc.).

TCIC/NCIC System: Check for warrants and stolen property. This database includes files for articles, guns, vehicles, wanted persons, criminal history, and more.

IX & X. Injury & Harassment Procedures

Injury: If a prisoner is injured, call for medical assistance, photograph the prisoner and officer, and write a proper report.

Communicable Diseases (CCP 18.22): A court can order testing if an arrestee causes bodily fluid contact with an officer.

Harassment by Persons in Facilities (PC 22.11): An inmate who, with intent to assault/harass/alarm, causes an officer to contact bodily fluids commits a 3rd Degree Felony.

Consular Notification & Diplomatic Immunity

XII. Consular Notification (VCCR)

Purpose: To ensure a foreign national is not placed in a situation where they cannot receive assistance from their own government.

Basic Rule: A foreign detainee must be informed of their right to have their consulate notified. Their decision must be documented.

The 4 Steps for a Foreign National Arrest
  1. Ask the detainee if they are a U.S. citizen. If not, determine their country of citizenship.
  2. Check the list of mandatory notification countries. If their country is on the list, you MUST notify their consulate without delay (max 72 hours), regardless of the detainee's wishes.
  3. If NOT a mandatory country, ask if they wish for their consulate to be notified.
  4. Document everything: The detainee's response, and if notification is made, record the date, time, method, and person notified.
XIII. Diplomatic Immunity

Diplomatic agents and their families may have immunity from criminal prosecution. Immunity must be verified through the Office of Foreign Missions (OFM).

The C.A.N. Principle

  • CALL the Department of State to verify immunity status.
  • ASSIST by fully documenting the incident and issuing any relevant citations.
  • NOTIFY the Department of State by sending them copies of all reports and citations.

XIV-XVII. Post-Arrest Duties & Timelines

Taking Before Magistrate (CCP 15.17)

Must be done without unnecessary delay, but no later than 48 hours after arrest. The magistrate must inform the person of charges, right to counsel, right to remain silent, right to terminate interview, and right to an examining trial.

Release on Bond if No PC (CCP 17.033)
  • Misdemeanor: Release on bond (max $5,000) not later than 24 hours if no PC determination.
  • Felony: Release on bond (max $10,000) not later than 48 hours if no PC determination.
  • If unable to obtain surety, must be released on personal bond.
Commitment & Bail (CCP 16.20, 17.20-22)

Commitment: A magistrate's written order directing a sheriff to receive and place a person in jail.

Bail: In misdemeanors, a sheriff/peace officer/jailer MAY take bail. In felonies, they may only take bail in the amount set by the court (unless court is not in session).

Out-of-County Warrants (CCP 15.19-21)

If a person is arrested on an out-of-county warrant and does not give bail, the magistrate notifies the sheriff of the county where the offense is alleged.

If the proper agency does not demand and take charge of the arrested person before the 11th day after commitment, the magistrate shall release the person on a personal bond.

Glossary of Key Terms

Booking Process
The administrative procedure of officially registering an arrest, which includes recording information, fingerprinting, photographing, and inventorying property.
CCP (Code of Criminal Procedure)
A body of law that outlines the procedures for criminal prosecution in Texas.
Commitment
A formal order signed by a magistrate directing a sheriff to receive and place a person into jail.
Consular Notification
Informing a foreign detainee of their right to have their consulate notified, or the mandatory notification of certain consulates.
Diplomatic Immunity
A privilege providing diplomatic personnel with protection from arrest and prosecution.
Florence v. Board (2012)
A U.S. Supreme Court case ruling that strip searches of arrestees entering a jail, even for minor offenses, do not require reasonable suspicion.
Harassment by Persons in Certain Facilities (PC 22.11)
An offense where a confined person causes another to contact bodily fluids with intent to assault, harass, or alarm.
Mandatory Notification Countries
Countries with treaties requiring their consulates to be notified of a national's arrest, regardless of the detaineeâ€™s wishes.
PC (Penal Code)
The body of law that defines criminal offenses and their corresponding punishments in Texas.
Personal Bond (PR Bond)
A bond where the defendant promises to appear in court and pays no money upfront.
TCIC/NCIC
Texas/National Crime Information Center; computerized indexes of criminal justice information.
Unnecessary Delay
A legal term defined by specific time limits (e.g., 48 hours in Texas) for bringing an arrestee before a magistrate.
VCCR (Vienna Convention on Consular Relations)
An international treaty defining the framework for consular relations, including notification of arrested nationals.
