Types of Property Offenders

Professional Thief

  • Motive: Profit. Operates to minimize chances of observation.
  • Planning: Requires significant planning, direction, and skill.
  • Methods: Often works with criminal receivers (fences) to dispose of property. Rarely armed.
  • Crimes: Burglary, white-collar crimes, vehicle theft, agricultural crimes, arson.
  • Weakness: Can be traced when stolen property is recovered.

Semi-professional (Unskilled) Thief

  • Motive: Still profit, but often to get money for drugs.
  • Planning: Less involved in planning; acts on opportunity (e.g., cruising robber).
  • Behavior: More likely to resort to violence, especially when cornered.
  • Rationality: Less rational than their professional counterparts.
  • Danger: Presents a special danger when interrupted.

Burglar

  • Motivation: Same as a professional thief (profit).
  • Skills: Ability to gain entry by stealth or defeating locks/alarms.
  • Operations: Can plan, direct, and execute an operation.
  • Business Sense: Can distinguish between valuable and worthless items.
  • Network: Has connections to dispose of the stolen property.
  • Clues: The type of burglary (residential, commercial, safe) can give clues to identity.

Street Thief

  • Behavior: Often carry weapons and take reckless chances to avoid apprehension.
  • Motive: Usually stealing to trade for narcotics or alcohol.
  • Specialty: Considered the most dangerous auto thieves.
  • Encounter Rate: Patrol officers encounter street thieves more often than professionals.
  • Detection: Good interview techniques and attention to detail are key to detecting them.

Chapter 28: Foundational Definitions

Key Terms (Sec. 28.01)

  • Habitation: A structure/vehicle adapted for overnight accommodation.
  • Building: An enclosed structure for habitation, trade, manufacture, etc.
  • Vehicle: Any device for transporting people or property.
  • Open-space land: Real property undeveloped for human habitation.

Actor's Interest in Property (Sec. 28.05)

It is NOT a defense that the actor has an interest in the property if another person also has an interest that the actor is not entitled to infringe upon.

Amount of Pecuniary Loss (Sec. 28.06)

  • If property is destroyed, the loss is its fair market value or replacement cost.
  • If property is damaged, the loss is the cost of repairing or restoring it.
  • For documents without a market value (like a check), the loss is the amount due and collectible.
  • If loss cannot be ascertained by other means, it is deemed to be between $750 and $2,500 (Class A Misdemeanor).
Criminal Mischief (Sec. 28.03)

An offense if, without the owner's effective consent, a person intentionally or knowingly damages, destroys, tampers with, or makes markings on tangible property.

Criminal Mischief Offense Level Chart

Offense Level Value / Pecuniary Loss Special Item or Situation
Class C Misdemeanor Less than $100 Or causes substantial inconvenience to others.
Class B Misdemeanor $100 to less than $750 -
Class A Misdemeanor $750 to less than $2,500 OR impairs/interrupts a public water supply.
State Jail Felony $2,500 to less than $30,000 OR damage is to a habitation by firearm/explosive (< $2,500), OR a fence for livestock/game (< $2,500), OR impairs public services (< $30,000), OR involves catalytic converter removal (< $30,000).
3rd Degree Felony $30,000 to less than $150,000 OR causes death of cattle/bison/horse, OR impairs access to an ATM, OR impairs public power supply (< $150,000).
2nd Degree Felony $150,000 to less than $300,000 -
1st Degree Felony $300,000 or more OR causes damage to livestock by introducing a disease like bovine spongiform encephalopathy (mad cow disease).

Additional Rules:

  • Presumption: A person receiving the benefit from a public utility (water, gas, power) is presumed to have tampered with the meter if it has been diverted or prevented from registering correctly.
  • Aggregation: If multiple items are damaged in one scheme or continuing course of conduct, the amounts can be aggregated to determine the grade of the offense.
Graffiti (Sec. 28.08)

An offense if, without the owner's effective consent, a person intentionally or knowingly makes markings on tangible property with paint, an indelible marker, or an etching/engraving device.

Offense Levels:

  • Follows the standard value ladder (from Class C Misdemeanor to 1st Degree Felony).
  • State Jail Felony (Enhanced): If the damage is between $750 - $30,000 AND the marking is made on a school, place of worship, public monument, human burial site, or certain community centers.
Arson (Sec. 28.02)

An offense if a person starts a fire or causes an explosion with intent to destroy or damage property under specific circumstances.

Arson (a) - 2nd Degree Felony

Starting a fire/explosion with intent to damage a building, habitation, or vehicle, knowing it's in a city, insured, on another's property, etc., OR being reckless about endangering life/property.

Enhanced to 1st Degree Felony if it causes BI/death or the target is a habitation or place of worship.

Arson (a-1) - State Jail Felony

Recklessly starting a fire/explosion while manufacturing a controlled substance, which then damages a building, habitation, or vehicle.

Enhanced to 3rd Degree Felony if it causes BI/death.

Arson (a-2) - State Jail Felony

Intentionally starting a fire/explosion that recklessly damages another's building OR recklessly causes BI/death to another person.

Other Property Offenses
Reckless Damage or Destruction (Sec. 28.04): Recklessly damaging/destroying property without owner's consent. Class C Misdemeanor.
Interference with Railroad Property (Sec. 28.07):
  • Throwing an object at a train: Class B Misdemeanor (or 3rd Degree Felony if it causes BI).
  • Entering/remaining on railroad property: Class C Misdemeanor.
  • Tampering, obstructing, or causing derailment: Follows the theft ladder based on pecuniary loss.

Chapter 42: Cruelty to Animals

Cruelty to Livestock Animals (PC 42.09)

It is an offense to intentionally or knowingly commit various acts of cruelty against livestock animals (cattle, horses, sheep, goats, etc.).

State Jail Felony Offenses

  • Tortures an animal
  • Poisons an animal
  • Causes one animal to fight another
  • Uses a live animal as a lure
  • Trips a horse

Enhanced to 3rd Degree Felony with 2 prior convictions.

Class A Misdemeanor Offenses

  • Fails to provide necessary food, water, or care
  • Abandons an animal
  • Transports an animal in a cruel manner
  • Seriously overworks an animal

Enhanced to State Jail Felony with 2 prior convictions.

Cruelty to Non-Livestock Animals (PC 42.092)

It is an offense to intentionally, knowingly, or recklessly commit cruelty against non-livestock animals (e.g., domesticated pets).

Felony Offenses

  • 3rd Degree Felony: Tortures, cruelly kills, poisons, or causes SBI to an animal. (Enhanced to 2nd Degree with prior).
  • State Jail Felony: Causes one animal to fight another (not a dog); uses a live animal as a lure. (Enhanced to 3rd Degree with prior).

Class A Misdemeanor Offenses

  • Fails to provide food, water, care, or shelter
  • Abandons an animal
  • Transports in a cruel manner
  • Causes bodily injury
  • Seriously overworks an animal

Enhanced to State Jail Felony with 2 prior convictions.

Specialized Animal Offenses
Attack on Assistance Animal (42.091): Ranges from Class A Misdemeanor (attacks) to State Jail Felony (injures) to 3rd Degree Felony (kills).
Interference with Police Service Animal (38.151): Ranges from Class C Misdemeanor (taunts) to 2nd Degree Felony (kills or permanently injures).
Dog Fighting (42.10): Causing a dog to fight is a State Jail Felony. Attending as a spectator is a Class A Misdemeanor.
Bestiality (21.09): A State Jail Felony, enhanced to a 2nd Degree Felony if it results in SBI/death to the animal or is done in the presence of a child.

Chapter 30: Criminal Trespass

Key Definitions for Trespass
  • Habitation: A structure or vehicle adapted for the overnight accommodation of persons.
  • Building: An enclosed structure intended for use as a habitation or for trade, manufacture, ornament, or use.
  • Entry (for Criminal Trespass): The intrusion of the entire body.
  • Notice: Can be given by oral/written communication, fencing, signs, visible crops, or specific purple paint marks.
Criminal Trespass (Sec. 30.05)

A person commits an offense if they enter or remain on another's property without effective consent AND they had notice entry was forbidden, OR received notice to depart and failed to do so.

Punishment Levels:

  • Class C Misdemeanor: On agricultural land within 100 ft of the boundary.
  • Class B Misdemeanor: The default offense level.
  • Class A Misdemeanor: If in a habitation, shelter center, Superfund site, critical infrastructure facility, or while carrying a deadly weapon.
  • 3rd Degree Felony: If committed in the course of a Smuggling of Persons offense.

Firearms Trespass:

  • A property owner can prohibit firearms by posting a specific sign (PC 30.05).
  • Entering with a firearm where this is the only prohibition is a Class C Misdemeanor.
  • It becomes a Class A Misdemeanor if the actor is given personal/oral notice and then fails to depart.
Trespass by License Holder (30.06 & 30.07)

A licensed handgun carrier commits an offense if they carry onto property where they have received notice that it's forbidden. The notice must be specific and properly posted.

30.06 (Concealed Carry)

"PURSUANT TO SECTION 30.06, PENAL CODE... A PERSON LICENSED UNDER SUBCHAPTER H, CHAPTER 411, GOVERNMENT CODE... MAY NOT ENTER THIS PROPERTY WITH A CONCEALED HANDGUN"

30.07 (Open Carry)

"PURSUANT TO SECTION 30.07, PENAL CODE... A PERSON LICENSED UNDER SUBCHAPTER H, CHAPTER 411, GOVERNMENT CODE... MAY NOT ENTER THIS PROPERTY WITH A HANDGUN THAT IS CARRIED OPENLY"

Sign Requirements: Must be in English and Spanish, in contrasting colors with block letters at least 1 inch high, and displayed conspicuously.

Punishment: Default is a Class C Misdemeanor. Becomes a Class A if the person is given oral notice and fails to depart.
