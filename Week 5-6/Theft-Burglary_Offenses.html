Preventing Consequences of Theft (CCP 18.16)

This crucial article of the Code of Criminal Procedure allows any person to seize stolen property and the person suspected of theft under specific circumstances.

Rule: Any person has a right to prevent the consequences of theft by seizing any personal property that has been stolen and bringing it, along with the person suspected of committing the theft, before a magistrate for examination, or delivering them to a peace officer for that purpose.

Requirement: To justify the seizure, there must be reasonable grounds to believe the property is stolen, and the seizure must be openly made and followed by immediate delivery to authorities.

Theft Offenses (Chapter 31)

Theft (PC 31.03)

A person commits theft if they unlawfully appropriate property with the intent to deprive the owner of the property. Appropriation is unlawful if it's without the owner's effective consent, or if the property is known to be stolen.

Key Definitions (Flashcards)

Deprive
To withhold property permanently or for so long that a major portion of its value is lost; or to dispose of it in a way that makes recovery unlikely.
Appropriate
To acquire or exercise control over property (other than real property).
Effective Consent
Consent is NOT effective if induced by deception/coercion, or from someone you know can't give it (e.g., due to youth, intoxication, advanced age).
Presumption of Theft for Dealers

An actor who is in the business of buying and selling used or secondhand personal property is presumed to know property is stolen if they pay $25 or more for it and knowingly or recklessly fail to:

  • Record the seller's name, address, and physical description.
  • Record a complete description of the property, including serial number.
  • Obtain a signed warranty from the seller stating they have the right to possess the property.

Offense Enhancements

The offense level is increased to the next higher category if it is shown that:

  • The actor was a public servant or a government contractor acting in their capacity.
  • The victim was elderly, disabled, or a non-profit organization.
  • The actor, in committing the theft, deactivated a fire alarm or retail theft detector.

Burglary Offenses (Chapter 30)

Enter
Intrusion of any part of the body, or any physical object connected to the body.
Habitation
A structure or vehicle adapted for the overnight accommodation of persons. Includes separately secured portions.
Building
Any enclosed structure intended for use or occupation as a habitation or for trade, manufacture, ornament, or use.
Burglary (PC 30.02)

A person commits burglary if, without effective consent, they:

  • Enter a habitation or building (not open to public) with intent to commit a felony, theft, or assault.
  • Remain concealed in a habitation or building with that same intent.
  • Enter a habitation or building and commit or attempt to commit a felony, theft, or assault.

Burglary of Habitation

  • Default: 2nd Degree Felony.
  • Enhanced: 1st Degree Felony if the intended or committed offense is a felony other than theft.

Burglary of Building

  • Default: State Jail Felony.
Burglary of Vehicles (PC 30.04)

A person commits this offense if, without effective consent, they break into or enter a vehicle (or any part of it) with intent to commit any felony or theft.

  • Default: Class A Misdemeanor.
  • Enhanced: State Jail Felony if they have 2+ prior convictions for this offense, or if the vehicle is a rail car.
  • Enhanced: 3rd Degree Felony if it's a drug wholesaler's vehicle and the intent is to steal controlled substances.
Burglary of Coin-Operated Machine (PC 30.03)

Breaking or entering any coin-operated or coin-activated machine with intent to obtain property or services. This is a Class A Misdemeanor.

Auto Theft Investigation

Unauthorized Use of a Vehicle (UUMV) (PC 31.07)

A person commits UUMV if they intentionally or knowingly operate another's boat, airplane, or motor-propelled vehicle without the owner's effective consent. This is a State Jail Felony.

The Salvage Switch

A method of disguising a stolen car by altering its identity. Thieves place the Vehicle Identification Number (VIN) from a salvaged (totaled) vehicle onto a stolen vehicle of the same make and model. This allows the thief to sell the stolen car with what appears to be a legitimate title.

Detection:

  • A VIN check returns a "junk" or "salvage" title.
  • VIN plate or rivets appear damaged, distorted, or are not the factory-type.
  • Compare the dashboard VIN with secondary VINs (door jamb sticker, engine block, etc.).
  • Spelling errors or incorrect logos on the Federal Safety Certification Label (door jamb sticker).
Rental Cars: Civil vs. Criminal

An overdue rental car is typically a civil matter. It becomes a criminal matter (Theft of Service) only after the rental agency sends a written demand for return of the vehicle via certified mail and the renter fails to return it within the specified time frame. Officers should verify if the company has followed these steps before taking criminal action.

Indicators of a Stolen Vehicle

Exterior Indicators (Pre-Stop)

  • Broken glass on seats or floor.
  • License plate doesn't match vehicle registration or is for a different vehicle type.
  • Clean plate on a dirty car (or vice-versa).
  • Plates held on by wire, magnets, or other temporary means.
  • Punched out door or trunk locks.

Interior Indicators (Post-Stop)

  • Steering column is broken, cracked, or covered.
  • Ignition has been punched out or pulled; wires are exposed.
  • Key is not the correct one for the vehicle (e.g., a shaved key, screwdriver).
  • VIN plate is loose, damaged, or obscured.
  • Major components (stereo, airbag) are missing.

Other Related Offenses

Theft of Service (PC 31.04)

Intentionally or knowingly securing performance of a service (labor, lodging, rental, etc.) by deception, threat, or false token with intent to avoid payment.

Punishment follows the standard Theft Ladder.

Presumption of Intent: Intent to avoid payment is presumed if a person absconds from a hotel/restaurant, or fails to return a rental car after receiving a proper written demand.

Specialized Theft Offenses
Mail Theft (PC 31.20): Ranges from Class A Misdemeanor (fewer than 10 addressees) to 1st Degree Felony (50+ addressees with ID info).
Organized Retail Theft (PC 31.16): Intentionally conducting, promoting, or facilitating activities in which a person receives, possesses, conceals, or sells stolen retail merchandise. Follows the Theft Ladder and is enhanced if the actor is an organizer/manager.
Cargo Theft (PC 31.18): Knowingly dealing in stolen commercial freight. Ranges from State Jail Felony ($1,500+) to 1st Degree Felony ($200,000+).
Tampering with ID Numbers (PC 31.11): Knowingly removing, altering, or possessing property with an obliterated serial number. A Class A Misdemeanor.
Preparatory Offenses
Criminal Attempt (PC 15.01): An act beyond mere preparation that tends but fails to commit an offense. Punished one category lower than the target offense.
Unlawful Use of Criminal Instrument (PC 16.01): Possessing an instrument specially designed/adapted for crime with intent to use it. Punished one category lower than the intended offense. Manufacturing is a State Jail Felony.
Unlawful Use of Retail Theft Instruments (PC 16.02): Possessing, manufacturing, or selling a "shielding or deactivation instrument" for retail theft is a Class A Misdemeanor.

Scenario Q&A

What are the core elements required to prove "Theft" under Texas law (31.03)?
To prove theft, a prosecutor must show that a person unlawfully appropriated property with the specific intent to deprive the owner of that property. The appropriation is unlawful if it is without the owner's effective consent or if the property was stolen and the actor knew it was stolen.
Provide two examples of when "consent" is NOT considered effective.
Consent is not effective if it is induced by deception or coercion. It is also not effective if given by a person the actor knows is not legally authorized to act for the owner, such as an employee giving away company property against policy, or by someone known to be incapacitated due to youth, mental defect, or intoxication.
Explain the concept of "deprive" as it relates to theft.
"Deprive" means more than just temporary taking. It means to withhold property from the owner permanently or for such an extended period that a major portion of its value or enjoyment is lost. It also includes actions like disposing of the property in a way that makes its recovery by the owner unlikely.
Describe the "preventing consequences of theft" rule (CCP 18.16).
CCP 18.16 allows any person, not just law enforcement, to seize property they have reasonable grounds to believe is stolen. They can also seize the person suspected of the theft. The seizer must then immediately take the property and suspect to a peace officer or magistrate.
List three exterior indicators that an officer might observe to suspect a vehicle is stolen.
An officer might suspect a vehicle is stolen if they see broken glass on the floor or seats, indicating forced entry. Other indicators include a license plate that doesn't match the vehicle's registration, or a plate that is attached with wire or magnets, suggesting it was recently and hastily put on.
What is a "salvage switch" in the context of auto theft?
A "salvage switch" is the criminal practice of altering a stolen vehicle's identity by replacing its VIN plate with one from a similar make and model that has been wrecked or "salvaged." This makes the stolen vehicle appear legitimate, allowing the thief to obtain a title and sell it.
How can law enforcement distinguish between a civil and criminal issue with an overdue rental car?
An overdue rental is a civil issue until the rental company follows the specific procedures in the Penal Code to establish criminal intent. This involves sending a formal written demand for the car's return by certified mail. Only after the renter fails to comply with this notice does it typically become a criminal Theft of Service case.
Define "habitation" and "building" as they are used in burglary offenses.
"Habitation" is a structure or vehicle adapted for the overnight accommodation of persons, including any attached structures or separately secured portions. "Building" is a broader term for any enclosed structure intended for use as a habitation or for purposes of trade, manufacture, or use.

Glossary of Key Terms

Appropriate
To acquire or otherwise exercise control over property other than real property.
Building
Any enclosed structure intended for use or occupation as a habitation or for some purpose of trade, manufacture, ornament, or use.
Criminal Instrument
Anything specially designed, made, or adapted for use in the commission of an offense.
Deception
Creating a false impression, failing to correct a false impression, or promising performance with no intent to perform.
Deprive
To withhold property permanently or for an extended period making recovery unlikely, or to restore property only upon compensation.
Effective Consent
Consent by a person legally authorized to act, which is not induced by deception/coercion or given by someone known to be incapacitated.
Enter (Burglary)
To intrude any part of the body or any physical object connected with the body.
Habitation
A structure or vehicle adapted for the overnight accommodation of persons, including separately secured portions.
Property
Real property, tangible or intangible personal property, or a document representing anything of value.
Service
Includes labor, professional services, transportation, lodging, restaurant service, and the supply of a motor vehicle for use.
Shielding or Deactivation Instrument
A device used to prevent or attempt to prevent detection by a retail theft detector.
Unauthorized Use of a Vehicle (UUV)
Intentionally or knowingly operating another's boat, airplane, or motor-propelled vehicle without the owner’s effective consent.
