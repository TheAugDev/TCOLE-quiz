Verbal Persuasion: Handling Non-Compliance

An Interactive Study Guide

Understanding Crisis & Non-Compliance

What is a Crisis?

A temporary state of upset and disorganization, characterized by an inability to cope with a situation using customary problem-solving methods. In a crisis, a person's emotions are high, and their ability to think rationally is low.

Voluntary vs. Involuntary Non-Compliance

It's crucial to determine WHY a person is not cooperating. Are they choosing not to comply, or are they unable to comply due to being in a state of crisis?

  • Voluntary Non-Compliance: The person is making a conscious choice to refuse. The communication models in this guide (4 Appeals, LEAPS) are designed for this.
  • Involuntary Non-Compliance: The person is overwhelmed by emotion (crisis, intoxicants, etc.) and cannot think rationally. This requires specialized de-escalation tactics.

The 4 Verbal Appeals (5-Step Model)

This model provides a structured approach for gaining voluntary compliance from someone who is refusing lawful orders.

Step 1: Ethical Appeal (Ask)

Based on your position as a professional officer. You make a polite request, appealing to their sense of decency. This aligns with the universal truth that people want to be asked, not told.

Example: "Sir, for my safety and yours, could you please show me your hands?"

Step 2: Rational Appeal (Why)

Appeals to their common sense and reason. You explain the 'why' behind your request, which satisfies another universal truth. This is valuable for people with a strong sense of right and wrong.

Example: "I need to see your hands because I can't see what's in them, and that's a safety concern for both of us."

Step 3: Practical Appeal (Options)

Based on the urgent need to change the situation. You present their options, always starting with the positive choice first. This gives them control and makes them think about consequences.

Example: "You have a choice here. You can show me your hands, and we can continue this conversation calmly. If you choose not to, you will force me to take action to ensure my safety, and I don't want to do that."

Step 4 & 5: Personal Appeal (Confirm) & Act

Step 4 (Confirm Refusal): You give them one last chance to comply by confirming their decision. This makes sure they understand what is about to happen.

Example: "Is there anything I can say to get you to show me your hands? This is your last chance to work with me here."

Step 5 (Act): If they continue to refuse, you take the appropriate and lawful action. If they ask a legitimate question, you can answer it and loop back, but avoid excessive repetition.

The L.E.A.P.S. Model

A powerful communication pattern for awkward or tense situations, designed to build rapport and de-escalate by showing you are engaged and understanding.

Listen Actively

Fully concentrate on what is being said, both verbally and non-verbally. Pay attention to word choice, tone, and body language. Don't just wait for your turn to talk.

Empathize

Understand and acknowledge the other person's feelings from their perspective, without judgment. "See through the eye of another."

Ask Questions

Use open-ended questions (Who, What, When, Where, How, Why) to gather information, clarify their story, and show you are interested.

Paraphrase

Restate what the person said in your own words. This confirms your understanding, makes them a better listener, and gives you control of the conversation.

Summarize

Conclude the conversation by summarizing their main points and stating the resolution or what will happen next. This provides a clear bottom line.

Communication Blunders to Avoid

The Four Horsemen of Communication Breakdown
  • Criticism: Attacking the person's character instead of their behavior. (e.g., "You're acting like a jerk.")
  • Contempt: The worst blunder. Conveying disgust through sarcasm, name-calling, eye-rolling, or mockery.
  • Defensiveness: Blaming the other person ("The problem isn't me, it's you."). This shuts down cooperation.
  • Stonewalling: Tuning out, disengaging, and not allowing the other person to be heard.
Other Common Blunders
  • Making assumptions before hearing the whole message.
  • Letting your mind drift instead of actively listening.
  • Interrupting, which non-verbally says "I'm more important than you."
  • Having apathetic body language (e.g., crossing arms, avoiding eye contact).
  • Needing to "win" an argument, which creates defensiveness and prevents cooperation.
