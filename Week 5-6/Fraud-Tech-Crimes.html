Fraud Offenses (Chapters 31, 32, 37)

Characteristics of Con Artists

  • Promise unusual returns ("something for nothing").
  • Use a victim's own greed against them.
  • Often use props and work with partners.
  • Prey on the elderly, are highly mobile, and tend to be non-violent.
Forgery (PC 32.21)

A person commits an offense if they forge a writing with intent to defraud or harm another. "Forge" means to alter, make, complete, execute, or authenticate a writing so it purports to be the act of another who did not authorize it.

  • Default Offense: Class A Misdemeanor.
  • State Jail Felony: If the writing is a check, credit card, will, deed, mortgage, or other commercial instrument.
  • 3rd Degree Felony: If the writing is money, stamps, a government record, or stock/bond.
  • Theft Ladder Applies: If the forgery was committed to obtain or attempt to obtain property or service, the punishment follows the theft value ladder.
Tampering with Governmental Record (PC 37.10)

An offense if a person knowingly makes a false entry in, makes/presents/uses a false record, or intentionally destroys, conceals, or removes a governmental record.

  • Punishments range from a Class C Misdemeanor to a 2nd Degree Felony depending on the type of document and its use.
  • It is NOT a defense that the document was marked "NOT A GOVERNMENT DOCUMENT" unless specific formatting rules are followed.
Other Fraud-Related Offenses
Criminal Simulation (PC 32.22): With intent to defraud, making or altering an object so it appears to have a value it does not (due to age, rarity, source, etc.). A Class A Misdemeanor.
Deceptive Business Practices (PC 32.42): A broad offense covering acts like using false weights/measures, selling less than the represented quantity, passing off property as another's, or making false statements in advertising. Punishments range from Class C Misdemeanor to Class A Misdemeanor.
Fraudulent Destruction, Removal, or Concealment of Writing (PC 32.47): With intent to defraud, destroying or concealing a writing like a will, deed, or price tag. A Class A Misdemeanor, but a State Jail Felony if the writing is a will, deed, mortgage, etc.
Fraudulent Securing of Document Execution (PC 32.46): By deception, causing another to sign a document affecting property or pecuniary interest. Punishment follows the theft ladder. It is a State Jail Felony to cause a public servant to file a fraudulent judgment.

Insurance & Healthcare Fraud (Chapters 35 & 35A)

Insurance Fraud (PC 35.02)

A person commits an offense if, with intent to defraud or deceive an insurer, they prepare or present a statement in support of a claim or application for a policy that they know contains false or misleading material information.

  • Claim Fraud: Punishment follows the theft ladder based on the value of the claim.
  • Application Fraud: A State Jail Felony.
  • Enhancement: The offense for claim fraud becomes a 1st Degree Felony if the act places a person at risk of death or SBI.
Healthcare Fraud (PC Chapter 35A)

A broad statute covering fraudulent acts related to healthcare. The offense level follows the standard theft value ladder and is stackable with other offenses.

Technology & Computer Crimes (Chapters 33 & 33A)

Breach of Computer Security (PC 33.02)

Simple Access: Knowingly accessing a computer, network, or system without the effective consent of the owner. A Class B Misdemeanor, enhanced to a State Jail Felony if the system belongs to the government or a critical infrastructure facility.

Access with Intent to Harm/Defraud: Knowingly accessing a computer, etc., with the intent to defraud or harm another, or to alter, damage, or delete property. This offense follows the theft ladder based on the value of the loss, with specific enhancements for targeting critical infrastructure or obtaining large amounts of identifying information.

Online Impersonation (PC 33.07)

An offense if a person, with intent to harm, defraud, intimidate, or threaten, uses the name or persona of another person to create a web page or post/send messages through a social networking site.

  • A 3rd Degree Felony.
  • Sending a fraudulent email/text/IM with similar intent is a Class A Misdemeanor, but becomes a 3rd Degree Felony if done to solicit a response by emergency personnel.
Telecommunications Crimes (Chapter 33A)

This chapter covers crimes related to the theft and unauthorized use of telecommunication services (e.g., phone, internet).

  • Theft of Telecommunications Service (PC 33A.04): Knowingly obtaining service to avoid lawful charges.
  • Unauthorized Use of Telecommunications Service (PC 33A.02): An employee of a provider knowingly using or diverting service for their own benefit.
  • Punishment: Both offenses follow a unique punishment ladder, starting at a Class B Misdemeanor (less than $500) and going up to a 1st Degree Felony ($200,000 or more).
  • Manufacture/Possession/Delivery of Unlawful Device (PC 33A.03): Making or possessing a counterfeit telecommunications device. A 3rd Degree Felony.
