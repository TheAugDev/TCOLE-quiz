Chapter 35: Patrol Fundamentals ▶

Objectives of Patrol

  • Preserve the peace by mere presence and proper action (CCP 2.13).
  • Prevent crime by opportunity reduction; duty to prevent (CCP 6.06, 6.07).
  • Suppress crime by timely response to crimes in progress and by properly investigating offenses (CCP 6.05, 6.06).
  • Apprehend suspects.
  • Regulate non-criminal conduct by obtaining and maintaining good officer-citizen relationships to protect life and property (CCP 6.07).

Importance of Patrol

Protection: Prevention of crime is the soundest of all criminological theories.

Service: To the community of which each peace officer is a part.

Mental & Physical Preparation ▶

Mental Preparation & Mindset

The single most important factor in officer survival is mental conditioning. The transition from civilian to police work requires mental and emotional conditioning to adapt to psychological pressures.

Key Factors in Psychological Preparedness:

  • Attitude development and preparation.
  • Sociological and psychological maturity.
  • Both formal and informal education.
  • A strong personal value system.

Attitudes to Avoid:

  • Isolationism: Feeling that you can no longer connect with non-police friends and family.
  • Paranoia: Regarding all people suspiciously or as would-be criminals.
  • Withdrawal: Failing to be a normal part of the community.

Physical Preparation (Before Each Shift)

  • Personal Grooming: Cleanliness, uniform tailored and pressed, shined footwear.
  • Personal Equipment: Notebook, pen, sidearm (clean, operational, loaded), extra ammunition, foul weather gear, handcuffs (clean, operational with key readily available), body armor (worn at all times on duty), flashlight, traffic whistle.
  • Personal Actions: Maintain good posture, keep hands out of pockets, avoid stuffed pockets, do not smoke while confronting the public, wear headgear properly.

Beat Knowledge & Patrol Hazards ▶

Beat Characteristics

Knowing your beat increases on-site arrests, officer safety, and decreases response time.

  • Geography: Main streets, businesses, schools, hospitals, street directions (N/S/E/W), block numbers, dead ends, alleys, one-ways, overpasses.
  • Conditions: Crime hazards, types of crimes committed, socio-economic characteristics, quality of life issues (e.g., abandoned houses, potholes).
  • Known Offenders: Familiarity with their habits and types of crimes (TCIC/NCIC info).

TCOLE's 3 Kinds of Patrol Hazards (Click to Flip)

1. Silhouetting

(Click to reveal)

Placing yourself or your unit in a position that provides the suspect with a definite, identifiable target. Avoid standing in doorways, peering openly through windows, or using flashlights improperly.

2. Telltale Noises

(Click to reveal)

Noises that can give away your position: slamming car doors, loud radio, seatbelt buckle pop, jangling keys/equipment. Secure equipment to avoid "jingle jangle" noise.

3. Suspect's Hands

(Click to reveal)

A primary hazard. Demand hands are visible (in front, palms up). Do not allow them in pockets, as they could be hiding weapons, contraband, or identifying marks. If hands are already in pockets, do not allow removal without specific tactical considerations.

Cover vs. Concealment

Cover: Any material reasonably expected to stop a bullet (brick walls, engine block, large tree trunk). PREFERRED.

Concealment: Hides you from view but offers no protection (shrubs, drywall, window blinds). Use at the greatest distance possible if cover is unavailable.

Patrol Modes & Methods ▶

Problem Area Patrols

  • Preventive Patrol: High visibility, random patterns, property checks, questioning suspicious persons. Includes Preventive Enforcement (varied, random presence) and Selective Patrol (focusing on specific problems at specific times/locations).
  • Apprehension Patrol: Low visibility, surveillance, and covert tactics to catch offenders in the act.

Patrol Modes & Advantages

Bicycle: High visibility, community relations, flexible in areas cars can't go.
Motorcycle: Quick response, traffic enforcement, escort units.
Foot: Immediate traffic control, person-to-person contact, develop informants, increased knowledge of beat.
Automobile: Speed, mobility, protection, allows for extra equipment.
Fixed Wing/Helicopter: Covers enormous area, sky view of fleeing suspects, great apprehension tool.
Mounted (Horse): Good public relations, mobility in crowds/rough terrain.

One vs. Two Officer Patrol

One-Officer Patrol

  • Doubles preventive enforcement (more cars).
  • Officer develops self-reliance.
  • Fewer personality clashes.
  • More attention devoted to patrol functions.

Two-Officer Patrol

  • Greater safety factor.
  • Can be a training aid.
  • Share driving duties.
  • Two pairs of eyes are better than one.

Pedestrian Stops & Field Interviews ▶

When to Conduct a Pedestrian Stop

When a pedestrian is: carrying something suspicious, acting strangely, staying in the same place for no reason, or stopping other people on the street. Until you have reasonable suspicion, it is a consensual encounter.

How to Perform a Pedestrian Stop

  • Where: Note escape routes, gauge officer safety, consider potential for hostages, and report location to dispatch.
  • How: Use effective verbal communication, approach carefully, maintain visual contact, watch for furtive movements.
  • Approach Tactics:
    • Direct/Front: Can observe all movements but lose surprise and are vulnerable.
    • Rear: Gain surprise and reduce direct attack probability, but may provoke a defensive physical response.
    • Right/Left Side: Keeps suspect off balance. Right side is often preferred as most people are right-handed.
  • Positioning: Gun side away, maintain distance (arm's length+), stay aware of surroundings. Do not get boxed in.

Interviewing Multiple Subjects

  • One Officer, Multiple Subjects: Assume a safe position for observation and control. Interview separately. Do not perform non-emergency actions without backup.
  • Two Officers, One Subject: The contact officer takes the lead. The cover officer takes a position to the right or left rear to avoid crossfire.
  • Two Officers, Multiple Subjects: Use a spread "V" formation. The cover officer maintains maximum visibility of all subjects and communicates any threats to the contact officer.

4 Personal Contact Risk Zones (Scan Continuously)

1. Face & Chest

  • Friend or Foe?: Assess their role (cop/victim/witness), look for badges, etc.
  • Facial Expression: What is their intent and purpose? Look for furtive movements upon arrival.
  • Behavioral Cues:
    • Repetitive aggressive inquiries.
    • Voice pace changes (e.g., slowing down could indicate planning/stalling).
    • Inappropriate or ignoring responses to questions.
    • Looking toward a specific area after a contraband-related question.
    • Looking around for escape routes or weapons as interview deteriorates.
  • Signs of Influence/Crisis: Grief, anxiety, fear, anger, mental illness.
  • Intoxication Signs:
    • Eyes: red, droopy, glassy, dilated, or constricted.
    • Presence of paraphernalia.
  • Anger/Danger Signs:
    • 1000-yard stare, profanity, breaking stare to look for targets (your body/weapons).
    • Facial color pales, eyebrows drop, lips tighten, growling.
    • Head drops forward to protect throat.

2. Hands (Most Critical Zone)

  • Position: Ideally, hands should be in front and palms up.
  • Hands in Pockets/Out of Sight: Could be nerves, or hiding weapons, contraband, or identifying marks (scars, tattoos).
  • Tactical Consideration: If hands are in pockets, have the person turn their head away, kneel, or even go prone before frisking. Do not let them remove their hands without your control.
  • Anxiety/Disinterest Signs:
    • Crossing arms, tapping fingers, jangling keys.
    • Repeatedly rubbing back of neck, ears, or eyes.
    • Grooming (nervous energy), "facial wipe."
  • Anger Signs: Fist making, flailing hands, hands raised above waist.

3. Waistband

  • A common place for weapons.
  • Can you see the waistline, or is it covered by a shirt/coat?
  • Look for unnatural bulges under the shirt or in pockets.
  • Keep their hands away from their waistband.
  • Look for accelerated breathing, which can be associated with stress or intent.

4. Stance

  • Pre-Attack Indicators:
    • Bobbing up and down or rocking back and forth.
    • Bladed stance with shoulders shifted and tense.
    • Standing tall to maximize height.
    • Kicking the ground.
    • Pacing, backing away, or sudden stop-and-start behavior.
    • Exaggerated movements.
    • Lowering of body before a final attack, especially if out of breath.

Cover Officer Duties & Pursuit Tactics

Contact & Cover Principles

  • The Cover Officer is NOT the Contact Officer's servant; their role is protection.
  • Maintain a position of maximum visibility of ALL involved parties.
  • Avoid lengthy conversations that detract from the primary cover duty.
  • Communicate or handle any observed threats immediately.

If a Suspect Runs (Pursuit Policy)

  • NO PURSUIT: If you are alone with multiple other subjects. Broadcast a description and direction of travel instead.
  • NO PURSUIT: If pursuing the suspect leaves your partner in an unsafe situation.
  • PURSUE: Only if it is safe to do so and within your departmental policy.

Foot Pursuit Tactics

  • Radio First: In a loud, calm, controlled voice, announce your call sign, "foot pursuit," location, and direction of travel. Follow up with a suspect description.
  • Maintain Visuals: If you cannot catch the suspect, keep them in sight and continue to radio updates.
  • Watch for Ambushes: Be extremely cautious. Do not cut corners without "pie-ing" them (slicing the pie) to check for threats.
  • Apprehension: When you catch up, tripping is often a better tactic than tackling.
Levels of Police Interaction ▶

Level 0: Consensual Encounter

Officer is free to approach and ask questions. Person is free to refuse to answer, refuse to ID, and walk away. No suspicion needed.

Case Law: Florida v. Royer

Level 1: Investigative Stop/Detention (Terry Stop)

Temporary seizure for investigation. Requires Reasonable Suspicion: Articulable facts and circumstances that would lead a reasonable officer to believe that a crime has occurred, is occurring, or is about to occur.

Case Law: Terry v. Ohio, Alabama v. White

Level 2: Arrest

Taking a person into custody to charge them with a crime. Requires Probable Cause: A reasonable ground to suspect that a person has committed or is committing a crime.

Case Law: U.S. v. Mendenhall, Illinois v. Gates

Scenario Analysis ▶

Scenario 1

At 1300 hours on a Tuesday, an officer observes a subject who appears to be a juvenile looking into car windows in a store parking lot while school is in session. The subject tries the door handles on two cars. A few moments later, he pulls open the door of a third car and begins to rummage around inside. When contacted, he says the car is his and he lost his keys. The car is registered to someone else who, when contacted, says she does not know the juvenile.

At the moment the officer sees the juvenile rummaging in the car, what is the highest level of justification the officer has?

The officer has articulable facts (time of day, school in session, looking in windows, trying handles, entering vehicle) to form reasonable suspicion that a Burglary of a Vehicle is in progress, justifying a detention. Probable Cause is developed later when the owner is contacted.

Scenario 2

An officer is dispatched to a family violence call. Upon arrival, the officer knocks but gets no answer. The officer then hears what sounds like someone screaming from the back of the residence.

What legal principle allows the officer to enter the residence without a warrant?

Correct. The exigent circumstance of providing emergency aid to prevent serious injury allows the officer to enter. The sound of a scream provides the probable cause to believe someone inside is in danger.
Chapter 27: The Critical Decision-Making Model (CDM) ▶

The CDM: An Overview

Core Principles: The Moral Compass

Ethics • Values • Proportionality • Sanctity of Human Life

These principles are unique to an agency's mission and must guide every step. No action should contradict this core.

What Can the CDM Do For Us?

  • Help handle calls more effectively and safely.
  • Provide an organized and improved approach to decision-making.
  • Provide more officer options and techniques.
  • Serve as a problem-solving model for complex community issues.

The 5 Steps of the CDM (The "Spinning Model")

The CDM is a fluid, "spinning" model. An officer can always spin back to a previous step as a situation evolves. Be aware of implicit bias (subconscious attitudes) as it can affect understanding and actions at any step.

Step 1: Collect Information

This is an ongoing process. The foundational goals are to obtain proper CONTEXT for the call and form the optimal MINDSET.

  • Sources: Dispatch, other officers, computer networks, witnesses, family.
  • Key Questions: Who called and why? Are weapons/children/animals present? What is the location's history (previous calls, violence, MH issues)? What further information do I need?
  • Legal Math: While collecting info, constantly assess if the facts amount to reasonable suspicion, probable cause, or nothing at all.

Step 2: Assess Situation, Threats, and Risks

Analyze the information collected to understand the current situation.

  • Key Questions: What is the threat level (Low/High/Unknown)? Do I need to take immediate action? Am I trained/equipped? What is the source of the subject's aggression (malice, crisis, etc.)?
  • Threat Assessment: Consider the subject's Means, Ability, Opportunity, and Intent.
  • Officer Mindset: Keep complacency and emotions in check. Model the desired behavior and avoid malice transfer.
  • Resource Needs: Do I need a Supervisor, SWAT, Negotiators, K9, or other agencies?

Step 3: Consider Police Powers and Agency Policy

Understand your legal standing and departmental rules.

  • What is my legal authority to be here?
  • Is this a police matter?
  • What legal powers do I have to take action?
  • What agency policies control my response?

Step 4: Identify Options and Determine Best Course of Action

Develop a plan based on the assessment.

  • Do I have to act now, or can I "spin back" to collect more info?
  • How proportional is my planned response to the situation?
  • What are my backup plans and contingencies?

Step 5: Act, Review, and Re-assess

Execute the plan and evaluate its effectiveness.

  • Take action.
  • Did it resolve the situation? If not, "spin the model" again to another step.
  • After the incident, conduct self-reflection for lessons learned to apply in the future.

Benefits of Using the CDM

  • Organizes the decision-making process, which helps officers make better, more sound decisions.
  • The "spinning model" concept acknowledges the need for urgent, split-second decisions while still providing a framework.
  • Assists in articulating actions after an incident by providing a structured, rational, and credible explanation for reports and testimony.
    • "First, I collected the following information..."
    • "Next, I assessed the threats and risks, and developed a working strategy..."
    • "Then, I considered applicable laws and agency policies..."
Public Service & Community Resources ▶

Methods to Enhance Public Service

  • Visual Presence: Being seen in a patrol car, on foot, or other modes.
  • Non-Punitive Interaction: Getting out of the car and simply talking to people.
  • Community Involvement: Public speaking at civic gatherings, participating in clubs or service agencies (e.g., YMCA, Big Brother/Big Sister).
  • Seeking Citizen Input: Using informal conversations or formal methods like questionnaires and "Citizen Police Academies" to understand community concerns.
  • Public Service Announcements: Using media for crime prevention tips, radar locations, "most wanted" lists, etc.

Emergency Community Resources & Referrals

A key service role is making appropriate referrals when a problem is not a direct police function. Know your community's resources.

Referral Examples:

  • Salvation Army / Shelters
  • Rape Crisis Centers
  • United Way
  • Animal Control
  • City Services (Code Enforcement)
  • Medical Examiner / JP
  • State Dept. of Mental Health
  • Child/Elderly Protective Services

Important Warning

Officers should never make specific referrals to private businesses like private attorneys or bail bondsmen, as this could lead to liability.

Conclusion & Final Thoughts

Stay vigilant in your careers for signs of apathy, pessimism, isolation, and negative police subculture elements in your work and personal lives.

Avoid the "us against them" mentality and remember that MOST of the public supports and believes in you and your efforts.
