Criminal Investigation & Crime Scene Processing

An Interactive Study Guide

Fundamentals of Criminal Investigation

Key Definitions

Criminal Investigation: The process of legally gathering evidence of a crime that has been or is being committed.

Corpus Delicti: Literally "the body of the crime"; the proof establishing that a crime has occurred.

Evidence: Anything offered in court to prove the truth or falsity of a fact in issue.

Modus Operandi (M.O.): A criminal's characteristic method of operation.

Probable Cause: Credible information that would cause a reasonable person to believe a suspect committed a crime or that evidence will be found in a particular place.

Reasonable Suspicion: Specific articulable facts that would reasonably warrant the detention of a person.

Goals & Tools of an Investigator

Primary Goals:

  • To determine if a crime has been committed.
  • To legally obtain information and evidence to identify the person(s) responsible.
  • To legally arrest the suspect(s).
  • To recover all stolen property.
  • To present the best case possible to the prosecutor.

Investigator's Tools:

  • Information: Knowledge gathered from victims, witnesses, suspects, and records.
  • Instrumentation (Forensic Science): Techniques like fingerprinting, serology, ballistics, and DNA analysis.
  • Interviewing: The questioning of victims, witnesses, or suspects.
  • Laws of Arrest, Search, and Seizure: Knowledge of legal guidelines to ensure evidence is admissible.
Characteristics of a Criminal Investigator
  • Suspicious: Takes nothing for granted and verifies all information.
  • Curious: Desires to learn the facts and truth about people, places, and objects.
  • Observant: Develops the ability to take accurate notice using all five senses.
  • Unbiased & Unprejudiced: Avoids personal distortions of judgment.
  • Develops Rapport: Uses interpersonal skills to build trust with victims and witnesses.
  • Logical Thinking: Applies logic to compare statements, behavior, and physical evidence.
  • Creative Problem Solving: Thinks of alternative ways to accomplish tasks or secure information.

The 5 Stages of a Preliminary Investigation

Stage 1: Scene Security and Protection

The first responder's initial actions. The priority is safety of persons first, then evidence. This includes checking for signs of life, noting any changes made during rescue operations, and beginning the chain of custody by controlling access.

Stage 2: First Responder Investigation

Involves a careful survey of the scene, conducting initial interviews, and documenting all initial observations, changes, sounds, and smells.

Stage 3: Processing the Crime Scene

The systematic process of searching for evidence, photographing the scene and evidence, sketching the scene, and then identifying, collecting, marking, and preserving evidence.

Stage 4: Scene Wrap-up

Before leaving, personnel conduct a final survey of the scene, debrief to share findings, and inventory all evidence and equipment to ensure nothing is left behind.

Stage 5: Conclude and Document

The final stage involves transporting evidence, notifying next of kin if necessary, creating the case file, and writing all required reports.

Crime Scene Search & Processing

Crime Scene Search Methods

Strip/Grid/Line Search

Among the most effective for outside searches. Can be done in one direction (strip) or two compass directions (grid) for a second view of the same area.

Circular (Spiral) Search

Useful when an item is missing from the center of an area. The search can spiral inward or outward.

Quadrant/Sector/Zone Search

Effective for indoor and outdoor scenes with defined borders. The scene is divided into sections (e.g., rooms in a house), which are searched individually.

Point-to-Point Search

A preliminary method where the search moves in order of the appearance of evidence. Can be disorganized and may cause trace evidence to be overlooked.

Crime Scene Photography

Photography provides a permanent visual record of the scene as it was found. All photos should be logged, and no photos (even blurry ones) should be deleted.

The 3 Vantage Points:

  • Overall / Long-Range: An overview of the scene to establish its location (e.g., exterior of a building, aerial shots).
  • Mid-Range: Taken from 10-20 feet away, these photos show the relationship between items of evidence and other features of the scene.
  • Close-Up: Taken 5 feet or less from the subject, these are detailed photos of individual items of evidence. Examination-quality close-ups must include a scale and be taken at a 90-degree angle.
Crime Scene Sketching

Sketches supplement photos by providing accurate distance measurements and spatial relationships between objects.

Measurement Methods:

  • Triangulation: Measuring from two fixed points to a piece of evidence, forming a triangle. Best for scenes with clear landmarks.
  • Rectangular Coordinates: Measuring an object's distance from two mutually perpendicular lines (e.g., the walls of a room).
  • Transecting Baseline: A tape measure is run through the scene, and objects are measured by their distance from the tape at a right angle. Useful for large, irregular outdoor areas.

Evidence & Case Management

Chain of Custody

The process of recording all transactions of evidence from person to person since its acquisition. It maintains the integrity of the evidence by accounting for who found it, who handled it, where it was stored, and who transported it.

To maintain the chain of custody, limit the number of people who handle evidence and ensure every transfer is documented with names, dates, times, and receipts.

Evidence Packaging & Preservation
  • The purpose of proper packaging is to prevent breaking, spoiling, loss, and contamination.
  • Each different item of evidence should be packaged separately.
  • Wet evidence (e.g., bloody clothing) must be allowed to air-dry before being packaged in paper bags to prevent rotting and mold.
  • Firearms should be made safe (unloaded), but magazines should not be unloaded. Never stick anything down the barrel.
  • Paint evidence should be collected in a paper fold or tin, not a plastic bag, to avoid static electricity.
Follow-up Investigation & Case Management

Follow-up Investigation:

This involves continuing inquiries, developing and revising a theory of the crime, re-interviewing witnesses, preparing line-ups (sequential is preferred), and liaising with lab technicians.

Case Management:

A properly prepared case folder is essential for prosecution. It should contain offense reports, witness/victim statements, confessions, expert reports, and a prosecution summary that includes case facts, legality of arrest/seizure, and witness information.
