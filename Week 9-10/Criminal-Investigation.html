Criminal Investigation

An Interactive Study Guide

Fundamentals & Goals

Core Concepts & Definitions

Criminal Investigation: The process of legally gathering evidence of a crime that has been or is being committed.

Crime Scene Search: A planned and coordinated legal search of a crime scene to locate physical evidence or witnesses to the crime.

Corpus Delicti: Literally "the body of the crime." The proof establishing that a crime has occurred.

Key Terms:

  • Circumstantial Evidence: Indirect evidence from which deductions are drawn.
  • Complainant: The person requesting an investigation.
  • Elements of a Crime: Conditions that must occur for an act to be called a specific crime.
  • Evidence: Anything offered in court to prove the truth or falsity of a fact.
  • Modus Operandi (MO): A criminal's characteristic method of operation.
  • Probable Cause: Credible information that would cause a reasonable person to believe a suspect committed a crime or that evidence will be found in a particular place.
  • Reasonable Suspicion: Specific, articulable facts that would reasonably warrant the detention of a person.
Goals of a Criminal Investigator

The ultimate goal is to determine the truth about how a crime occurred. This includes:

  • To determine if a crime has been committed.
  • To legally obtain information and evidence to identify the person(s) responsible.
  • To legally arrest the suspect(s).
  • To recover all stolen property.
  • To present the best case possible to the prosecutor.
Characteristics of a Criminal Investigator
  • Suspicious: Takes nothing for granted and verifies all information.
  • Curious: Desires to learn the facts and truth.
  • Observant: Trained to use all five senses to give attention to details.
  • Unbiased & Unprejudiced: Avoids letting personal feelings influence the investigation.
  • Develops Rapport: Patient, courteous, and sympathetic to build trust.
  • Logical Thinking: Applies logic to compare behavior, statements, and evidence.
  • Creative Problem Solving: Thinks of alternative ways to accomplish tasks and secure evidence.

Preliminary Investigation & Scene Security

5 Stages of the Preliminary Investigation
  1. Scene Security and Protection: Prioritize safety of persons, then evidence. Check for signs of life and begin the chain of custody.
  2. First Responder Investigation: Conduct a careful scene survey, interview witnesses, and document all initial observations (sights, smells, changes).
  3. Processing the Crime Scene: The detailed process of searching, photographing, sketching, and collecting evidence.
  4. Scene Wrap-up: Debrief personnel, conduct a final survey, inventory evidence, and transport everything securely.
  5. Conclude and Document: Finalize reports and documentation.
Arrival & Securing the Scene

Upon Arrival:

  • Determine if suspects are at or near the scene.
  • Assess for weapons and determine appropriate tactical action.
  • Render first aid and call for EMS if needed.
  • Locate victims, witnesses, and complainants.
  • Share known suspect/vehicle descriptions, weapons, and direction of travel with other officers.

Securing the Scene:

  • Establish defined entry and exit points.
  • Set up inner and outer perimeters using tape, barricades, or personnel.
  • Protect the scene from contamination and removal of evidence.
  • Legal authority to protect the scene comes from CCP 6.06, 6.07, and Chapter 49.
Special Units & Assistance

Determine if specialized units are needed, such as:

  • EMS / Medical Examiner / JP
  • Bomb Squad / Fire Department
  • Narcotics Investigators / Other LE Agencies
  • Crime Scene Technicians / Detectives / Supervisors

Scene Processing: Search, Photos, Sketch

Crime Scene Search

Objectives of a Search:

  • Establish that a crime has been committed.
  • Place the suspect at the scene.
  • Eliminate persons as suspects.
  • Motivate confessions from suspects confronted with evidence.
  • Support witness testimony.
  • Determine the how, why, and when of the crime.

Search Methods:

  • Point-to-Point: Move in order of appearance of evidence. Often disorganized but can be a good preliminary step.
  • Strip/Grid/Line: Most effective for outside searches. A grid search provides two views of the same area.
  • Circular (Spiral): Useful when an item is missing from the center. Can start from inside or outside.
  • Quadrant/Sector/Zone: Effective for indoor/outdoor scenes with defined borders (e.g., rooms, vehicles).
  • Area: For small, specific areas like bushes or paths.
  • Aerial: May use infrared for things like gravesites.
Crime Scene Photography

Photography Procedures:

  • Take photos before the scene is disturbed. When in doubt, take the photo.
  • Maintain a photo log. Do not delete out-of-focus photos.
  • Use measurement scales (photograph item first without, then with the scale).
  • Work from exterior to interior, general to specific.

Three Major Vantage Points:

  1. Overall / Long-Range: Overview of the scene to establish location.
  2. Mid-Range: Shows relationship between evidence items. Taken from 10-20 feet.
  3. Close-Up: Detailed shots of individual evidence items. Taken from 5 feet or less. Examination quality photos must include a scale and be taken at a 90-degree angle.
Crime Scene Sketching

Value of a Sketch:

  • Provides a permanent record of distances and spatial relationships.
  • Helps in reconstructing the scene and corroborating testimony.
  • Eliminates confusing details.

Sketching Methods:

  • Triangulation: A bird's-eye view using two fixed points to plot items.
  • Rectangular Coordinates: Uses two mutually perpendicular lines (like walls) as baselines.
  • Transecting Baseline: A tape is run across the scene, and objects are measured at right angles from the tape. Useful for large, irregular outdoor areas.

A rough sketch should be made at the scene (accurate but not to scale), and a final, scaled drawing can be made later. The sketch must include a key/legend, scale, and indicate North.

Evidence Collection & Chain of Custody

Chain of Custody

Chain of Custody: The process of recording all transactions of evidence from person to person since its acquisition by law enforcement.

It accounts for who found the item, where it was found, who took custody, who transported it, where it was stored, and its journey to/from the lab.

  • Limit the number of people handling evidence.
  • Record every transfer of custody (to whom, date, time, reason).
  • Anyone handling evidence should affix their name/ID and date to the package.
  • Use signed receipts for transfers.
Packaging & Preservation

General Principles:

  • The purpose is to prevent breaking, spoiling, loss, and contamination.
  • Each item should be packaged separately.
  • Use paper bags instead of plastic for biological evidence to prevent moisture buildup and degradation.
  • Wet evidence (e.g., bloodstains) must be air-dried before final packaging. Liquid blood must be refrigerated.
  • Always use appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE).
Specific Evidence Collection

Firearms:

  • Document position of slide, cylinder, hammer, and safety before moving. Unload the weapon (but not the magazine). Do not clean.
  • Never place an object inside the barrel.
  • If found in water, submerge the firearm in the same water for transport to prevent rust.

Biological Evidence (Blood, Saliva, Semen):

  • Absorb liquid samples onto a clean cotton swab or cloth.
  • Moisten a swab with distilled water to collect dried stains.
  • Always collect an unstained control sample from near the evidence area.
  • Air-dry all samples before packaging in paper. Do not use plastic.
  • For sexual assault exams, use a standard kit and refrigerate samples.

Trace Evidence (Hair, Fibers, Paint):

  • Collect carefully using tweezers or by vacuuming. Package in a paper fold (druggist's fold) or a sealed container.
  • For paint, collect samples down to the metal/plastic substrate. Do not use tape lifts.
  • When removing a victim's/suspect's clothing, have them stand on clean paper to catch any falling trace evidence. Package each clothing item separately in paper bags.

Follow-Up & Case Management

Continuing Investigation

Elements of a Continuing Investigation:

  • Follow up on leads from the preliminary investigation.
  • Develop and revise a scheme of the crime.
  • Continue searching for and interviewing witnesses.
  • Prepare and conduct appropriate line-ups (sequential is preferred).
  • Liaise with lab technicians and assess forensic reports.
  • Identify, locate, and arrest the perpetrator(s).
  • Conduct surveillance and interrogations as needed.
  • Prepare the case and confer with the prosecutor.
Case Management

Value of a Properly Prepared Case Folder:

  • Provides an organized, factual, and thorough account of the investigation.
  • Prevents duplication of work and proves efficiency.
  • Useful for review by prosecutors and the grand jury.
  • Gives the defense attorney the impression of professionalism, which may help in obtaining a plea agreement.

Items Included in a Case Folder:

  • Offense and supplemental reports.
  • Witness and victim statements.
  • Voluntary confessions.
  • Fingerprint cards and expert comparison reports.
  • Other pertinent reports (lab results, autopsy, etc.).
