Statutory Training Requirements (Occupations Code)

Section 1701.253(g): The commission shall establish a statewide comprehensive education and training program on asset forfeiture under Chapter 59, Code of Criminal Procedure, for officers licensed under this chapter. An officer must complete this program not later than the second anniversary of the date the officer is licensed or applies for an intermediate proficiency certificate, whichever is earlier.

Section 1701.402(d): As a requirement for an intermediate proficiency certificate, an officer must complete an education and training program on asset forfeiture established by the commission under Section 1701.253(g).

Philosophy of Forfeiture

TAKE THE PROFIT MOTIVE OUT OF CRIME!

  • The main goal is to deprive criminals of the profits and instrumentalities of crime.
  • Forfeiture is considered a remedial action, not an extra punishment.
  • The 8th Amendment's Excessive Fines Clause applies; the value of property forfeited must be proportional to the seriousness of the offense.
  • A criminal conviction is not necessary for forfeiture to occur, but a dismissal or acquittal raises a presumption that the property should not be forfeited.
Definition of Contraband (CCP 59.01)

Contraband is property of any nature that is:

  • Used in the commission of: Any 1st or 2nd degree felony; various other felonies under the Penal Code (e.g., Arson, Robbery, Burglary, Theft); any felony under the Health and Safety Code (drug offenses).
  • The proceeds gained from the commission of: A listed felony or a crime of violence.
  • Acquired with proceeds from: A listed felony or a crime of violence.
  • Used or intended to be used to facilitate: Certain felonies, such as those under Human Trafficking or Prostitution statutes.

Example: A car used to transport a large quantity of cocaine for a drug deal is contraband because it was "used in the commission of" a felony under the Health and Safety Code.

Proceeds includes income a person receives from:

  • A movie, book, magazine article, or other media in which the crime was reenacted.
  • The sale of tangible property the value of which is increased by the notoriety gained from the conviction.
  • Commonly understood as property derived from an event or transaction.

Example: A drug trafficker uses cash from drug sales to buy a luxury watch. The watch is considered "property acquired with proceeds" and is subject to forfeiture.

Defenses to Forfeiture (CCP 59.02)

Statutory Defenses
  • Innocent Owner Defense: The owner acquired the property before the illegal act and did not know or should not have reasonably known about the act giving rise to the forfeiture.
  • Non-Consensual Use: The property was used without the owner's effective consent (e.g., it was stolen).
  • Dismissal or Acquittal of Related Charges: This raises a presumption that the property should not be forfeited. The state must then prove otherwise.
  • Family Violence Defense: A spouse may claim this defense if they were unable to prevent the criminal act due to family violence.

Example: A woman lends her car to her brother. Unbeknownst to her, he uses it to commit a robbery. She can claim the innocent owner defense because she did not know of the illegal act.

View CCP 59.02
Non-Statutory Defenses & Other Issues
  • Homestead: The issue of whether a homestead is protected from forfeiture is not fully resolved in Texas.
  • Legitimate Source of Income: The owner can provide evidence that the seized property was acquired with legitimate funds.
  • Fifth Amendment: In a civil forfeiture proceeding, a person's invocation of the right against self-incrimination can be commented on and may support an inference of guilt.
  • Suppression of Evidence: If evidence is suppressed in the criminal case, it may still be admissible in the civil forfeiture case because different rules apply.

Seizure Procedures

Affidavit Requirements & Waivers
  • Affidavit must include: Probable cause, location and date of seizure, owner and possessor identification, all essential parties, and justification for seizure (facts, circumstantial evidence, key factors such as proximity to drugs, canine alert, suspicious activity, amount of money, storage location).
  • Preferences: Written by the seizing officer, first person, avoid hearsay unless an exception applies, keep it simple, avoid uncommon acronyms, and include a complete report if the defendant's explanation is implausible.
  • Waivers: No longer allowed to solicit waivers. If no one is in possession, public notice in a newspaper is required.
  • Official Oppression: Officers cannot induce a person to waive rights to seized property (Penal Code 39.03).
Pre-Seizure Planning

Before seizing property, an officer should consider:

  • Ownership: Is the property community or separate? Leased or owned?
  • Liens & Mortgages: Is there enough equity in the property to make forfeiture worthwhile after paying off lienholders?
  • Risks & Liabilities: What are the costs associated with seizing, storing, and maintaining the property?
  • Bank Accounts: Identify account numbers, monitor activity to determine the best time to seize, and know if the account is collateral for a loan.
Methods of Seizure (CCP 59.03)

Without a Warrant:

  • The owner, operator, or agent knowingly consents.
  • The seizure is incident to a lawful arrest or search.
  • The property has been the subject of a prior judgment in favor of the state.

With a Warrant:

  • Rules from CCP Chapter 18 apply.
  • The affidavit must show probable cause for the specific felony offense committed and describe how the property meets the definition of contraband.

Checklist for Civil Forfeiture Case Documentation
  • Sworn/Notarized statement (acknowledgement of seizure, reasons/statutory basis, schedule of property seized)
  • Title/Lien/Balance info on vehicle/real property
  • Photos of vehicle (front and side)
  • Kelly Blue Book estimate (if vehicle seized)
  • Copy of search/seizure warrant, probable cause affidavit, and return
  • Copy of arrest, incident, and/or prosecution reports
  • Copy of DVD/CD evidence (photos, videos, statements, downloads, etc.)
  • Criminal and work history (if money seized)
  • Misc. documentation to enhance the civil case (must have DOB/SSN of defendants before filing)

Post-Seizure Procedures (CCP 59.03)

A peace officer who seizes property has custody and must:

  • Provide the attorney representing the state with a sworn statement listing the property seized and the reasons for seizure.
  • No later than 72 hours after the seizure, either place the property under seal, remove it to a place ordered by the court, or have the agency take custody.
  • If currency is seized, it must be deposited in an interest-bearing account.
Forfeiture Proceedings (CCP 59.04)
  • Forfeiture is a civil proceeding. The standard of proof is a preponderance of the evidence.
  • The attorney representing the state must begin proceedings within 30 days of the date of seizure.
  • The state files a "Notice of Seizure and Intended Forfeiture," which is served on the owner and any interest holders.
View CCP 59.04

Use of Forfeited Funds (CCP 59.06)

Approved Uses

Forfeited funds must be used for a "law enforcement purpose." This includes:

  • Equipment & Supplies
  • Investigative and training-related travel expenses
  • Crime prevention and treatment programs
  • Facility costs
  • Witness-related costs
  • Audit costs and legal fees
Prohibited Uses

Forfeiture money cannot be used for:

  • Political campaign donations
  • Training for any member of the judiciary
  • Purchase of alcoholic beverages
  • Increasing salaries (unless approved by the governing body)
  • Any expenditure not approved by the commissioners' court or governing body of the municipality.
