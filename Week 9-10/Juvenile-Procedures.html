Juvenile Procedures

A Comprehensive Study Guide

Key Definitions & Types of Conduct

Key Definitions (FC 51.02)
  • Child: A person who is 10 years of age or older and under 17; OR 17 or older and under 18 who is alleged to have engaged in delinquent conduct or CINS as a result of acts committed before turning 17.
  • Status Offender: A child accused of conduct that would NOT be a crime if committed by an adult (e.g., runaway, truancy).
  • Nonoffender: A child subject to court jurisdiction for abuse/neglect, or held solely for deportation.
  • Secure Correctional/Detention Facility: A facility with construction fixtures designed to physically restrict the movements of juveniles.
  • Referral to juvenile court: The referral of a child or the child's case to the office or official... designated by the juvenile board to process children within the juvenile justice system.
View Family Code 51.02
Delinquent Conduct vs. CINS (FC 51.03)

Delinquent Conduct:

  • Conduct, other than a traffic offense, that violates a state or federal penal law punishable by imprisonment or jail. (Most Class B misdemeanors and above).
  • Violations of certain court orders (contempt).
  • DWI, Intoxication Assault, Intoxication Manslaughter.

Example: A 15-year-old steals a car (Unauthorized Use of a Vehicle, a state jail felony).

Conduct Indicating a Need for Supervision (CINS):

  • Class C misdemeanors (other than traffic).
  • Running away from home.
  • Inhaling paint or glue fumes.
  • Expellable violations of a school district's code of conduct.

Example: A 14-year-old is found by police at 2 AM after having left home two days ago without permission.

View Family Code 51.03
Habitual Felony Conduct (FC 51.031)

Conduct violating a penal law of the grade of felony (other than a state jail felony), IF:

  • The child who engaged in the conduct has at least two previous final adjudications as having engaged in delinquent conduct violating a penal law of the grade of felony;
  • The second previous final adjudication is for conduct that occurred after the date the first previous adjudication became final; and
  • All appeals relating to the previous adjudications have been exhausted.

Example: A 16-year-old commits an Aggravated Robbery and has two prior final adjudications for Burglary of a Habitation.

View Family Code 51.031

Taking into Custody & Processing

Taking into Custody & Release (FC 52.01, 52.02)

Taking a child into custody is not an arrest except for the purpose of determining the validity of taking him into custody or the validity of a search.

After taking a child into custody, an officer shall without unnecessary delay:

  • Release the child to a parent, guardian, or other responsible adult.
  • Bring the child before the office or official designated by the juvenile board.
  • Bring the child to a designated detention facility.

Example: An officer sees a 15-year-old spray-painting graffiti on a wall, takes them into custody, and transports them to the county's juvenile processing office to await a parent.

View Family Code 52.01 & 52.02
Juvenile Processing Office & Nonsecure Custody

Juvenile Processing Office (FC 52.025):

  • A child may be detained here for no longer than six hours.
  • The office may NOT be a cell or holding facility.
  • A child may NOT be left unattended.

Nonsecure Custody for JP/Municipal Offenses (CCP 45A.453):

  • A child may not be secured physically to a cuffing rail, chair, or any stationary object.
  • The child must be under continuous visual supervision.
  • Detention in a place of nonsecure custody may not exceed six hours.
View Family Code 52.025
Collection of Records (FC 58.001)

Law enforcement shall forward all information (reports, fingerprints, photos) for a child taken into custody to the juvenile court on or before the 10th calendar day after the date the child is taken into custody.

Example: An officer takes a child into custody on August 1st. The complete file must be sent to the juvenile probation department by August 11th.

View Family Code 58.001

Statements, Waivers & Rights

Admissibility of a Statement (FC 51.095)

These rules apply to a child subjected to custodial interrogation. If the interview is non-custodial, these strict requirements do not apply, but the statement must still be voluntary.

Written Statement:

  1. The child must be given their rights by a magistrate.
  2. The child must knowingly, intelligently, and voluntarily waive those rights.
  3. The statement must be signed by the child in the presence of the magistrate with no law enforcement officer present.
  4. The magistrate must be fully convinced the child understands the statement and is signing voluntarily, and must certify this in writing.

Example: A detective takes a 16-year-old burglary suspect to a magistrate. The judge reads the rights, the juvenile waives them, writes a confession, and signs it in front of the judge alone. The statement is admissible.

View Family Code 51.095
Waiver of Rights & Polygraph (FC 51.09, 51.151)

Waiver of Rights: Any right granted to a child may be waived in a proceeding if the waiver is made by the child AND the child's attorney.

Polygraph Examination: If a child is in custody, a person may NOT administer a polygraph exam without the consent of the child's attorney or the juvenile court.

View Family Code 51.09

Court Process & Dispositions

Detention Hearing (FC 54.01)

If a child is not released, a detention hearing without a jury SHALL be held no later than the second working day after the child is taken into custody.

Example: A child is taken into custody for Aggravated Robbery on a Friday. The detention hearing must be held by the end of the day the following Tuesday.

View Family Code 54.01
Waiver of Jurisdiction (Transfer to Adult Court) (FC 54.02)

The juvenile court may waive its jurisdiction and transfer a child to be tried as an adult in criminal court for serious offenses.

Example: A 16-year-old is charged with murder. The prosecutor petitions the juvenile court to waive jurisdiction so the case can be tried in the adult criminal justice system due to the severity of the crime.

View Family Code 54.02

Parental Rights & School Notification

Rights and Duties of a Parent (FC 151.001)

Parents have the right and duty to provide care, control, protection, reasonable discipline, and support for their child.

View Family Code 151.001
Notification to Schools Required (CCP 15.27)

A law enforcement agency that arrests or refers a student for any felony or certain misdemeanors (including weapons, drugs, or assaultive offenses) **shall orally notify the school superintendent** or designee.

  • Notification must be made within 24 hours or before the next school day, whichever is earlier.
  • Written notification must be mailed within 7 days.

Example: An SRO arrests a 15-year-old student for possessing a handgun (felony) at 2:00 PM on a Tuesday. The SRO's agency must orally notify the superintendent by 2:00 PM on Wednesday.

View CCP 15.27

Youth Gang Involvement

Five Domains of Risk Factors
  1. Individual: Problem behaviors, acting out, negative life events, victimization.
  2. Family: Disrupted family structure, poor supervision, inconsistent discipline.
  3. School: Poor academic performance, low attachment to school.
  4. Peers: (One of the strongest factors) Normalization of anti-social behavior.
  5. Community: Poverty, environmental instability, neighborhood disorganization.
