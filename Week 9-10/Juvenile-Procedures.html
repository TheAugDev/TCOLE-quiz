Juvenile Procedures

A Comprehensive Study Guide

Key Definitions & Statutory Authority

Comprehensive Definitions & Purpose (FC 51.01, 51.02)
  • Purpose (FC 51.01): Protection of the public, promote punishment for criminal acts, remove taint of criminality from children, provide treatment/rehab, protect community welfare, and ensure fair judicial procedure. View FC 51.01
  • Child: 10-16 years old, or 17+ and under 18 if alleged to have engaged in delinquent conduct or CINS before turning 17. View FC 51.02
  • Status Offender: Accused/adjudicated/convicted for conduct that would not be criminal for an adult (truancy, runaway, school code violations, TABC/minor-only offenses, etc.).
  • Nonoffender: Child subject to court jurisdiction for abuse/neglect, or held solely for deportation.
  • Secure Correctional/Detention Facility: Facility with construction fixtures designed to physically restrict movement; used for adjudicated juveniles or nonoffenders.
  • Referral to juvenile court: Any way a case gets to juvenile officials (arrest, report, etc.).
  • Other Key Terms: Parent, custodian, guardian, dual status child, party, prosecuting attorney, valid court order, etc. (see FC 51.02 for all definitions).
  • CCP 15.27: Law enforcement must notify school officials within 24 hours of arrest or referral for felonies or certain misdemeanors. View CCP 15.27
  • CCP 45A.453: Nonsecure custody for JP/municipal cases: max 6 hours, no physical restraints, continuous visual supervision, unlocked area. View CCP 45A.453
  • Health & Safety Code 261.101: Officers must report suspected child abuse/neglect to CPS. View FC 261.101
Delinquent Conduct & CINS (FC 51.03, 51.031)

Delinquent Conduct: Violates penal law (Class B or above), court order contempt, DWI/Intoxication offenses, repeat DUI-minor, etc. View FC 51.03

Habitual Felony Conduct: Two prior final felony adjudications, third offense is a felony (other than state jail). View FC 51.031

CINS: Class C misdemeanors (other than traffic), runaway, inhalant use, school code violations, prostitution, electronic transmission of certain visual material, etc.

School Notification & Reporting

Statutory Requirement (CCP 15.27)
  • Oral notification to the superintendent or designee must be made within 24 hours or before the next school day, whichever is earlier, for a student's arrest, referral, conviction, or adjudication for felonies and certain misdemeanors.
  • Written notification (marked "PERSONAL and CONFIDENTIAL") must be mailed within 7 days of the oral notice and include all facts from the oral notice, the name of the person notified, and the date/time of the oral notice.
  • Both oral and written notices must include all pertinent details of the arrest/referral, including any assaultive behavior, violence, weapons used or possessed, and any information needed for threat assessment or safety planning if requested by the superintendent.
  • Electronic notice may substitute for oral notice; if so, written notice is not required.
  • School officials must notify all personnel with direct supervisory responsibility over the student. All personnel must keep the information confidential.
  • Unauthorized disclosure is a Class C misdemeanor. Failure to report by law enforcement, school, or prosecution personnel must be reported to TCOLE, State Board for Educator Certification, or the elected DA, as appropriate.
  • Prosecutors and probation/parole officers have similar notification duties for convictions, adjudications, and student transfers.
View CCP 15.27
Who Must Be Notified & What Must Be Included
  • Notification must go to the superintendent or designee of the school district in which the student is enrolled (or principal for private schools).
  • School officials must notify all instructional/support personnel with direct supervisory responsibility over the student.
  • Notices must include all pertinent details: offense, violence, weapons, and any threat assessment or safety plan information requested.
  • All personnel must keep the information confidential; unauthorized disclosure is a Class C misdemeanor.
Failure to Report & Penalties
  • Failure to report by law enforcement, school, or prosecution personnel must be reported to TCOLE, State Board for Educator Certification, or the elected DA, as appropriate.
  • Prosecutors and probation/parole officers have similar notification duties for convictions, adjudications, and student transfers.
  • It is a Class C misdemeanor to intentionally disclose unauthorized information.
Example Scenario

Example: An officer arrests a 16-year-old student for a felony. The agency must orally notify the school superintendent within 24 hours or before the next school day, and mail written notice within 7 days. The school must notify all staff with direct supervisory responsibility for the student.

Intake, Detention, and Hearings

Preliminary Investigation (FC 53.01)

Upon referral, the intake/probation officer or board designee must determine if the person is a child and if there is probable cause for delinquent conduct, CINS, or nonoffender status. If not, the person must be released.

View FC 53.01
Release from Detention (FC 53.02)
  • Release is required unless detention is warranted for: risk of absconding, no suitable supervision, no parent to return child to court, danger to self/public, prior delinquency and likely to reoffend, or use/possession of firearm in alleged offense.
  • Conditions of release may be imposed (similar to bond).
  • Absent these, the child must be released while the case proceeds.
View FC 53.02
Detention Hearings (FC 54.01, 54.011)
  • Detention hearing (no jury) must be held within 2 working days (or 1st working day if detained on Friday/Saturday).
  • Parents must be notified; court must appoint attorney if not retained.
  • Written reports from probation/court staff are considered; officers should have all reports/statements ready.
  • Unless justified, the court must release the child. If detained, new hearing every 10 days.
  • Status offenders/nonoffenders: hearing within 24 hours, must be released unless valid court order violation; nonoffenders may not be detained in secure facilities (Class B misdemeanor for violation).
View FC 54.01 | View FC 54.011

Example: A 14-year-old is detained for aggravated assault. The court must hold a hearing within 2 working days and notify the parents. If the child is a status offender, a hearing must be held within 24 hours and the child released unless a valid court order was violated.

  • Secure Detention Facility: Used for temporary placement of accused juveniles or nonoffenders. Must be physically separated from adults (sight and sound separation).
  • Secure Correctional Facility: Used for adjudicated juveniles or nonoffenders, includes construction fixtures for physical restriction.
  • Nonsecure Custody: Unlocked, multipurpose area (not a cell), no physical restraints, continuous visual supervision, max 6 hours.
Directive to Apprehend & Field Release (FC 52.015, CCP 14.06)
  • Directive to Apprehend: Juvenile court may issue on request of law enforcement/probation officer if probable cause exists. Not subject to appeal.
  • Field Release: Officers may issue a citation instead of custody for certain fine-only or traffic offenses, or for public intoxication if released to a responsible adult.

Example: An officer issues a field release citation to a 16-year-old for a traffic offense, releasing them to their parent at the scene.

View FC 52.015 | View CCP 14.06

Disposition, First Offender Programs, and JP/Municipal Court

Disposition Without Referral (FC 52.03, 52.031, 52.032)
  • Class C (non-traffic) cases may be disposed of without court referral if authorized by juvenile board guidelines and reported in writing by the officer.
  • No disposition may keep the child in law enforcement custody or require periodic reporting.
  • First Offender Programs: For CINS, Class C (non-traffic), or delinquent conduct (not for felonies/violence/firearms). Child/parent must consent; successful completion closes the case without court referral.
View FC 52.03 | View FC 52.031 | View FC 52.032
JP/Municipal Court: Fines, Contempt, and Orders (CCP 45A.461, 45A.457)
  • Children (10-17) may not be confined for failure to pay fines, appear, or contempt in JP/municipal court.
  • Contempt: Court may refer to juvenile court or impose fines (up to $500) or suspend/deny driver's license.
  • Orders may require services, program attendance, parental involvement, and address notification. Failure to comply may result in contempt or Class C misdemeanor.
View CCP 45A.461 | View CCP 45A.457

Parental Rights & Duties

Parental Rights & Duties (FC 151.001, 151.003, 153.074)
  • Physical possession, moral/religious training, designate residence
  • Care, control, protection, reasonable discipline
  • Support (clothing, food, shelter, medical/dental care, education)
  • Manage child's estate (unless guardian appointed)
  • Consent to marriage, enlistment, medical/psychological/surgical care
  • Represent child in legal actions, make legal decisions
  • Receive/give support payments, hold/disburse funds
  • Inheritance rights
  • Make educational decisions
  • Corporal punishment: Only by parent, grandparent, stepparent, or guardian with duty of control
  • State agencies may not violate fundamental parental rights (FC 151.003)
  • During periods of possession, conservators have care, support, medical, and religious training rights (FC 153.074)

Example: A parent may consent to medical care for their child, but a stepparent may only do so if they have legal authority or are the managing conservator.

View FC 151.001 | View FC 151.003 | View FC 153.074

Youth Gangs: Risk Factors & Needs

Youth Gangs: Risk Factors & Needs
  • Five Domains of Risk Factors: Individual, Family, School, Peers, Community
  • Needs met by gangs: Status, self-worth, acceptance, fun/excitement, credibility, protection, identity, bonding, wealth, survival
  • Societal strains: Unemployment, lack of bonding, discrimination, uncaring community, poor self-control, intimidation/aggression, illegal activity profitability
  • Phases: Minor mischief, seeking identity (colors, signals, graffiti, etc.), crime/criminal behavior
  • Most youth are not forced to join; joining is not permanent; multiple risk domains increase likelihood

Example: A 15-year-old joins a gang for protection and acceptance at school. The school resource officer identifies risk factors in the family and peer domains and refers the youth to intervention services.

Taking into Custody & Processing

Taking into Custody & Release (FC 52.01, 52.02)

Taking a child into custody is not an arrest except for the purpose of determining the validity of taking him into custody or the validity of a search.

  • Release to parent, guardian, custodian, or other responsible adult if they promise to bring the child to court.
  • Bring the child before the office/official designated by the juvenile board.
  • Bring the child to a designated detention facility or magistrate for delinquent conduct.
  • For Class C and Transportation Code offenses, citation may be issued instead of custody.

Example: An officer sees a 15-year-old spray-painting graffiti on a wall, takes them into custody, and transports them to the county's juvenile processing office to await a parent.

  • Nonsecure Custody: Unlocked, multipurpose area (not a cell), no physical restraints, continuous visual supervision, max 6 hours. May not be used for residential purposes.
  • Cannot be secured to a stationary object (e.g., cuffing rail, chair, desk).
  • Must be under continuous visual supervision by law enforcement or facility staff.

Example: A 13-year-old is held in a police station lobby (not a cell) for processing, under constant supervision, and released to a parent within 2 hours.

  • Field Release: For traffic or fine-only offenses, officers may issue a citation and release the child to a responsible adult at the scene.

Example: An officer issues a citation to a 14-year-old for a city ordinance violation and releases them to their guardian at the scene.

  • Civil vs. Criminal Incidents: Many custody disputes are civil. Officers may only intervene if a crime is about to occur or the child is in immediate danger. Must report to CPS if there is concern for the child's health or safety, even if no criminal offense is met.
  • Relevant Penal Code Offenses: Interference with Child Custody (PC 25.03), Enticing a Child (PC 25.04), Harboring Runaway Child (PC 25.06), Interference with Rights of Guardian (PC 25.10).

Example: During a custody dispute, an officer finds no crime but is concerned for the child's safety. The officer files a CPS report and documents the report number in their incident report.

View Family Code 52.01 & 52.02
Juvenile Processing Office & Nonsecure Custody

Juvenile Processing Office (FC 52.025):

  • A child may be detained here for no longer than six hours.
  • The office may NOT be a cell or holding facility.
  • A child may NOT be left unattended.

Nonsecure Custody for JP/Municipal Offenses (CCP 45A.453):

  • A child may not be secured physically to a cuffing rail, chair, or any stationary object.
  • The child must be under continuous visual supervision.
  • Detention in a place of nonsecure custody may not exceed six hours.
  • Nonsecure custody must be in an unlocked, multipurpose area (not a cell or secure area).
  • Residential use of the area is prohibited.
  • Field Release (CCP 14.06): Officers may issue a citation instead of custody for certain fine-only or traffic offenses. View CCP 14.06
View Family Code 52.025
Collection of Records (FC 58.001)

Law enforcement shall forward all information (reports, fingerprints, photos) for a child taken into custody to the juvenile court on or before the 10th calendar day after the date the child is taken into custody.

Example: An officer takes a child into custody on August 1st. The complete file must be sent to the juvenile probation department by August 11th.

  • CCP 45A.454: Special requirements for citations/complaints for conduct on school property or vehicles (must submit offense report and witness/victim statements for children 12+). View CCP 45A.454
View Family Code 58.001

Statements, Waivers & Rights

Admissibility of a Statement (FC 51.095)

These rules apply to a child subjected to custodial interrogation. If the interview is non-custodial, these strict requirements do not apply, but the statement must still be voluntary.

Written Statement:

  1. The child must be given their rights by a magistrate.
  2. The child must knowingly, intelligently, and voluntarily waive those rights.
  3. The statement must be signed by the child in the presence of the magistrate with no law enforcement officer present.
  4. The magistrate must be fully convinced the child understands the statement and is signing voluntarily, and must certify this in writing.

Example: A detective takes a 16-year-old burglary suspect to a magistrate. The judge reads the rights, the juvenile waives them, writes a confession, and signs it in front of the judge alone. The statement is admissible.

View Family Code 51.095

Audio/Video Statement: Same magistrate warnings as written, but warnings must be recorded and child must waive each right on the recording. Each voice/person must be identified. Defense attorney is entitled to a copy at least 20 days before the proceeding.

Oral Statement: Admissible if facts are found to be true and tend to establish guilt (e.g., finding stolen property), or if made in open court, grand jury, or preliminary hearing.

Example: A juvenile confesses to theft during a recorded interview after being warned by a magistrate. The recording is provided to the defense attorney before trial.

  • Important: If the offense occurred before age 17 but the person is now 18+, adult rules apply for interrogation (CCP 38.22 & 2.32), but the case may start in juvenile court.
  • Best Practice: If age is in question, follow juvenile procedures—they satisfy adult requirements but not vice versa.
Waiver of Rights & Polygraph (FC 51.09, 51.151)

Waiver of Rights: Any right granted to a child may be waived in a proceeding if the waiver is made by the child AND the child's attorney.

  • The child and attorney must be informed of and understand the right and possible consequences of waiving it.
  • The waiver must be voluntary and made in writing or in court proceedings that are recorded.

Polygraph Examination: If a child is in custody, a person may NOT administer a polygraph exam without the consent of the child's attorney or the juvenile court.

View Family Code 51.09

Example: A 15-year-old and their attorney sign a written waiver of the right to trial. The waiver is valid if both understand the right and consequences, and it is voluntary.

Court Process & Dispositions

Detention Hearing (FC 54.01)

If a child is not released, a detention hearing without a jury SHALL be held no later than the second working day after the child is taken into custody.

Example: A child is taken into custody for Aggravated Robbery on a Friday. The detention hearing must be held by the end of the day the following Tuesday.

View Family Code 54.01
Waiver of Jurisdiction (Transfer to Adult Court) (FC 54.02)

The juvenile court may waive its jurisdiction and transfer a child to be tried as an adult in criminal court for serious offenses.

Example: A 16-year-old is charged with murder. The prosecutor petitions the juvenile court to waive jurisdiction so the case can be tried in the adult criminal justice system due to the severity of the crime.

View Family Code 54.02

Case Terminology: Adult vs. Juvenile
  • Criminal Offense (adult) vs. Delinquent Conduct (juvenile)
  • Arrest Warrant vs. Directive to Apprehend
  • Indictment/Information vs. Juvenile Petition
  • Defendant vs. Respondent
  • Conviction vs. Adjudication
  • Sentencing Hearing vs. Disposition Hearing
  • Guilty/Not Guilty Plea vs. Plea of True/Not True
Edge Cases & Special Considerations
  • Perjury: Children can be prosecuted for perjury (FC 51.03(c)).
  • Age Gray Area: If the offense occurred before 17 but the person is now 18+, adult rules apply for interrogation, but the case may start in juvenile court.
  • Safer Practice: If age is in question, follow juvenile procedures—they satisfy adult requirements but not vice versa.
