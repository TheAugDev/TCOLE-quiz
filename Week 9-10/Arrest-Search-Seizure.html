TCOLE Training Requirements

Asset Forfeiture Training (Occupations Code 1701)

Sec. 1701.253(g): TCOLE shall establish a statewide comprehensive education and training program on asset forfeiture under CCP Chapter 59. An officer must complete this program not later than the second anniversary of the date the officer is licensed or applies for an intermediate proficiency certificate, whichever is earlier.

Sec. 1701.402(d): As a requirement for an intermediate proficiency certificate, an officer must complete the asset forfeiture training program.

Definitions & Grounds for Issuance

Search Warrant Definition (CCP 18.01)

A "search warrant" is a written order, issued by a magistrate and directed to a peace officer, commanding him to search for any property or thing and to seize the same and bring it before such magistrate.

  • Also may command search for and photograph a child and deliver any film exposed to the magistrate (CCP 18.01(a)).
  • Black’s Law: A judge’s written order authorizing a law enforcement officer to conduct a search of a specified place and to seize evidence.
View CCP 18.01
Grounds for Issuance (CCP 18.02)

A search warrant may be issued to search for and seize the following, as specified in CCP 18.02:

  1. Property acquired by theft or in any other manner which makes its acquisition a penal offense.
  2. Property specially designed, made, or adapted for or commonly used in the commission of an offense.
  3. Arms and munitions kept or prepared for the purposes of insurrection or riot.
  4. Weapons prohibited by the Penal Code.
  5. Gambling devices or equipment, altered gambling equipment, or gambling paraphernalia.
  6. Obscene materials kept or prepared for commercial distribution or exhibition, subject to the additional rules set forth by law.
  7. A drug, controlled substance, immediate precursor, chemical precursor, or other controlled substance property, including an apparatus or paraphernalia kept, prepared, or manufactured in violation of the laws of this state.
  8. Any property the possession of which is prohibited by law.
  9. Implements or instruments used in the commission of a crime.
  10. Evidentiary Search Warrant (18.02(a)(10)): Property or items, except the accused’s personal writings, constituting evidence of an offense or tending to show someone committed an offense.
    Note: A search warrant may not be issued under (10) to search for and seize property or items that are not described in (1-9) and are located in an office of a newspaper, news magazine, television station, or radio station. In no event may property or items not described in (1-9) be legally seized pursuant to a search warrant of an office of a newspaper, news magazine, television station, or radio station.
  11. Persons.
  12. Contraband subject to forfeiture under Chapter 59 of this code.
  13. Electronic customer data held in electronic storage, including the contents of and records and other information related to a wire communication or electronic communication held in electronic storage.
  14. A cellular telephone or other wireless communications device, subject to Article 18.0215.
  15. Item 20 (from TXT): Any other item specifically described by law for which a search warrant may be issued.

Judicial Requirements: Search warrants under 18.02 (10 & 12) may only be issued by:
- A judge of a municipal court of record or county court who is a licensed attorney, or judge of a statutory county court; or
- A judge of district courts, court of criminal appeals, or supreme court.
When a county does not have judges listed above, any magistrate may issue a search warrant under Article 18.02 (a)(10) or (12). This does not apply to subsequent search warrants under (10), which may only be issued by a judge of a district court, a court of appeals, the court of criminal appeals, or the supreme court.

Blood Specimen Warrants: Any magistrate who is an attorney licensed by this state may issue a search warrant under 18.02 (10) to collect a blood specimen from a person who is arrested for an offense under Section 49.04, 49.045, 49.05, 49.06, 49.065, 49.07, or 49.08, Penal Code, and refuses to submit to a breath or blood alcohol test.

View CCP 18.02
Warrant May Order Arrest (CCP 18.03)

If the facts presented to the magistrate also establish probable cause that a person has committed an offense, the search warrant may, in addition, order the arrest of such person.

Example: An affidavit for a search warrant for stolen goods at a suspect's home also contains facts showing the suspect committed the burglary. The warrant can command officers to both search the home and arrest the suspect.

View CCP 18.03
Plain View Doctrine (Horton v. California)

Officers may seize evidence in plain view from a lawful vantage point, even if not listed in the warrant, as long as they are lawfully present and its incriminating character is immediately apparent.

Example: While searching for stolen TVs, officers see illegal narcotics in plain view and may seize them under the plain view doctrine.

Warrant Application & Issuance

The Sworn Affidavit (CCP 18.01(b))

No search warrant shall issue unless sufficient facts are first presented to satisfy the magistrate that probable cause does in fact exist. A sworn affidavit setting forth substantial facts establishing probable cause shall be filed in every instance.

For an Evidentiary Search Warrant (18.02(a)(10)), the affidavit must establish PC that:

  • A specific offense has been committed;
  • The described property constitutes evidence of that offense; AND
  • The property to be seized is located at the particular place to be searched.
  • For blood specimen warrants (DWI), the affidavit must show the person is arrested for a qualifying offense and refused a test (18.01(j)).
  • For photographing an injured child, the affidavit must show a specific offense, a specifically described victim, and that evidence can be detected by photographic means (18.021).
Contents of a Warrant (CCP 18.04)

A search warrant shall be sufficient if it contains the following:

  • It must run in the name of "The State of Texas".
  • It must identify, as near as may be, that which is to be seized and the person, place, or thing to be searched.
  • It must command any peace officer of the proper county to search forthwith.
  • It must be dated and signed by the magistrate.
  • The magistrate's name must appear in clearly legible handwriting or typewritten form with signature.

Advantages of a Search Warrant:

  • Evidence seized is more readily accepted by courts.
  • Officer may be protected from civil liability.
  • May shift the legal burden to the defendant to show the evidence was seized illegally.
View CCP 18.04
Warrants for Fire, Health, and Code Inspections (CCP 18.05)

May be issued to a fire marshal, health officer, or code enforcement official to inspect premises for fire/health hazards or unsafe building conditions, or violations of regulations/statutes/ordinances.

  • Probable cause may be based on age, condition, type, use, or prior violations of the premises or nearby premises.
  • Each city/county may designate officials for this purpose.
  • Not available to county code enforcement in counties with population ≥ 3.3 million for building code violations.
Sealing of Affidavit (CCP 18.011)

A prosecutor may request a judge to seal a search warrant affidavit if there is a compelling state interest, such as:

  • Public disclosure would jeopardize the safety of a victim, witness, or informant.
  • Public disclosure would cause the destruction of evidence.

A sealing order expires on the 31st day after the warrant is executed, but can be granted one 30-day extension.

View CCP 18.011

Execution of Warrants

Timeline and Manner of Execution (CCP 18.06, 18.07)

An officer must execute the warrant without delay and return it to the proper magistrate.

Days Allowed for Warrant to Run (excluding day of issuance and execution):

  • 3 whole days for a typical search warrant.
  • 10 whole days for a warrant for electronic customer data.
  • 15 whole days for a warrant to seize DNA specimens.

Upon searching, the officer must present a copy of the warrant to the owner (if present) and prepare a written inventory of property to be taken.

  • Officer must legibly endorse their name on the inventory and present or leave a copy with the owner/possessor.
View CCP 18.06 & 18.07
Warrants for Electronic Data (CCP 18B.354)

District judges may issue warrants for electronic customer data held in electronic storage (e.g., cloud, social media, email). Application must show probable cause and be supported by oath. Only data described in the affidavit may be seized.

Knock-and-Announce vs. No-Knock

Wilson v. Arkansas: The Fourth Amendment ordinarily requires officers to knock and announce their presence and authority before entering a home to execute a warrant.

No-Knock Entry: An exception is allowed if police have a reasonable suspicion that knocking and announcing would be dangerous (threat of violence) or futile, or that it would inhibit the investigation (e.g., allow for the destruction of evidence).

Video: Wilson v. Arkansas explained

Detention of Persons During Search

Muehler v. Mena: Officers executing a search warrant for contraband have the authority to detain the occupants of the premises while a proper search is conducted.

Example: While executing a search warrant for narcotics at a house, officers can lawfully handcuff and detain all occupants in the living room for the duration of the search to ensure officer safety and prevent the destruction of evidence.

Video: Muehler v. Mena explained

Return of Warrant (CCP 18.10)

Not later than three whole days after executing a search warrant, the officer shall return the warrant to the magistrate. The officer must state on the back of the warrant how it was executed and deliver a copy of the inventory of seized property.

View CCP 18.10

Special Warrants

Warrant to Photograph Injured Child (CCP 18.021)

A warrant may be issued to search for and photograph a child alleged to be a victim of Injury to a Child, Sexual Assault, or related offenses. The warrant must identify the child and the place to be searched. The child may not be removed from the premises.

  • Officer executing the warrant may be accompanied by a law enforcement agency photographer.
  • Officer or assistant must be of the same sex as the victim, or be assisted by someone of the same sex.
  • After execution, officer must deliver the film/photos to the magistrate.
View CCP 18.021
ACCESS TO CELL PHONE OR OTHER WIRELESS DEVICE (CCP 18.0215)

General Rule: Peace officers may not search a cell phone or wireless device incident to a lawful arrest without a warrant.

Application Requirements: Applications for a device warrant must be written, signed, and sworn to or affirmed before a judge, and must include:

  • The name, department, agency, and address of the applicant.
  • Identification of the device to be searched.
  • The name of the owner or possessor of the device to be searched.
  • The judicial district for: (1) the peace officer’s agency if in the officer's possession; or (2) the likely location of the device.
  • The facts and circumstances establishing probable cause that criminal activity has been, is, or will be committed, and that searching the device is likely to produce evidence in the investigation.

Exceptions (No Warrant Needed):

  • The owner or possessor consents to the search.
  • The device is reported stolen by the owner or possessor.
  • The officer reasonably believes the device is in the possession of a fugitive with a felony warrant.
  • There exists an immediate life-threatening situation (hostage, barricade, or other emergency where a person unlawfully and directly threatens another with death or exposes to risk of serious bodily injury, under 18A.201).

Emergency Search: If a device is searched in a fugitive or life-threatening situation, the officer must apply for a device warrant as soon as practicable after the search. If the judge finds that the situation did not occur and declines the warrant, any evidence obtained is not admissible in a criminal action.

Judicial District: Warrants may only be issued by judges in the same judicial district for: (1) the peace officer’s agency if in the officer's possession; or (2) the likely location of the device.

View CCP 18.0215
Warrants for Fire, Health, and Code Inspections (CCP 18.05)

May be issued to inspect premises for fire/health hazards or unsafe building conditions, or violations of regulations/statutes/ordinances. Probable cause may be based on age, condition, type, use, or prior violations of the premises or nearby premises.

  • Each city/county may designate officials for this purpose.
  • Not available to county code enforcement in counties with population ≥ 3.3 million for building code violations.
Sealing of Affidavit (CCP 18.011)

A felony prosecutor may request a judge to seal a search warrant affidavit if public disclosure would jeopardize safety or destroy evidence, or if the affidavit contains info from an unexpired wiretap order.

  • Sealing order expires on the 31st day after execution, with one possible 30-day extension.
  • Does not prohibit disclosure of warrant contents, return, or inventory, or affect a defendant’s right to discover the affidavit.
View CCP 18.011
Warrant for Blood Specimen (CCP 18.01(j))

Any magistrate who is a licensed attorney may issue a search warrant to collect a blood specimen from a person who:

  1. Is arrested for an intoxication offense (e.g., DWI); and
  2. Refuses to submit to a breath or blood alcohol test.
Warrant for Cell Phone or Wireless Device (CCP 18.0215)

Peace officers may not search a cell phone incident to a lawful arrest without a warrant.

Exceptions (No Warrant Needed):

  • The owner consents to the search.
  • The device is reported stolen by the owner.
  • The officer reasonably believes the device is in possession of a fugitive or there is an immediate life-threatening situation.
View CCP 18.0215
